1. Time Series Foundations

A time series is a sequence of data points recorded at successive equally spaced time intervals. In quant finance, the series is usually asset prices, returns, trading volumes, or macroeconomic indicators.

Components of a Time Series

Most series can be decomposed into four parts. Understanding them helps you choose the right model.

  • Trend: Long-term direction. Example: a stock index drifting upward over decades due to economic growth.
  • Seasonality: Repeating pattern at fixed intervals. Example: retail sales spike every December.
  • Cyclical: Oscillations without a fixed period, usually tied to business cycles (multi-year).
  • Irregular / Noise: Random, unpredictable fluctuations.

Stationarity

A stationary series has statistical properties that do not change over time. This is the single most important prerequisite for many models.

Weak (Wide-Sense) Stationarity

  1. Constant mean: E[Yt] = μ for all t
  2. Constant variance: Var(Yt) = σ²
  3. Covariance depends only on lag k: Cov(Yt, Yt-k) = γ(k)

Strict Stationarity

The entire joint distribution is time-invariant. Much stronger and rarely needed in practice.

Why it matters: If means and variances shift over time, a model trained on historical data is describing a distribution that no longer exists. Non-stationary series tend to produce spurious regressions.

White Noise & Random Walk

  • White Noise (εt): E[εt]=0, constant variance, zero autocorrelation. The "unpredictable" part of a good model should look like white noise.
  • Random Walk: Yt = Yt-1 + εt. The variance grows with time (t·σ²), so it is non-stationary. Differencing it once yields white noise.

2. Exploratory Analysis & Stationarity Tests

Before modeling, you must inspect the data. EDA for time series is different from cross-sectional EDA because order matters.

Autocorrelation Function (ACF)

ACF measures the linear relationship between Yt and Yt-k for various lags k.

ρ(k) = Cov(Yt, Yt-k) / Var(Yt)

For a stationary series, ACF decays quickly. For a random walk, ACF decays very slowly because Yt is strongly correlated with all past values.

Partial Autocorrelation Function (PACF)

PACF measures the correlation between Yt and Yt-k after removing the effect of intermediate lags 1...k-1. It is crucial for identifying the order p in AR models.

Decomposition

Two classical models:

  • Additive: Yt = Tt + St + εt (seasonal fluctuations are constant in magnitude)
  • Multiplicative: Yt = Tt × St × εt (seasonal fluctuations grow with the trend). Use log-transform to convert to additive.

Unit Root Tests

TestNull HypothesisStationarity ConclusionTypical Use
Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF)Unit root exists (non-stationary)Reject null → stationaryMost common first test
KPSSSeries is stationaryReject null → non-stationaryConfirmatory; opposite null to ADF
PP (Phillips-Perron)Unit root existsRobust to heteroskedasticityAlternative to ADF
Rule of thumb: If ADF says non-stationary and KPSS says non-stationary → difference the series and test again. If they disagree, inspect the series visually.

3. ARIMA Family of Models

ARIMA is the workhorse of univariate time series forecasting. The name encodes three operations: AR (autoregression), I (differencing), and MA (moving average).

AR(p): Autoregressive Model

The current value is a linear combination of past values plus noise.

Yt = c + φ1·Yt-1 + φ2·Yt-2 + ... + φp·Yt-p + εt

For stationarity, the roots of the characteristic polynomial must lie outside the unit circle. In practice for AR(1): |φ1| < 1.

Identification via PACF

For an AR(p) process, the PACF "cuts off" after lag p. The ACF decays exponentially or as a damped sine wave.

MA(q): Moving Average Model

Yt = μ + εt + θ1·εt-1 + θ2·εt-2 + ... + θq·εt-q

Here the current value depends on past shocks (errors), not past values. MA models are always stationary. They are invertible if roots lie outside the unit circle.

Identification via ACF

For an MA(q) process, the ACF cuts off after lag q. The PACF decays gradually.

ARMA(p,q)

Combine both:

Yt = c + Σ φi·Yt-i + εt + Σ θj·εt-j

Use ACF/PACF together to identify orders, or use information criteria.

ARIMA(p,d,q)

If a series is non-stationary, difference it d times until it becomes stationary, then fit ARMA.

  • d=0: original series is stationary
  • d=1: first differences (e.g., random walk → white noise)
  • d=2: second differences (rarely needed in finance)

SARIMA(p,d,q)(P,D,Q)m

Extends ARIMA with seasonal terms. m is the seasonal period (e.g., 12 for monthly data, 7 for daily with weekly seasonality).

Model Selection: AIC & BIC

CriterionFormula IdeaBehavior
AICGoodness-of-fit − 2·kCan overfit; tends to pick larger models
BICGoodness-of-fit − ln(n)·kHeavier penalty for parameters; more parsimonious

where k = number of parameters, n = sample size. Lower is better.

Box-Jenkins Methodology

  1. Identification: Use plots, ACF/PACF, and ADF to choose p, d, q.
  2. Estimation: Fit the model via maximum likelihood or least squares.
  3. Diagnostic Checking: Residuals should look like white noise. Check ACF of residuals and use Ljung-Box test.
  4. Forecasting: Project forward; confidence intervals widen with horizon.
Quant tip: In trading, ARIMA forecasts are rarely tradable alone because the signal-to-noise ratio is low. They are more useful for variance modeling, mean-reversion detection, or as a baseline.

4. Volatility Modeling: ARCH & GARCH

For quants, modeling the mean is often less profitable than modeling the variance (volatility). Volatility clustering is the phenomenon where large changes tend to be followed by large changes, of either sign.

Heteroskedasticity

Standard regression assumes constant variance (homoskedasticity). Financial returns violate this: calm periods alternate with turbulent periods.

ARCH(q): Autoregressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity

Engle (1982). The variance at time t depends on past squared residuals.

σt² = ω + α1·εt-1² + α2·εt-2² + ... + αq·εt-q²

Constraints: ω > 0, αi ≥ 0, and Σαi < 1 for stationarity of variance.

GARCH(p,q): Generalized ARCH

Bollerslev (1986). Adds autoregressive terms for variance itself—the most widely used model in quant finance.

σt² = ω + Σ αi·εt-i² + Σ βj·σt-j²

GARCH(1,1) is the industry standard:

σt² = ω + α·εt-1² + β·σt-1²

Persistence & Half-Life

Persistence = α + β. If close to 1, shocks decay slowly. The half-life of a volatility shock is:

HL = ln(0.5) / ln(α + β)

Extensions

  • EGARCH: Models log-variance; captures leverage effect (bad news increases volatility more than good news).
  • GJR-GARCH: Adds an asymmetry term for negative shocks.

5. State Space Models & Kalman Filter

State space models represent a time series as the observable output of a hidden (latent) state that evolves over time. They unify ARIMA, exponential smoothing, and many structural models under one framework.

The Two Equations

  • Observation (Measurement) Equation: Yt = Z·αt + εt
  • State Transition Equation: αt = T·αt-1 + R·ηt

Where αt is the hidden state vector, εt and ηt are noise terms.

Kalman Filter Intuition

Imagine tracking a drone with a GPS sensor. The drone has a true position (state) and the GPS gives a noisy measurement. The Kalman filter optimally blends the model prediction with the new measurement, weighted by their respective uncertainties.

  1. Predict: Use the state equation to guess the next state and its uncertainty.
  2. Update: When the observation arrives, compute the prediction error (innovation). Adjust the state estimate proportionally to the Kalman Gain K. Higher K means you trust the observation more than the prediction.

Why Quants Care

  • Dynamic Hedge Ratios: In pairs trading, the hedge ratio between two assets changes over time. Kalman filter estimates it recursively.
  • Missing Data: Handles irregularly spaced observations naturally.
  • Online Learning: Updates instantly as new data arrives—perfect for high-frequency settings.

Colab Experiment: Kalman Filter for Pairs Trading Hedge Ratio

!pip install pykalman -q
import numpy as np, pandas as pd, yfinance as yf
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from pykalman import KalmanFilter

# Download two correlated assets (e.g., Coca-Cola vs Pepsi)
tickers = ['KO', 'PEP']
data = yf.download(tickers, start='2020-01-01', progress=False)['Adj Close']
x = np.log(data['KO'])
y = np.log(data['PEP'])

# Kalman filter to estimate dynamic slope (hedge ratio) and intercept
kf = KalmanFilter(
    n_dim_obs=1,
    n_dim_state=2,
    initial_state_mean=np.zeros(2),
    initial_state_covariance=np.ones((2,2)),
    observation_matrices=np.vstack([x, np.ones(len(x))]).T[:, np.newaxis, :],
    observation_covariance=1.0,
    transition_covariance=0.001 * np.eye(2),
    transition_matrices=np.eye(2)
)
state_means, state_covs = kf.filter(y.values)
slope = state_means[:, 0]
intercept = state_means[:, 1]
spread = y - slope * x - intercept

fig, ax = plt.subplots(2,1,figsize=(10,6), sharex=True)
ax[0].plot(data.index, slope, color='#38bdf8'); ax[0].set_title('Dynamic Hedge Ratio (Slope)')
ax[1].plot(data.index, spread, color='#fbbf24'); ax[1].set_title('Kalman Spread (Mean-Reverting)')
ax[1].axhline(0, color='white', linestyle='--', alpha=0.4)
plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()

6. Multivariate Time Series, VAR & Cointegration

So far we modeled one series. In quant finance, you almost always care about multiple assets simultaneously—pairs trading, statistical arbitrage, and macro forecasting all require multivariate tools.

VAR(p): Vector Autoregression

Each variable is modeled as a linear function of its own lags and the lags of all other variables in the system.

Yt = c + A1·Yt-1 + A2·Yt-2 + ... + Ap·Yt-p + εt

Where Yt is a vector, and Ai are coefficient matrices. A VAR(1) with 2 variables looks like:

y1,t = c1 + a11·y1,t-1 + a12·y2,t-1 + ε1,t
y2,t = c2 + a21·y1,t-1 + a22·y2,t-1 + ε2,t

Granger Causality

If the lags of y2 significantly improve the prediction of y1, we say y2 "Granger-causes" y1. This is a predictive concept, not true causality.

Cointegration

Two non-stationary series can have a stationary linear combination. This is the statistical foundation of pairs trading.

Engle-Granger Two-Step Method

  1. Regress Yt on Xt: Yt = α + β·Xt + εt
  2. Test residuals εt for stationarity (ADF). If stationary, the pair is cointegrated.

Vector Error Correction Model (VECM)

If variables are cointegrated, a VAR in differences misses the long-run equilibrium. VECM includes the error correction term:

ΔYt = α·β'·Yt-1 + Σ Γi·ΔYt-i + εt

The term β'·Yt-1 is the disequilibrium; α is the speed of adjustment back to equilibrium.

Colab Experiment: Cointegrated Pairs

!pip install statsmodels -q
import numpy as np, pandas as pd, yfinance as yf
from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import coint, adfuller
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Download two cointegrated-looking stocks (e.g., energy peers)
tickers = ['XOM', 'CVX']
data = yf.download(tickers, start='2019-01-01', progress=False)['Adj Close']
y1 = np.log(data['XOM'])
y2 = np.log(data['CVX'])

# Engle-Granger test
score, pvalue, _ = coint(y1, y2)
print(f'Cointegration test p-value: {pvalue:.4f}')
print('Cointegrated' if pvalue < 0.05 else 'Not cointegrated')

# Estimate hedge ratio via OLS
import statsmodels.api as sm
x = sm.add_constant(y2)
model = sm.OLS(y1, x).fit()
beta = model.params[1]
spread = y1 - beta * y2

# Plot
fig, ax = plt.subplots(2,1,figsize=(10,6), sharex=True)
ax[0].plot(data.index, y1, label='XOM'); ax[0].plot(data.index, y2, label='CVX')
ax[0].legend(); ax[0].set_title('Log Prices')
ax[1].plot(data.index, spread, color='#fbbf24'); ax[1].set_title('Spread (Cointegrating Residual)')
ax[1].axhline(spread.mean(), color='white', linestyle='--', alpha=0.5)
plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()

# ADF on spread
print('Spread ADF p-value:', adfuller(spread)[1])

7. Machine Learning for Time Series

Tree-based models (XGBoost, LightGBM, Random Forest) do not assume linearity and can capture complex interactions. However, they require careful feature engineering and validation because standard random train/test splits leak future information.

Feature Engineering for Time Series

  • Lag features: Yt-1, Yt-2, ...
  • Rolling statistics: mean, std, min, max over windows (7, 30, 60 days).
  • Date/time features: day of week, month, quarter, holidays.
  • Differences / momentum: Yt - Yt-k, percentage change.
  • Target encoding caution: Never use future information to compute features.

Cross-Validation: The Time Dimension

Never use KFold randomly! Use rolling-origin or expanding-window validation.

Tree-Based Models

XGBoost and LightGBM are popular because they:

  • Handle non-linearities
  • Provide feature importance
  • Are fast and well-supported

However, they do not extrapolate trends well beyond the training range. For strongly trending series, detrend first or use differenced targets.

!pip install xgboost -q
import numpy as np, pandas as pd
from xgboost import XGBRegressor
from sklearn.metrics import mean_squared_error
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Synthetic series with trend + seasonality + noise
np.random.seed(5)
n = 800
t = np.arange(n)
y = 0.02*t + 10*np.sin(2*np.pi*t/50) + np.random.normal(0, 2, n)
df = pd.DataFrame({'y': y})

# Feature engineering
for lag in [1,2,3,5,10]:
    df[f'lag_{lag}'] = df['y'].shift(lag)
for w in [7,14,30]:
    df[f'roll_mean_{w}'] = df['y'].shift(1).rolling(w).mean()
    df[f'roll_std_{w}'] = df['y'].shift(1).rolling(w).std()
df = df.dropna()

# Train/test split (time-based)
train = df.iloc[:600]
test = df.iloc[600:]
X_cols = [c for c in df.columns if c != 'y']

model = XGBRegressor(n_estimators=300, learning_rate=0.05, max_depth=4)
model.fit(train[X_cols], train['y'])
preds = model.predict(test[X_cols])

print('Test RMSE:', np.sqrt(mean_squared_error(test['y'], preds)))

# Plot
plt.figure(figsize=(10,4))
plt.plot(range(n), y, label='Actual', alpha=0.7)
plt.plot(test.index, preds, label='XGBoost Forecast', color='#f87171')
plt.legend(); plt.title('XGBoost Time Series Forecast'); plt.show()

# Feature importance
importances = pd.Series(model.feature_importances_, index=X_cols).sort_values(ascending=False)
print(importances.head(10))

8. Deep Learning for Time Series

Neural networks can learn representations automatically. For sequences, we use architectures that respect temporal ordering.

RNN & LSTM

A Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) maintains a hidden state that acts as memory. However, basic RNNs suffer from vanishing gradients, making them forget long-term dependencies.

LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory) solves this with gating mechanisms:

  • Forget gate: what to erase from memory
  • Input gate: what to write to memory
  • Output gate: what to output based on memory

1D CNNs

Convolutional layers can detect local patterns (e.g., momentum bursts) across time. They are faster to train than RNNs and often combined with them.

Transformers

Self-attention allows the model to look at all time steps simultaneously and learn which past moments are relevant for the current prediction. Challenges for finance:

  • Quadratic complexity with sequence length
  • Positional encoding must encode time explicitly
  • Overfitting is a major risk with limited financial data
Quant warning: Deep learning is data-hungry. A 10-year daily stock history is only ~2,520 points. Unless you have a large panel of assets or high-frequency data, regularized linear models or trees often outperform LSTMs.

import numpy as np, pandas as pd, yfinance as yf
from tensorflow.keras.models import Sequential
from tensorflow.keras.layers import LSTM, Dense
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Data
spy = yf.download('^GSPC', start='2015-01-01', end='2024-01-01', progress=False)
rets = spy['Adj Close'].pct_change().dropna().values.reshape(-1,1)

# Target: realized volatility proxy (squared returns)
# Features: past 20 days of returns
window = 20
X, y = [], []
for i in range(window, len(rets)):
    X.append(rets[i-window:i])
    y.append(rets[i]**2)  # proxy for volatility
X, y = np.array(X), np.array(y)

# Scale
scaler = StandardScaler()
X = scaler.fit_transform(X.reshape(-1,1)).reshape(X.shape)

# Split
split = int(0.8*len(X))
X_train, X_test = X[:split], X[split:]
y_train, y_test = y[:split], y[split:]

# LSTM
model = Sequential([
    LSTM(32, input_shape=(window,1)),
    Dense(16, activation='relu'),
    Dense(1)
])
model.compile(optimizer='adam', loss='mse')
model.fit(X_train, y_train, epochs=15, batch_size=32, validation_split=0.1, verbose=1)

preds = model.predict(X_test).flatten()

plt.figure(figsize=(10,4))
plt.plot(y_test, label='Realized (Proxy)', alpha=0.6)
plt.plot(preds, label='LSTM Forecast', color='#f87171')
plt.title('LSTM Volatility Forecast'); plt.legend(); plt.show()

9. Backtesting & Quant Risk Management

A model is useless if it cannot be translated into a tradable strategy and validated without cheating. Backtesting is where most quant strategies die.

Walk-Forward Analysis

Instead of one train/test split, retrain the model periodically and test on the subsequent period. This mimics real deployment.

Data Leakage: The Silent Killer

  • Look-ahead bias: Using information not available at time t (e.g., end-of-day data to make an intraday decision).
  • Survivorship bias: Only testing on stocks that still exist today (dead companies are excluded).
  • Point-in-time data: Ensure your features reflect what was known at timestamp t, not revised data.

Risk Metrics

Sharpe Ratio(Rp − Rf) / σpRisk-adjusted return; higher is better

Transaction Costs & Slippage

Always subtract realistic costs. A strategy that looks amazing at 0 cost often disappears with 5-10 bps per trade. Model:

Net Return = Gross Return − (commission + spread/2 + slippage_estimate)

Overfitting & The Multiple Testing Problem

If you test 100 strategies, ~5 will appear significant at p<0.05 by chance. Solutions:

  • Out-of-sample testing on truly untouched data
  • Lower significance thresholds (Bonferroni, False Discovery Rate)
  • Simulate under the null (Monte Carlo permutation tests)

Colab Experiment: Backtest Framework

import numpy as np, pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Synthetic price and signal
np.random.seed(7)
n = 1000
price = 100 * np.exp(np.cumsum(np.random.normal(0.0002, 0.01, n)))
# Simple mean-reversion signal: z-score of 20-day returns
returns = pd.Series(price).pct_change()
z = (returns - returns.rolling(20).mean()) / returns.rolling(20).std()
signal = np.where(z > 1, -1, np.where(z < -1, 1, 0))  # short if high, long if low

# Backtest with transaction cost
tcost = 0.001  # 10 bps
position = pd.Series(signal).shift(1).fillna(0)  # no lookahead
strategy_returns = position * returns - np.abs(position.diff()) * tcost
cumulative = (1 + strategy_returns.fillna(0)).cumprod()
buyhold = (1 + returns.fillna(0)).cumprod()

plt.figure(figsize=(10,4))
plt.plot(cumulative, label='Strategy', color='#38bdf8')
plt.plot(buyhold, label='Buy & Hold', color='#94a3b8', alpha=0.7)
plt.title('Simple Mean-Reversion Backtest'); plt.legend(); plt.yscale('log')
plt.grid(True, alpha=0.3); plt.show()

# Metrics
def sharpe(r): return np.sqrt(252) * r.mean() / r.std()
print('Strategy Sharpe:', sharpe(strategy_returns))
print('Max Drawdown:', ((cumulative / cumulative.cummax()) - 1).min())
