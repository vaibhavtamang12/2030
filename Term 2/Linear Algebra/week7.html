Orthogonality & Gram-Schmidt

A comprehensive, visual, and interactive study guide for aspiring quant engineers. From inner products to QR decomposition, risk factor orthogonalization, and stable regression — fully offline-capable.

Prerequisites & Learning Roadmap

Before diving into orthogonality, you need a solid foundation. Think of these as the "alphabet" before you can read the "sentences" of linear algebra. Each concept below is explained in the simplest possible terms.

Vectors & Vector Spaces

A vector is just an arrow with magnitude and direction, or a list of numbers. A vector space is a collection of vectors where you can add them and scale them, and the result stays in the collection. Think of it as a "playground" where vectors live.

Linear Independence

Vectors are linearly independent if none of them can be built from the others. If one vector is just a scaled copy of another, they are dependent. Independence means each vector brings genuinely new information.

Span & Basis

The span of vectors is all possible vectors you can reach by combining them. A basis is the smallest set of independent vectors that can reach every point in the space. It is like a coordinate system.

Matrices as Transformations

A matrix is a grid of numbers that acts like a function: it takes a vector and transforms it (rotates, stretches, projects). Matrix multiplication is applying one transformation after another.

Transpose (AT)

The transpose flips a matrix over its diagonal: rows become columns. It is crucial because (AB)T = BTAT, and it connects dot products to matrix form: uTv.

Norms (Length of a Vector)

The norm (or length) of a vector v is ||v|| = sqrt(v12 + v22 + ...). It measures "how big" a vector is. A vector with norm 1 is called a unit vector.

Why this order matters

You cannot understand orthogonality without knowing what a dot product is. You cannot understand projections without orthogonality. You cannot understand Gram-Schmidt without projections. And you cannot understand QR without Gram-Schmidt. This guide follows this exact dependency chain.

1. Inner Products: The Foundation of Everything

The inner product is the single most important operation in this entire guide. It is a way to multiply two vectors and get a single number. That number tells you "how much" the vectors point in the same direction.

1.1 What is an Inner Product?

Formally, an inner product is a function that takes two vectors and returns a scalar (a number), satisfying four rules:

  1. Symmetry: <u, v> = <v, u>
  2. Linearity: <au + bw, v> = a<u,v> + b<w,v>
  3. Positive-definite: <v, v> >= 0, and equals 0 only if v = 0

1.2 The Dot Product (Standard Inner Product)

In Rn, the most common inner product is the dot product:

u · v = uTv = u1v1 + u2v2 + ... + unvn

Intuition: Multiply matching components and sum. If both vectors point "the same way," the sum is large and positive. If they point "opposite ways," it is negative. If they are perpendicular, it is zero.

Figure 1.1: Geometric Meaning of Dot Product

Key insight: The dot product encodes both lengths and the angle between vectors. When θ = 90°, cos(θ) = 0, so u·v = 0. This is the geometric origin of orthogonality.

1.3 Inner Product as "Overlap"

Think of the dot product as measuring overlap or agreement:

  • u·v > 0: Vectors point in generally the same direction (acute angle)
  • u·v < 0: Vectors point in generally opposite directions (obtuse angle)
  • u·v = 0: Vectors are perpendicular (orthogonal) — no overlap at all

1.4 General Inner Products (Beyond Dot Product)

In quant finance, we often need weighted inner products. For example, if different assets have different variances, we might define:

<u, v>W = uTWv

where W is a positive-definite weight matrix (often the inverse covariance matrix in portfolio theory). This is called a Mahalanobis-like inner product.

Quant Connection

In portfolio theory, the "overlap" between two return streams is their covariance. Covariance is essentially an inner product centered at the mean. When we orthogonalize risk factors, we are making their covariances (inner products) equal to zero.

1.6 Worked Example: Computing a Dot Product

Let u = [2, 3] and v = [4, 1] in R2.

u·v = (2)(4) + (3)(1) = 8 + 3 = 11

Now compute norms:

||u|| = sqrt(4 + 9) = sqrt(13) ≈ 3.606
||v|| = sqrt(16 + 1) = sqrt(17) ≈ 4.123

Therefore, the angle between them:

cos(θ) = 11 / (3.606 × 4.123) ≈ 11 / 14.87 ≈ 0.739
θ ≈ arccos(0.739) ≈ 42.3°

Since the dot product is positive, the angle is acute (less than 90°).

2. Orthogonal & Orthonormal Vectors

Now that we understand inner products, we can define the core concepts of this guide. Orthogonal means "perpendicular" in higher dimensions. Orthonormal means "perpendicular AND unit length."

2.1 Definition: Orthogonal Vectors

Two vectors u and v are orthogonal if their inner product is zero:

u ⊥ v ⟺ <u, v> = 0

Intuition: They have "nothing in common." No component of one lies along the other. In 2D, this means a 90° angle. In 100D, it means the same thing — just harder to visualize.

Figure 2.1: Orthogonal vs Non-Orthogonal Vectors

2.2 Definition: Orthonormal Vectors

A set of vectors is orthonormal if:

  1. Every pair is orthogonal: <qi, qj> = 0 for i ≠ j
  2. Every vector has unit length: ||qi|| = 1 for all i
<qi, qj> = δij = { 1 if i=j, 0 if i≠j }

The symbol δij is the Kronecker delta. It is a compact way to write both conditions at once.

The Standard Basis is Orthonormal

In R3, the vectors e1 = [1,0,0], e2 = [0,1,0], e3 = [0,0,1] are orthonormal. They are the "default" coordinate axes. But orthonormal bases are not unique — there are infinitely many rotated versions!

2.3 Why Orthonormal Bases Are Magical

Suppose {q1, q2, ..., qn} is an orthonormal basis. Any vector v can be written as:

v = c1q1 + c2q2 + ... + cnqn

Here is the magic: to find any coefficient ci, just take the inner product!

ci = <v, qi>

Why? Because all cross-terms vanish due to orthogonality:

<v, qi> = <c1q1 + ... + cnqn, qi>
= c1<q1,qi> + ... + ci<qi,qi> + ... + cn<qn,qi>
= 0 + ... + ci(1) + ... + 0 = ci

This makes computations stable and simple. No need to solve a system of equations.

2.4 Orthogonal Matrices

A square matrix Q is orthogonal if its columns are orthonormal. This gives it extraordinary properties:

QTQ = QQT = I ⟹ Q-1 = QT

Key implications:

  • Preserves length: ||Qx|| = ||x|| for any vector x
  • Preserves angles: <Qx, Qy> = <x,y>
  • Determinant is ±1: Represents pure rotation or reflection
  • Easy inverse: Just transpose! No expensive matrix inversion needed
Don't confuse "orthogonal matrix" with "orthogonal vectors"

An orthogonal matrix is a matrix whose columns are orthonormal (not just orthogonal). The naming is slightly misleading — some texts call them "orthonormal matrices" to be precise, but "orthogonal matrix" is standard.

2.5 Worked Example: Checking Orthonormality

Consider these two vectors in R2:

q₁ = [1/√2, 1/√2] ≈ [0.707, 0.707]
q₂ = [-1/√2, 1/√2] ≈ [-0.707, 0.707]

Step 1: Check orthogonality (dot product):

q₁·q₂ = (1/√2)(-1/√2) + (1/√2)(1/√2) = -1/2 + 1/2 = 0 ✓

Step 2: Check unit length:

||q₁||² = (1/√2)² + (1/√2)² = 1/2 + 1/2 = 1 ✓
||q₂||² = (-1/√2)² + (1/√2)² = 1/2 + 1/2 = 1 ✓

They are orthonormal. They form a 45° rotated coordinate system.

3. Projections: Decomposing Vectors

A projection is the "shadow" that one vector casts onto another (or onto a subspace). Projections are the engine behind Gram-Schmidt, least squares regression, and hedging ratios.

3.1 Projection onto a Vector

Given vectors a and b, the projection of b onto a is the closest point to b that lies on the line through a.

Figure 3.1: Projection of b onto a

The projection is the component of b that lies along a. The error vector e is what remains — it is orthogonal to a by construction. This orthogonality of the error is the key to least squares.

3.2 The Projection Formula

proja(b) = [(aTb) / (aTa)] a

The scalar (aTb)/(aTa) is the "amount" of a needed to best approximate b. It is exactly the coefficient you would get in simple linear regression of b on a.

3.3 Projection onto a Subspace

What if we want to project onto a plane (or higher-dimensional subspace) spanned by multiple vectors? Let A be a matrix whose columns are the basis vectors of the subspace. The projection of b onto col(A) is:

projcol(A)(b) = A(ATA)-1ATb

The matrix P = A(ATA)-1AT is called the projection matrix. It has special properties:

  • P2 = P (idempotent: projecting twice is the same as projecting once)
  • PT = P (symmetric)
  • Eigenvalues are only 0 or 1
Quant Connection: Hedging Ratios

In quant finance, if b is a portfolio return and A contains factor returns (market, size, value), then projA(b) is the systematic (hedgeable) component, and e = b - projA(b) is the idiosyncratic (alpha) component. The coefficients (ATA)-1ATb are the hedging ratios — how much of each factor to hold to replicate the portfolio.

3.4 When Columns Are Orthonormal

If the columns of A are orthonormal (call it Q), the formula becomes trivially simple:

QTQ = I ⟹ projcol(Q)(b) = QQTb

No matrix inversion needed! This numerical stability is why we orthogonalize before projecting in quant models.

3.5 Worked Example: Projection in R²

Let a = [3, 4] and b = [2, 5].

Step 1: Compute dot products

a·b = 3(2) + 4(5) = 6 + 20 = 26
a·a = 3(3) + 4(4) = 9 + 16 = 25

Step 2: Compute scalar coefficient

c = 26/25 = 1.04

Step 3: Multiply by a

proja(b) = 1.04 × [3, 4] = [3.12, 4.16]

Step 4: Compute error and verify orthogonality

e = b - proja(b) = [2,5] - [3.12,4.16] = [-1.12, 0.84]
a·e = 3(-1.12) + 4(0.84) = -3.36 + 3.36 = 0 ✓

The error is indeed orthogonal to a. This verifies our projection is correct.

4. Gram-Schmidt Process: Building Orthonormal Bases

The Gram-Schmidt process is an algorithm that takes a set of linearly independent vectors and converts them into an orthonormal set with the same span. It is the bridge from "any basis" to "a perfect basis."

4.1 The Core Idea

Given vectors {a1, a2, ..., an}, we want to produce {q1, q2, ..., qn} such that:

  • Each qi has unit length
  • qi ⊥ qj for all i ≠ j
  • span{a1,...,ak} = span{q1,...,qk} for every k

The strategy: Process vectors one by one. For each new vector, subtract its projection onto all previously built q's. What remains is orthogonal to everything before it. Then normalize.

4.2 The Algorithm (Step by Step)

Step 1: u₁ = a₁, q₁ = u₁ / ||u₁||

Step 2: u₂ = a₂ - projq₁(a₂) = a₂ - (q₁Ta₂)q₁, q₂ = u₂ / ||u₂||

Step 3: u₃ = a₃ - projq₁(a₃) - projq₂(a₃) = a₃ - (q₁Ta₃)q₁ - (q₂Ta₃)q₂, q₃ = u₃ / ||u₃||

Step k: uk = ak - Σi=1k-1 (qiTak)qi, qk = uk / ||uk||
Figure 4.1: Gram-Schmidt Visualization in R²

4.3 Why Gram-Schmidt Works

At each step k, we take ak and remove all components that lie in the direction of previously constructed q1,...,qk-1. By the projection theorem, the residual uk is orthogonal to the entire subspace spanned by previous q's. Normalizing just scales it to unit length without changing direction.

Numerical Stability Warning

Classical Gram-Schmidt is numerically unstable when vectors are nearly linearly dependent. In quant finance, where risk factors are often highly correlated, use Modified Gram-Schmidt or Householder reflections instead. Modified GS orthogonalizes iteratively rather than all-at-once, reducing roundoff error accumulation.

4.4 Modified Gram-Schmidt (MGS)

The difference is subtle but crucial for numerical accuracy:

Classical: uk = ak - Σi<k projqi(ak) (all projections computed from original ak)

Modified: For each i from 1 to k-1: ak ← ak - projqi(ak) (update ak in place)
Then qk = ak / ||ak||

MGS is the standard in numerical libraries (LAPACK, numpy's QR).

4.5 Worked Example: Gram-Schmidt in R³

Given:

a₁ = [1, 1, 0]
a₂ = [1, 0, 1]
a₃ = [0, 1, 1]

Step 1: q₁ from a₁

||a₁|| = sqrt(1+1+0) = sqrt(2)
q₁ = [1/√2, 1/√2, 0] ≈ [0.707, 0.707, 0]

Step 2: q₂ from a₂

q₁Ta₂ = (1/√2)(1) + (1/√2)(0) + 0 = 1/√2
u₂ = a₂ - (1/√2)q₁ = [1,0,1] - [1/2, 1/2, 0] = [1/2, -1/2, 1]
||u₂|| = sqrt(1/4 + 1/4 + 1) = sqrt(1.5) ≈ 1.225
q₂ = [0.408, -0.408, 0.816]

Step 3: q₃ from a₃

q₁Ta₃ = 1/√2, q₂Ta₃ = -0.408 + 0.816 = 0.408
u₃ = a₃ - (1/√2)q₁ - 0.408q₂
= [0,1,1] - [0.5,0.5,0] - [0.167, -0.167, 0.333]
= [-0.667, 0.667, 0.667]
||u₃|| = sqrt(0.444+0.444+0.444) = sqrt(1.333) ≈ 1.155
q₃ = [-0.577, 0.577, 0.577]

Verification: q₁·q₂ = 0, q₁·q₃ = 0, q₂·q₃ = 0. All unit length. ✓

5. QR Decomposition

QR decomposition factorizes any matrix A into an orthogonal matrix Q and an upper triangular matrix R. It is one of the most important matrix factorizations in numerical computing and quantitative finance.

5.1 The Factorization

A = QR

where QTQ = I (Q has orthonormal columns)
and R is upper triangular (all entries below diagonal are zero)
Figure 5.1: QR Decomposition Structure
ij = qiTaj • diag(R) > 0 • Unique if A full rank

5.2 How Gram-Schmidt Produces QR

Run Gram-Schmidt on the columns of A. The resulting orthonormal vectors form Q. The upper triangular matrix R captures the "recipe" of how each original column is built from the q's:

a₁ = (q₁Ta₁) q₁ + 0 + 0 + ...
a₂ = (q₁Ta₂) q₁ + (q₂Ta₂) q₂ + 0 + ...
a₃ = (q₁Ta₃) q₁ + (q₂Ta₃) q₂ + (q₃Ta₃) q₃ + ...
...

In matrix form: A = QR where Rij = qiTaj for i ≤ j, and Rij = 0 for i > j

5.3 Solving Linear Systems with QR

Given Ax = b, substitute A = QR:

QRx = b
Multiply both sides by QT: Rx = QTb
Solve for x by back-substitution (R is triangular)

Advantages over Gaussian elimination:

  • No need to compute A-1 explicitly
  • QT is trivial to compute (just transpose)
  • Numerically stable (orthogonal transformations preserve conditioning)
  • Works for rectangular matrices (least squares)

5.4 Least Squares via QR

For overdetermined systems (more equations than unknowns), the least squares solution minimizes ||Ax - b||²:

x* = R-1QTb (solve Rx = QTb by back-substitution)

This is numerically far superior to the normal equations (ATA)x = ATb, which square the condition number and amplify errors.

Quant Connection: Stable Regression

In quant finance, regressing returns on correlated factors using (XTX)-1XTy can be numerically unstable when factors are collinear. Using QR decomposition (as numpy.linalg.lstsq does internally) gives the same coefficients but with much better numerical stability. The Q matrix represents orthogonalized risk factors, and R contains the regression coefficients in the orthogonalized basis.

5.5 Worked Example: QR of a 2×2 Matrix

Let A = [[3, 1], [4, 2]]. Columns are a₁ = [3,4] and a₂ = [1,2].

Step 1: q₁ from a₁

||a₁|| = 5
q₁ = [3/5, 4/5] = [0.6, 0.8]

Step 2: Orthogonalize a₂ against q₁

q₁Ta₂ = 0.6(1) + 0.8(2) = 0.6 + 1.6 = 2.2
u₂ = a₂ - 2.2q₁ = [1,2] - [1.32, 1.76] = [-0.32, 0.24]
||u₂|| = sqrt(0.1024 + 0.0576) = sqrt(0.16) = 0.4
q₂ = [-0.32/0.4, 0.24/0.4] = [-0.8, 0.6]

Step 3: Build R

R₁₁ = q₁Ta₁ = 5
R₁₂ = q₁Ta₂ = 2.2
R₂₂ = q₂Ta₂ = (-0.8)(1) + (0.6)(2) = -0.8 + 1.2 = 0.4

Q = [[0.6, -0.8], [0.8, 0.6]]
R = [[5, 2.2], [0, 0.4]]

Verify: QR = [[0.6×5 + (-0.8)×0, 0.6×2.2 + (-0.8)×0.4], [0.8×5 + 0.6×0, 0.8×2.2 + 0.6×0.4]] = [[3, 1], [4, 2]] = A ✓

6. Quantitative Finance Applications

Now we connect the mathematics to real quant engineering problems. These are not toy examples — they reflect actual workflows in systematic trading, risk management, and portfolio construction.

6.1 Application 1: Orthogonalizing Risk Factors

In multi-factor risk models (like Barra, Axioma, or custom quant models), factors like Market, Size, Value, Momentum, and Industry exposures are often correlated. Correlated factors make it impossible to cleanly attribute risk: if Value and Momentum are correlated, how much risk comes from each?

The Problem

Given correlated factor returns F = [f₁, f₂, ..., fₖ], we want uncorrelated (orthogonal) factors F̃ = [f̃₁, f̃₂, ..., f̃ₖ] such that each f̃ᵢ represents a pure, independent source of risk.

The Gram-Schmidt Solution

Treat each factor's historical return series as a vector in RT (where T is the number of time periods). Apply Gram-Schmidt:

f̃₁ = f₁ / ||f₁||
f̃₂ = [f₂ - projf̃₁(f₂)] / ||...||
f̃₃ = [f₃ - projf̃₁(f₃) - projf̃₂(f₃)] / ||...||
...

The resulting factors have zero sample covariance (their dot products are zero). Now risk attribution is clean: the variance of a portfolio along f̃ᵢ is purely from that factor, with no double-counting.

Ordering Matters

In Gram-Schmidt, the first vector is unchanged (except normalized). So if you put "Market" first, it remains "Market." Subsequent factors are purified of Market exposure. This lets you build a hierarchical risk model:

  1. f̃₁ = Market (raw market return)
  2. f̃₂ = Size orthogonal to Market (size effect with market beta removed)
  3. f̃₃ = Value orthogonal to Market and Size
  4. f̃₄ = Momentum orthogonal to Market, Size, and Value
Alternative: Schweinler-Wigner / Löwdin Orthogonalization

Unlike Gram-Schmidt, which is sequential and order-dependent, the Schweinler-Wigner (SW) method [^6^] orthogonalizes all factors symmetrically. It finds the orthonormal basis closest to the original factors in a least-squares sense. This is useful when no factor should be privileged over another.

6.2 Application 2: Stable Regression (Multicollinearity)

When regressing stock returns on multiple factors, multicollinearity (high correlation among regressors) makes the normal equations (XTX)-1XTy unstable. The matrix XTX becomes nearly singular, and small changes in data cause huge swings in coefficients.

QR-Based Regression

Decompose the design matrix X = QR:

β = (XTX)-1XTy ← Unstable when XTX is ill-conditioned

Better: X = QR
β = R-1QTy ← Stable! QTQ = I, no squaring of condition number

Why is this stable? The condition number of Q is 1 (perfect). The condition number of R equals that of X. We never form XTX, whose condition number is the square of X's. In quant terms: if your factors have correlation 0.99, XTX has effective correlation 0.9801, but QR works directly with the 0.99 level.

Geometric Interpretation

Q represents the same factor space as X, but with axes at right angles to each other. Regression in the Q-basis is trivial: each coefficient is just qiTy. The R matrix then transforms these orthogonal coefficients back to the original (interpretable) factor loadings.

6.3 Application 3: Hedging Ratios via Projections

A portfolio manager holds a complex position (e.g., an exotic option, a basket of illiquid stocks, or a custom index). They want to hedge it using liquid instruments (futures, ETFs, or standard options).

The Projection Framework

Let b be the P&L vector of the position to hedge. Let A = [a₁, a₂, ..., aₖ] be the P&L vectors of available hedging instruments. The optimal hedge is the projection of b onto the span of A:

hedge = projcol(A)(b) = A(ATA)-1ATb = Aβ
where β = (ATA)-1ATb are the hedging ratios

The residual e = b - Aβ is the unhedgeable risk (the "alpha" or idiosyncratic component). By the projection theorem, this residual is orthogonal to all hedging instruments — you cannot hedge it further with the given tools.

Using QR for Hedging

If hedging instruments are correlated (e.g., SPY and QQQ are highly correlated), compute A = QR first:

β = R-1QTb
hedge = Q(QTb) = QQTb

The coefficients in the Q-basis (QTb) tell you how much of each independent risk dimension to hold. R then maps these back to actual instrument notionals.

Practical Note: Hedging Frequency

In practice, hedging ratios β are re-estimated daily or weekly using rolling windows. Using QR instead of normal equations prevents numerical blow-ups when markets become highly correlated during stress events (when XTX becomes nearly singular).

Experiment 2: Gram-Schmidt from Scratch

Python · Colab
import numpy as np

def gram_schmidt(A):
    """
    Classical Gram-Schmidt on columns of A.
    Returns Q (orthonormal) and R (upper triangular).
    """
    m, n = A.shape
    Q = np.zeros((m, n))
    R = np.zeros((n, n))

    for j in range(n):
        v = A[:, j].copy()
        for i in range(j):
            R[i, j] = np.dot(Q[:, i], A[:, j])
            v -= R[i, j] * Q[:, i]
        R[j, j] = np.linalg.norm(v)
        Q[:, j] = v / R[j, j]

    return Q, R

# Test with a 3x3 matrix
A = np.array([[1, 1, 0],
              [1, 0, 1],
              [0, 1, 1]], dtype=float)

Q, R = gram_schmidt(A)
print("Q:
", np.round(Q, 4))
print("
R:
", np.round(R, 4))
print("
Q^T Q (should be identity):
", np.round(Q.T @ Q, 4))
print("
QR (should equal A):
", np.round(Q @ R, 4))

# Check orthogonality
print("
Dot products between columns of Q:")
for i in range(3):
    for j in range(i+1, 3):
        print(f"  q{i+1}·q{j+1} = {np.dot(Q[:,i], Q[:,j]):.2e}")

Experiment 3: Modified Gram-Schmidt vs Classical

import numpy as np

def modified_gram_schmidt(A):
    m, n = A.shape
    Q = A.copy()
    R = np.zeros((n, n))

    for i in range(n):
        R[i, i] = np.linalg.norm(Q[:, i])
        Q[:, i] /= R[i, i]
        for j in range(i+1, n):
            R[i, j] = np.dot(Q[:, i], Q[:, j])
            Q[:, j] -= R[i, j] * Q[:, i]

    return Q, R

# Create nearly dependent columns
eps = 1e-10
A = np.array([[1, 1, 1],
              [1, 1, 1],
              [1, 1, 1+eps]], dtype=float)

Q_classical, _ = gram_schmidt(A)

Hard
  1. Prove that the Gram-Schmidt process produces an orthonormal set. Use induction on the number of vectors.
  2. Show that if A = QR via Gram-Schmidt, then R is upper triangular with positive diagonal entries. What is the geometric meaning of Rᵢᵢ?

3. For each j, inner products cost ~2m(j-1) flops, updates cost ~2m(j-1), normalization ~3m. Total ~ Σⱼ 4mj = 2mn² flops. Householder QR costs ~2mn² - (2/3)n³, comparable for m≫n but Householder is more stable.

Easy

  2. If A = QR, what is AᵀA in terms of R? Simplify using properties of Q.
  3. Use QR to solve Ax = b where A = [[1,1],[0,1],[1,0]] and b = [1,2,3]ᵀ.

2. AᵀA = (QR)ᵀ(QR) = RᵀQᵀQR = RᵀR. This is the Cholesky factorization of AᵀA.

3. Q = [[1/√2, 1/√6], [0, 2/√6], [1/√2, -1/√6]], R = [[√2, 1/√2], [0, √(3/2)]]. Solve Rx = Qᵀb by back-substitution.
Medium
  1. Prove that the QR decomposition of a full-rank matrix is unique if we require R to have positive diagonal entries.
  2. Show that for any orthogonal matrix Q, ||QA||_F = ||A||_F where ||·||_F is the Frobenius norm.
  3. Explain how QR decomposition can be used to find eigenvalues via the QR algorithm (basic idea).
Show Solutions
1. Suppose A = Q₁R₁ = Q₂R₂. Then Q₂ᵀQ₁ = R₂R₁⁻¹. Left side orthogonal, right side upper triangular with positive diagonals. The only such matrix is I. Thus Q₁=Q₂, R₁=R₂.

2. ||QA||_F² = tr((QA)ᵀ(QA)) = tr(AᵀQᵀQA) = tr(AᵀA) = ||A||_F². Orthogonal transformations preserve Frobenius norm.

3. QR algorithm: iterate Aₖ = QₖRₖ, then Aₖ₊₁ = RₖQₖ. Each Aₖ is similar to A (same eigenvalues). Under conditions, Aₖ converges to upper triangular (Schur form), revealing eigenvalues on diagonal.
Hard

  2. Prove that cond(R) = cond(A) when A = QR, but cond(AᵀA) = cond(A)². Explain why this makes QR-based least squares superior.

1. Ax-b = QRx-b. Let c=Qᵀb. ||QRx-b||² = ||Rx-c||² + ||(I-QQᵀ)b||² (Pythagorean). Minimized when Rx=c, giving residual norm ||(I-QQᵀ)b||.

2. cond(R) = ||R|| ||R⁻¹|| = ||QᵀA|| ||A⁻¹Q|| = ||A|| ||A⁻¹|| = cond(A) (since Q preserves norms). cond(AᵀA) = cond(RᵀR) = cond(R)² = cond(A)². Squaring amplifies numerical errors.

3. G = [[c, s], [-s, c]] where c=a/√(a²+b²), s=b/√(a²+b²). Then G[a,b]ᵀ = [√(a²+b²), 0]ᵀ. For m×n A, apply Givens rotations to zero out (2,1), (3,1), ..., (m,1), then (3,2), ..., building Q as product of rotations.

Hard
  1. (Factor risk model) Let X be the T×k matrix of factor returns. Show that the sample covariance of factors is (1/T)XᵀX if factors are centered. If X = QR, express the covariance in terms of R and discuss the implications for portfolio optimization.
  2. (Hedging with transaction costs) Suppose hedging instrument i has transaction cost cᵢ per dollar traded. Formulate the optimal hedge as a constrained optimization problem and show how QR preprocessing simplifies the solution.

2. Minimize ||b - Aβ||² + λΣcᵢ|βᵢ|. With A=QR, let γ=Rβ. Problem becomes ||Qᵀb - γ||² + λΣcᵢ|(R⁻¹γ)ᵢ|. Still complex, but QR reveals the effective dimensionality and condition number.

PCA is variance-optimal but less interpretable; GS is interpretable but order-dependent.
