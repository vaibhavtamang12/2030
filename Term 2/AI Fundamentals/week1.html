P1What is an Algorithm?

An algorithm is simply a step-by-step recipe for solving a problem. Think of it like a cooking recipe: given certain ingredients (inputs), you follow specific steps (process), and end up with a dish (output). Algorithms are deterministic — the same inputs always produce the same outputs.

🍳 Real-world Analogy Imagine you're making chai. You boil water → add tea leaves → add milk → add sugar → strain → serve. That's an algorithm. In programming, this becomes: INPUT: water, tea, milk, sugar → STEPS: boil, mix, strain → OUTPUT: chai.
🔷 Algorithm Structure

P2What is Data?

Data is any recorded information. In finance and quant work, data is your stock prices, order books, sentiment scores, economic indicators. In AI, data is the "experience" from which machines learn. The more quality data, the smarter the AI can become.

🔢
Structured Data
Organized in rows and columns. Examples: CSV files, stock price tables, spreadsheets. Easy for machines to process.
🖼️
Unstructured Data
Text, images, audio, video. Harder to process but rich in signal. E.g., earnings call transcripts, news articles.
⏱️
Time-Series Data
Data indexed by time. The bread and butter of quant finance — OHLCV bars, tick data, economic indicators.

P3What is a Model?

A model is a simplified mathematical representation of reality. Think of a map — it's not the actual terrain, but a useful simplification. In finance, you've used models like Black-Scholes. In AI, models are mathematical functions that take inputs (data) and produce outputs (predictions).

f(x) = y
A model is simply a function: given input x, produce output y

Topic 1

Defining AI, Machine Learning & Deep Learning

These three terms are the foundation. They're often used interchangeably in the news — but they're not the same thing. Understanding the relationship between them is critical before studying anything else.

Prerequisites ✓ AI Defined History AI Winters AI Types

01What is Artificial Intelligence?

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is the broad field of making machines perform tasks that would normally require human intelligence. This includes things like: recognizing faces in photos, translating languages, playing chess, or detecting fraud in financial transactions.

🎭 Simple Analogy Imagine you're hiring an employee. If that employee is a human, they use intelligence to complete tasks. Now imagine you could "teach" a machine to do the same tasks — automatically, without being explicitly programmed for every edge case. That's AI. The goal is to give machines the ability to "think" and "decide" the way humans do.

The formal definition from John McCarthy (one of AI's founding fathers): "AI is the science and engineering of making intelligent machines."

Notice what the definition does NOT say — it doesn't say "conscious" machines or "human-like" machines. AI is about solving problems intelligently, not necessarily the way humans do it.

02What is Machine Learning?

Machine Learning (ML) is a subset of AI. It's the idea that instead of explicitly programming rules, you give a machine data and let it learn the rules itself.

Traditional programming vs. Machine Learning — this is the key conceptual shift:

🔄 Traditional Programming vs Machine Learning
💡 Quant Analogy — Learning a Trading Rule Traditional: You say "BUY if RSI < 30 AND price > 200-day MA."

Machine Learning: You show the machine 10 years of historical data with labels ("this trade worked", "this didn't") and let it figure out which conditions predict profitable trades — including rules you might never have thought of.

The 3 Main Types of Machine Learning

🏷️
Supervised Learning
You give the machine labelled examples. "Here are 1000 emails, each tagged SPAM or NOT-SPAM. Learn to classify new ones."

Quant use: Predict next-day return (label = actual return).
🔍
Unsupervised Learning
No labels. The machine finds hidden structure on its own. "Here are 1000 stocks — group them by similarity."

Quant use: Clustering stocks into regimes, factor discovery.
🎮
Reinforcement Learning
Machine takes actions, gets rewards or penalties, and learns a strategy over time. Like training a dog with treats.

Quant use: Optimal execution, algorithmic trading agents.

03What is Deep Learning?

Deep Learning (DL) is a subset of machine learning that uses large neural networks with many layers (hence "deep"). The "depth" comes from stacking many layers of artificial neurons.

The key breakthrough of deep learning: instead of hand-crafting features (telling the machine "look at RSI, MACD, Bollinger Bands"), deep learning models automatically learn what features matter from raw data.

🔵 The AI → ML → DL Hierarchy (Nested Structure)
📌 Key Insight: Scope Deep Learning ⊂ Machine Learning ⊂ Artificial Intelligence. All deep learning is machine learning, and all machine learning is AI — but not all AI is machine learning, and not all ML is deep learning. A simple if-then rule is AI but not ML. A decision tree is ML but not DL.

How Do Neural Networks Actually Learn?

This is the "magic" of deep learning. The learning process has 4 steps, repeated thousands of times:

⚙️ The Training Loop
🎯 The Dart-Throwing Analogy Imagine throwing darts at a board blindfolded. After each throw, someone tells you "too far left, and a bit high." You adjust. You throw again. 04Scope & Limitations of AI

AI is powerful — but it has real limitations that every quant engineer must internalize to avoid expensive mistakes:

LimitationExplanationQuant Engineering Impact
Data Hungry Deep learning needs massive amounts of labelled data to perform well Financial data is scarce — only ~250 trading days/year. Models can overfit easily.
Black Box Hard to explain why a model made a prediction Regulators (MiFID II, SEC) demand explainability in trading systems
Non-stationarity Models trained on past data may fail when market regimes change A model trained pre-COVID may fail post-COVID. Constant retraining needed.
Correlation ≠ Causation ML finds patterns that correlate, not necessarily causal relationships A spurious correlation might work in backtest but fail in live trading
Adversarial Small deliberate changes in input can fool AI completely Market manipulation can fool AI trading systems if not robust
No Common Sense AI has no understanding of the world — only patterns in numbers AI won't "know" that war causes oil prices to spike unless that pattern is in training data
⚠️ Critical Quant Warning The biggest mistake quant engineers make with AI: overfitting. A model that memorizes your training data perfectly will fail miserably on new data. Always use proper walk-forward validation, not random cross-validation, for financial time series.

Build a simple supervised ML model that tries to predict whether tomorrow's stock return will be positive or negative, using basic technical indicators. This will give you a concrete feel for the AI/ML pipeline end to end.

# !pip install yfinance scikit-learn pandas matplotlib

import yfinance as yf
import pandas as pd
import numpy as np
from sklearn.tree import DecisionTreeClassifier
from sklearn.metrics import accuracy_score
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Step 1: Download data
print("📥 Downloading stock data...")
data = yf.download("SPY", start="2018-01-01", end="2023-12-31")
df = data[['Close', 'Volume']].copy()

# Step 2: Create features (what we use to predict)
df['Return_1d'] = df['Close'].pct_change()       # 1-day return
df['Return_5d'] = df['Close'].pct_change(5)       # 5-day return
df['MA_ratio'] = df['Close'] / df['Close'].rolling(20).mean()  # Price vs MA20

# Step 3: Create target (what we want to predict)
# Target: will tomorrow's return be positive?
df['Target'] = (df['Return_1d'].shift(-1) > 0).astype(int)

# Step 4: Clean up (remove NaN rows)
df.dropna(inplace=True)

# Step 5: Split into features (X) and target (y)
features = ['Return_1d', 'Return_5d', 'MA_ratio']
X = df[features]
y = df['Target']

# Step 6: Time-based train/test split (IMPORTANT for finance!)
# Never use random split for time series — future data leaks into past!
split = int(len(df) * 0.8)
X_train, X_test = X.iloc[:split], X.iloc[split:]
y_train, y_test = y.iloc[:split], y.iloc[split:]

# Step 7: Train the model
print("\n🤖 Training Decision Tree...")
model = DecisionTreeClassifier(max_depth=3, random_state=42)
model.fit(X_train, y_train)

# Step 8: Evaluate
predictions = model.predict(X_test)
accuracy = accuracy_score(y_test, predictions)
print(f"\n✅ Accuracy: {accuracy:.1%}")
print(f"   Baseline (always predict UP): {y_test.mean():.1%}")
print(f"\n💡 If accuracy > baseline, the model has learned something!")

# Step 9: Feature importance
importance = pd.Series(model.feature_importances_, index=features)
print("\n📊 Feature Importance (how much each feature helped):")
for feat, imp in importance.sort_values(ascending=False).items():
    bar = '█' * int(imp * 30)
    print(f"  {feat:20s} {bar} {imp:.1%}")

The History of AI: From Turing to Transformers

Understanding AI history isn't just academic — it teaches you why certain approaches work, what has been tried before, and how we arrived at today's models. Every "new" idea in AI has roots in decades of prior work.

Prerequisites ✓ AI Defined ✓ History AI Winters AI Types

01The Turing Test — Where It All Began (1950)

In 1950, mathematician Alan Turing asked a deceptively simple question in his paper "Computing Machinery and Intelligence": "Can machines think?" Since "thinking" is hard to define, he proposed a practical test.

🧪 The Turing Test — How It Works
🧩 Why Does This Matter? The Turing Test set the agenda for AI for decades. It said: intelligence is about behavior, not about being biological. This behaviorist view drove early AI research toward language, reasoning, and problem-solving. Today, LLMs like GPT-4 arguably pass the Turing Test in conversation — Turing's question is no longer theoretical.

02The Full AI Timeline

Here's the complete journey from Turing's paper to today's AI renaissance, with every major milestone explained:

1936 — Foundation
Turing Machine (Theoretical)
Alan Turing describes a theoretical machine that can compute anything computable. This is the mathematical foundation of all computers and, later, all AI.
1943
First Neural Network Model — McCulloch & Pitts
Warren McCulloch and Walter Pitts create a mathematical model of a biological neuron. They show that simple neuron-like units can compute logical operations. This is the ancestor of all neural networks.
1950
Turing Test Proposed
Alan Turing publishes "Computing Machinery and Intelligence." He proposes the imitation game as a test for machine intelligence and predicts that machines will be able to pass it by year 2000.
1956 — 🎉 Birth of AI
Dartmouth Conference — AI is Named
John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, Claude Shannon, and others gather at Dartmouth College. McCarthy coins the term "Artificial Intelligence." The conference marks the official birth of AI as a field. The organizers optimistically believed human-level AI would be achieved within a generation.
1957
Perceptron — First Learning Machine
Frank Rosenblatt creates the Perceptron, the first neural network that could actually learn from data. The US Navy funds it, envisioning it could recognize submarines. It could learn simple patterns but failed at complex ones (like XOR).
1966–1972 — ❄️ First AI Winter
Hype Crashes into Reality
Early AI programs were brittle — they worked on tiny toy problems but collapsed in the real world. Machine translation failed. The Lighthill Report (UK) devastatingly criticized AI progress. Funding was cut globally.
1974–1980
Expert Systems Rise
A new approach: encode human expert knowledge as "if-then" rules. MYCIN diagnoses bacterial infections. DENDRAL identifies chemical structures. These worked but required enormous manual effort and couldn't learn from data.
1986 — 🔑 Key Breakthrough
Backpropagation Popularized — Neural Nets Revive
Rumelhart, Hinton, and Williams publish the landmark paper on backpropagation — the algorithm that allows neural networks to learn from errors by propagating error signals backward through layers. This is still the core training algorithm of all deep learning today.
1987–1993 — ❄️ Second AI Winter
Expert Systems Collapse, Neural Nets Stall
Expert systems proved too expensive to maintain. LISP machines were rendered obsolete by cheap PCs. Neural networks were limited by computing power and data. Another funding crash.
1997
Deep Blue Defeats Kasparov
IBM's Deep Blue beats chess world champion Garry Kasparov in a 6-game match. A massive milestone for AI — though Deep Blue used search trees and evaluation functions, not machine learning. Still, it showed AI could beat humans at complex cognitive tasks.
1998
LeCun's CNN — Handwriting Recognition
Yann LeCun demonstrates LeNet, a Convolutional Neural Network, reading handwritten digits on checks. This was a watershed moment: neural networks learning directly from raw pixels. The architecture he developed still underlies modern image AI.
2006 — 🔥 Deep Learning Reborn
Hinton & Bengio Revive Neural Networks
Geoffrey Hinton (later called "Godfather of AI") and Yoshua Bengio publish papers showing that deep neural networks CAN be trained effectively with new techniques (unsupervised pretraining, better weight initialization). This reignites neural network research after years of dormancy.
2012 — 💥 The ImageNet Revolution
AlexNet — Deep Learning Conquers Vision
Hinton's student Alex Krizhevsky trains AlexNet — a deep CNN — on ImageNet, a dataset of 1.2 million images. It crushes the competition by a historic 10% margin. The AI community is stunned. GPUs and big data had unlocked deep learning's potential. This single event is often called the start of the modern AI era.
2014
GANs — Machines That Create
Ian Goodfellow invents Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) — one network generates fake images, another tries to detect fakes. They compete until the generator creates photorealistic images. The beginning of AI creativity.
2016
AlphaGo Defeats Lee Sedol at Go
DeepMind's AlphaGo beats the world's best Go player. Go has more possible positions than atoms in the universe — beating it with search algorithms alone was impossible. AlphaGo used reinforcement learning + Monte Carlo tree search + deep neural networks. A defining moment in AI history.
2017 — 🌟 Transformers Change Everything
"Attention Is All You Need" — Google Brain
The Transformer architecture is introduced. It replaced recurrent networks (RNNs) with a "self-attention" mechanism that could process entire sequences in parallel. Every modern LLM (GPT, BERT, Claude, Gemini) is built on this architecture. Arguably the most important paper in AI history since backpropagation.
2020
GPT-3 — Language at Scale
OpenAI releases GPT-3 with 175 billion parameters. It can write code, answer questions, translate — all from natural language prompts. No fine-tuning required. AI enters mainstream consciousness.
2022–present — 🚀 AI Renaissance
ChatGPT, Stable Diffusion, AlphaFold & Beyond
ChatGPT reaches 100M users in 2 months (fastest product in history). AI generates photorealistic images, folds proteins, writes code, controls robots. We are in an AI renaissance driven by: scale (bigger models), data (internet-scale), and compute (GPU clusters).

🧪 Colab Experiment #2 — Visualize the Perceptron (1957 First Neural Net)

Implement the original Perceptron algorithm from scratch in Python. This connects you directly to the 1957 breakthrough and shows you the fundamental learning mechanism that all modern AI descends from.

Neural Nets with Keras
Python — Perceptron from Scratch (1957 Algorithm) 
# =============================================
# EXPERIMENT 2: Build the 1957 Perceptron!
# =============================================
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

class Perceptron:
    """
    The original Perceptron — invented by Frank Rosenblatt in 1957.
    This is the ancestor of ALL modern neural networks.
    """
    def __init__(self, learning_rate=0.1, n_iterations=100):
        self.lr = learning_rate
        self.n_iter = n_iterations
        self.weights = None
        self.bias = None

    def step_function(self, x):
        """Simple threshold: output 1 if positive, 0 if negative"""
        return np.where(x >= 0, 1, 0)

    def fit(self, X, y):
        n_samples, n_features = X.shape
        self.weights = np.zeros(n_features)  # Start with zero weights
        self.bias = 0
        self.errors_per_epoch = []

        for epoch in range(self.n_iter):
            errors = 0
            for xi, yi in zip(X, y):
                # Forward pass: compute prediction
                prediction = self.step_function(np.dot(xi, self.weights) + self.bias)
                
                # Compute error
                error = yi - prediction
                
                # Update weights (this is the "learning" part!)
                # If we were wrong, adjust weights in the right direction
                self.weights += self.lr * error * xi
                self.bias += self.lr * error
                
                errors += int(error != 0)
            
            self.errors_per_epoch.append(errors)
            if errors == 0:
                print(f"✅ Converged at epoch {epoch}!")
                break

    def predict(self, X):
        return self.step_function(np.dot(X, self.weights) + self.bias)

# Create a simple dataset: classify stocks as "trending" or "not trending"
# Feature 1: 20-day momentum (normalized)
# Feature 2: Volume above average (normalized)
np.random.seed(42)

# Trending stocks: high momentum + high volume
trending = np.random.randn(50, 2) + np.array([2, 2])
# Non-trending stocks: low momentum + low volume
not_trending = np.random.randn(50, 2) + np.array([-2, -2])

X = np.vstack([trending, not_trending])
y = np.hstack([np.ones(50), np.zeros(50)])

# Shuffle
idx = np.random.permutation(len(y))
X, y = X[idx], y[idx]

# Train the 1957 Perceptron
print("🤖 Training the 1957 Perceptron...")
p = Perceptron(learning_rate=0.1, n_iterations=50)
p.fit(X, y)

# Accuracy
predictions = p.predict(X)
accuracy = np.mean(predictions == y)
print(f"\n📊 Accuracy: {accuracy:.1%}")
print(f"   Learned weights: {p.weights}")
print(f"   Learned bias: {p.bias:.3f}")

# Visualize
fig, (ax1, ax2) = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(12, 5))
fig.patch.set_facecolor('#08090d')

# Plot 1: Data + Decision Boundary
ax1.set_facecolor('#0f1117')
ax1.scatter(trending[:, 0], trending[:, 1], c='#00e5c0', s=30, label='Trending', alpha=0.7)
ax1.scatter(not_trending[:, 0], not_trending[:, 1], c='#ff6b6b', s=30, label='Not Trending', alpha=0.7)
# Decision boundary
x_range = np.linspace(-5, 6, 100)
y_boundary = -(p.weights[0] * x_range + p.bias) / (p.weights[1] + 1e-9)
ax1.plot(x_range, y_boundary, '#ffd166', linewidth=2, label='Decision Boundary')
ax1.set_xlabel('Momentum', color='#7a7f99')
ax1.set_ylabel('Volume Signal', color='#7a7f99')
ax1.set_title('Perceptron — Stock Classification', color='#e8eaf0', fontweight='bold')
ax1.legend(facecolor='#161820', edgecolor='#1e2130', labelcolor='#c8cad8')
ax1.tick_params(colors='#4a4f6a')

# Plot 2: Learning curve

Topic 3

AI Winters & the Current AI Renaissance

AI didn't grow smoothly from 1950 to today. It went through painful "winters" — periods of funding cuts and lost faith. Understanding why they happened prevents repeating the same mistakes in your own work.

Prerequisites ✓ AI Defined ✓ History ✓ AI Winters AI Types

01What is an AI Winter?

An AI Winter is a period of drastically reduced interest, funding, and progress in AI research — caused by a gap between inflated expectations and actual results. The term is borrowed from "nuclear winter" — a catastrophic cooling that follows a disaster.

📉 The Hype Cycle Analogy Think of AI winters as the "trough of disillusionment" in the Gartner Hype Cycle. In finance, this is like a bubble: assets become overvalued (hype), then crash when reality doesn't match expectations (winter). AI winters happened for the same reason — researchers overpromised, governments overfunded, and results underdelivered.
📊 The AI Hype Cycle — Two Winters Visualized

02First AI Winter (1966–1973) — Deep Dive

The first winter was triggered by a devastating 1966 report by the Automatic Language Processing Advisory Committee (ALPAC), which concluded that machine translation was twice as expensive and twice as slow as human translation — and far less accurate.

💬
The ALPAC Report (1966)
The US government had poured money into machine translation, expecting to automate Russian-to-English translation during the Cold War. ALPAC declared it a failure. Funding was cut overnight.
🚫
Perceptron Limitations Exposed
Marvin Minsky's book "Perceptrons" (1969) mathematically proved that single-layer perceptrons couldn't solve simple problems like XOR. This killed neural network funding for a decade.
📉
The Lighthill Report (1973)
UK government commissioned James Lighthill to assess AI. He concluded AI had failed to deliver promised results. UK funding for AI was decimated. Other countries followed.

Root Cause: Combinatorial Explosion

Early AI researchers dramatically underestimated complexity. Simple toy problems (10 pieces) scale to impossible problems (1000 pieces) exponentially. A chess board has more possible games than atoms in the universe — brute force doesn't scale.

Possible chess games ≈ 10120 > atoms in universe (≈ 1080)
This "combinatorial explosion" is why early search-based AI hit a wall. Modern AI learned to approximate rather than search exhaustively.

03Second AI Winter (1987–1993) — Deep Dive

The second winter was caused by the collapse of the Expert Systems bubble and the failure of hardware that was purpose-built for AI (LISP machines).

💸
Expert Systems Collapse
Companies spent millions on expert systems. Each required armies of "knowledge engineers" to manually encode expert knowledge. They broke down at edge cases. The maintenance cost was astronomical. The "AI Bubble" of the 1980s burst.
💻
LISP Machines Obsoleted
Specialized AI hardware (LISP machines by Symbolics and LISP Machine Inc.) were rendered obsolete by cheap, general-purpose desktop computers. The $10M LISP machine companies went bankrupt by 1987.
🔋
Neural Nets Hit a Wall
Despite backpropagation's promise, training deep networks required computing power and data that didn't exist yet. Results on real-world problems were disappointing. Funding dried up again.
⚠️ Lesson for Quant Engineers The AI winters happened because of "hype-driven investment" rather than "evidence-driven investment." As a quant engineer, you must distinguish between: (1) what AI can demonstrably do today, (2) what researchers believe it might do in 5 years, and (3) what salespeople claim. Apply Bayesian skepticism — extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence.

Topic 4

Narrow AI vs General AI vs Superintelligence

These three categories describe the scope of AI intelligence. Every AI system in existence today is Narrow AI. General AI remains a research goal. Superintelligence is theoretical. Understanding this spectrum is essential for realistic thinking about AI's role in quantitative finance.

Prerequisites ✓ AI Defined ✓ History ✓ AI Winters ✓ AI Types

01Narrow AI (ANI) — What We Have Today

Artificial Narrow Intelligence (ANI), also called Weak AI, is AI that excels at one specific task — and only that task. It cannot generalize to other domains. Every AI system deployed commercially today is ANI.

🔭 Analogy: The World-Class Specialist Imagine Magnus Carlsen — the best chess player in history. He is extraordinarily intelligent within chess, but that skill doesn't transfer: he can't use chess strategy to write a novel, perform surgery, or design a bridge. Narrow AI is the same: superhuman performance in one domain, zero capability elsewhere.
♟️
Game AI
AlphaGo, AlphaZero, Stockfish. Beat every human at their specific game. Cannot play any other game it wasn't trained for.
🖼️
Image Recognition
Google Photos identifies faces and objects with superhuman accuracy. Cannot understand what's happening in the image at a semantic level.
💬
Language Models
GPT-4, Claude, Gemini — excellent at language tasks. Impressive breadth, but fundamentally pattern-matching on training data, not true reasoning.
📊
Quant Finance AI
LSTM models for return prediction, NLP for earnings calls, RL for trade execution — all Narrow AI. Excellent at specific financial tasks.

02Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) — The Research Goal

Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) refers to a machine that can understand, learn, and apply intelligence across any intellectual task — just like a human. It can transfer knowledge from one domain to another. It can learn from minimal data. It has common sense.

AGI does not yet exist. It is the central long-term goal of AI research. When it will be achieved — or whether it's even possible — is genuinely debated:

03Superintelligence (ASI) — The Theoretical Extreme

Artificial Superintelligence (ASI) refers to an AI that surpasses the best human capabilities across every domain — science, creativity, social intelligence, strategy, everything. Nick Bostrom defined it as "any intellect that greatly exceeds the cognitive performance of humans in virtually all domains of interest."

📊 The Intelligence Spectrum

Why Does ASI Matter for Quant Finance?

💹 The Market Efficiency Paradox Here's a fascinating quant puzzle: If ASI existed and was deployed in markets, it would discover and trade away every possible alpha in milliseconds. Markets would become perfectly efficient. There would be no alpha left to extract. This means the existence of tradeable alpha is itself evidence that ASI doesn't exist yet. The presence of market inefficiencies is a lower bound on the gap between current AI and ASI.

# ================================================
# EXPERIMENT 4: Narrow AI — Transfer Learning Gap
# ================================================
# This shows WHY current AI is "narrow" — it can't
# easily transfer knowledge across very different domains
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Simulate: A model trained to predict stock patterns
# We'll use synthetic data to show the concept clearly

np.random.seed(42)

def generate_stock_data(n=500, regime='bull'):
    """Simulate stock-like time series"""
    if regime == 'bull':
        trend = np.linspace(0, 0.5, n)
        noise = np.random.normal(0, 0.02, n)
    else:  # bear
        trend = np.linspace(0, -0.5, n)
        noise = np.random.normal(0, 0.03, n)
    returns = trend/n + noise
    return returns

def generate_crypto_data(n=500):
    """Simulate crypto-like time series (very different behavior)"""
    # Crypto: much higher volatility + fat tails
    returns = np.random.standard_t(df=3, size=n) * 0.05  # Fat-tailed distribution
    # Add occasional crashes and moonshots
    crash_idx = np.random.choice(n, 5)
    moon_idx = np.random.choice(n, 5)
    returns[crash_idx] -= 0.15  # Flash crashes
    returns[moon_idx] += 0.20   # Moonshots
    return returns

# Generate data
stock_bull = generate_stock_data(500, 'bull')
stock_bear = generate_stock_data(500, 'bear')
crypto = generate_crypto_data(500)

# Simple "model": momentum signal
def momentum_signal(returns, window=5):
    """Rolling momentum indicator"""
    signals = []
    for i in range(window, len(returns)):
        past_return = returns[i-window:i].sum()
        signal = 1 if past_return > 0 else 0  # Buy if recent trend is up
        signals.append(signal)
    return np.array(signals)

def backtest(returns, signals):
    """Simple backtest: hold when signal=1, cash when signal=0"""
    strategy_returns = returns[5:] * signals
    return strategy_returns

# Train signal on stock data, test on crypto
stock_signals = momentum_signal(stock_bull)
stock_pnl = backtest(stock_bull, stock_signals)

# Apply SAME signal (trained on stocks) to crypto — this is the "narrow AI" problem
crypto_signals = momentum_signal(crypto)
crypto_pnl_native = backtest(crypto, crypto_signals)  # Signal retrained on crypto
crypto_pnl_transfer = backtest(crypto, stock_signals[:len(crypto)-5])  # Stock signal on crypto

# Results
print("=" * 55)
print("📊 NARROW AI: Domain Transfer Experiment")
print("=" * 55)
print(f"\n📈 Stock signal on STOCK data:")
print(f"   Sharpe: {stock_pnl.mean()/stock_pnl.std()*np.sqrt(252):.2f}")
print(f"   Total return: {stock_pnl.sum():.2%}")
print(f"\n₿  Stock signal on CRYPTO (transfer — narrow AI fails):")
print(f"   Sharpe: {(backtest(crypto, stock_signals[:len(crypto)-5])).mean()/(backtest(crypto, stock_signals[:len(crypto)-5])).std()*np.sqrt(252):.2f}")
print(f"   Total return: {(backtest(crypto, stock_signals[:len(crypto)-5])).sum():.2%}")
print(f"\n₿  Native crypto signal (in-domain):")
print(f"   Sharpe: {crypto_pnl_native.mean()/crypto_pnl_native.std()*np.sqrt(252):.2f}")
print(f"   Total return: {crypto_pnl_native.sum():.2%}")
print(f"\n💡 KEY INSIGHT: The stock-trained signal degrades on crypto.")
print(f"   This is NARROW AI in action — domain-specific, not general.")
print(f"   AGI would transfer the pattern-finding skill, not the specific patterns.")

# Visualize
fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 1, figsize=(12, 8))
fig.patch.set_facecolor('#08090d')
fig.suptitle('Narrow AI: Why Models Fail Across Domains', 
             color='#e8eaf0', fontsize=14, fontweight='bold', y=0.98)

for ax in [ax1, ax2]:

# Cumulative returns
ax2.plot(np.cumsum(stock_pnl), color='#00e5c0', linewidth=2, label='Stock signal → Stock (in-domain)')
ax2.plot(np.cumsum(backtest(crypto, stock_signals[:len(crypto)-5])), color='#ff6b6b', linewidth=2, label='Stock signal → Crypto (transfer)')
