Prerequisites

Before we touch any pricing formula, you need fluency in these 8 foundational ideas. Don't skip — every single topic in this guide builds on them.

Math Finance Basics Probability

1. What is a Financial Market?

A financial market is simply a place (physical or electronic) where buyers and sellers exchange things of value — assets. Think of it like eBay, but for stocks, bonds, currencies, and contracts.

Spot Market

You buy or sell something right now at today's price. Example: buying Apple stock at $185 today. The transaction settles within 1-2 days.

Derivatives Market

You trade a contract whose value depends on another asset. The actual asset might not change hands at all. Example: an option to buy Apple at $200 in 3 months.

2. Time Value of Money (TVM)

Core Idea

$100 today is worth more than $100 a year from now. Why? Because you can invest $100 today and have more than $100 in a year. This single concept underpins ALL of finance.

Future Value = PV × er·T
Continuous compounding · PV = Present Value · r = risk-free rate · T = time in years

Present Value (PV)

How much is a future cash flow worth today? Discount it back: PV = FV × e^(-rT). If r=5%, T=1, then $100 in 1 year = $95.12 today.

Risk-Free Rate (r)

The return you earn with zero risk. In practice, this is the government bond yield (e.g., US Treasury). In Black-Scholes, we use this to discount future payoffs.

4. Logarithms & Exponentials

Black-Scholes uses natural logarithm (ln) and Euler's number (e ≈ 2.718). Here's all you need:

ln(x)

The power you raise e to get x. ln(e) = 1. ln(1) = 0. ln(100) ≈ 4.6

e^x (exp)

Continuous growth. e^0=1, e^1≈2.718. Used for continuous compounding of interest.

Log Returns

ln(S₁/S₀) = log return. These are additive and normally distributed — key assumption in BS.

5. Calculus Intuition (Don't Fear It)

Quant Tip

You don't need to derive anything from scratch. You need to understand what derivatives (∂) mean: the rate of change. Greeks are literally just partial derivatives of the option price.

Derivative (∂f/∂x)

How much does f change when x changes by a tiny amount? Delta (Δ) in options = ∂Price/∂StockPrice. If stock goes up $1, how much does my option move?

Partial Derivative

Same idea, but we change ONE variable while keeping others fixed. ∂V/∂S means: change in option value (V) when stock price (S) moves, holding everything else constant.

6. Volatility (σ)

Most Important Parameter in Options

Volatility is the standard deviation of annualized log returns. It measures how wildly a stock's price moves. A stock with σ=20% is calm. σ=80% is wild (think crypto). Options on high-volatility assets cost MORE because there's more chance of a big payout.

7. Stochastic Processes & Random Walks

Stock prices don't move in a straight line — they random walk. Each day's move is unpredictable, like flipping a coin. The mathematical model is called Brownian Motion.

dS = μS dt + σS dWt
Geometric Brownian Motion — The model behind Black-Scholes · dW = random Wiener process increment

Breaking It Down

dS = tiny change in stock price in a tiny time interval dt

μS dt = the "drift" — on average, stocks trend upward (this is like expected return). This is the predictable part.

σS dW = the "diffusion" — random shocks to price. dW ~ N(0, dt). This is the unpredictable part.

8. Itô's Lemma (Just Know What It Does)

What You Need to Know

If a stock follows GBM, then the function of the stock price (like an option's value V) follows a specific rule for its own dynamics. Itô's Lemma gives us the formula for dV given dS. The key result: it adds an extra term ½σ²S²(∂²V/∂S²) because randomness has a "second-order" effect. You'll see this as Gamma (Γ) later.

Intro to Derivatives

A derivative is a contract whose value is derived from something else. Master this first — every pricing model is just a tool to answer one question: "What is this contract worth today?"

Options Forwards Payoff Diagrams No-Arbitrage

What is a Derivative?

One-Line Definition

A derivative is a contract between two parties whose value depends on the price of an underlying asset (stock, bond, commodity, index, currency, etc.).

🌽 The Insurance Analogy

A put option is like car insurance. You pay a premium upfront. If something bad happens (price falls / car crash), you're protected. If nothing happens, you lose only the premium.

🏠 The Real Estate Analogy

A call option is like a deposit on a house. You pay $5K to lock in the right to buy at $400K in 6 months. If the price rises to $450K, you exercise and profit $45K. If it falls, walk away (only lose $5K).

📦 Forward Contract Analogy

Agree today to buy wheat in 3 months at a fixed price. The farmer is protected if wheat price falls. The baker is protected if wheat price rises. Both sides lock in certainty.

Options Deep Dive

Call Option

Payoff at expiry: max(S - K, 0)

You have the RIGHT to buy the asset at strike price K. You exercise only if S > K (in-the-money). Otherwise you let it expire worthless. Profit from rising prices.

Example: K=$100, S=$120 → Payoff = $20. K=$100, S=$80 → Payoff = $0.

Put Option

Payoff at expiry: max(K - S, 0)

You have the RIGHT to sell the asset at strike price K. You exercise only if S < K (in-the-money). Profit from falling prices. Acts like insurance.

Example: K=$100, S=$80 → Payoff = $20. K=$100, S=$120 → Payoff = $0.

No-Arbitrage Pricing

The Most Important Principle in Finance

No free lunch. If you can construct two portfolios that have the exact same future payoff in ALL possible scenarios, they MUST have the same price today. If they don't, arbitrageurs will exploit the difference until prices converge.

Put-Call Parity

The most famous no-arbitrage relationship: a call + present value of strike = put + stock.

C + K·e-rT = P + S

This must hold or there's an arbitrage opportunity. If you know C, you can always find P and vice versa.

Black-Scholes Model

Fischer Black, Myron Scholes, and Robert Merton cracked the options pricing problem in 1973. The formula won a Nobel Prize. Here's everything — the assumptions, the formula, and what it really means.

Assumptions BS Formula Implied Volatility Risk-Neutral Pricing

The 8 Assumptions of Black-Scholes

Critical to Understand

The Black-Scholes formula is exact ONLY if all these assumptions hold. In reality, NONE of them hold perfectly. Understanding each assumption — and how reality violates it — is what separates a real quant from someone who just plugs numbers into a formula.

A1
Geometric Brownian Motion
Stock prices follow GBM with constant μ and σ. Log returns are normally distributed. Reality: fat tails, jumps (crashes) exist.
A2
Constant Volatility
σ is fixed over the option's life. Reality: volatility changes daily — "volatility of volatility" exists. This leads to the vol smile.
A3
No Dividends
The underlying pays no dividends during the option's life. Extensions exist for dividend-paying stocks (Merton model).
A4
Constant Risk-Free Rate
r is fixed and known. Reality: interest rates move. For short-dated options, this is a minor issue.
A5
European Exercise Only
Option can only be exercised at expiry T. Black-Scholes doesn't price American options exactly. Binomial trees are needed.
A6
No Transaction Costs
No bid-ask spread, no commissions. Reality: all real markets have frictions. Continuous hedging would be infinitely expensive.
A7
Continuous Trading
You can trade at any moment. In practice, markets close. Jumps happen overnight. The hedge rebalancing is discrete.
A8
No Arbitrage
Markets are efficient — mispricings are exploited away immediately. This allows risk-neutral pricing to work.

Risk-Neutral Pricing — The Magic Insight

The Core Trick

Under the risk-neutral measure, ALL assets grow at the risk-free rate r (not their real expected return μ). This seems wrong — why would a risky stock grow like a treasury bill? Because risk has been "priced in" to the option premium. We compute the expected payoff assuming the stock grows at r, then discount back at r. The real-world drift μ completely cancels out!

V(S,t) = e-r(T-t) · EQ[max(ST - K, 0)]
Option price = discounted expected payoff under risk-neutral measure Q

The Black-Scholes Formula

C = S·N(d₁) - K·e-rT·N(d₂)
P = K·e-rT·N(-d₂) - S·N(-d₁)
Call (C) and Put (P) prices for European options
d₁ = [ln(S/K) + (r + σ²/2)·T] / (σ·√T)
d₂ = d₁ - σ·√T
Where N(·) = cumulative standard normal CDF

Decoding d₁ and d₂

What is N(d₂)?

N(d₂) = the risk-neutral probability that the option expires in-the-money (S_T > K). It's the probability you "win" under the risk-neutral measure.

What is N(d₁)?

N(d₁) = the Delta of the call option. It tells you how many shares of stock you need to hold to perfectly hedge the call. Also called the option's "hedge ratio."

Intuition for the Call Formula: C = S·N(d₁) - K·e⁻ʳᵀ·N(d₂)

S·N(d₁): Expected value of receiving the stock, conditional on the option expiring ITM, discounted to today. N(d₁) is the probability-weighted share.

K·e⁻ʳᵀ·N(d₂): Expected value of PAYING the strike K, conditional on expiry ITM, discounted to today.

The call is worth: "what I receive (stock)" minus "what I pay (strike)" — weighted by probability of exercise.

Implied Volatility (IV)

Reverse Engineering Black-Scholes

In practice, we OBSERVE market option prices and SOLVE for the volatility that would produce that price under Black-Scholes. This is Implied Volatility (IV). It's the market's consensus forecast of future volatility. If IV > historical vol, options are "expensive." If IV < historical vol, options are "cheap."

The Volatility Smile / Skew

If Black-Scholes were perfect, IV would be constant across all strikes. In reality, if you plot IV vs. Strike, you get a smile (higher IV for deep ITM and OTM options) or skew (higher IV for lower strikes in equity markets). This tells us the market doesn't believe in the lognormal assumption — it prices in tail risk.

The Greeks

Greeks measure how sensitive an option's price is to various inputs. They're the language of every options trading desk. Master them and you can understand — and manage — any options position.

Delta Δ Gamma Γ Vega ν Theta Θ Rho ρ

Why Greeks Matter

The Trading Desk View

Imagine you're running a book of thousands of options. You can't track every individual position. Instead, you aggregate your exposure to each risk factor using Greeks. "I'm long 500 Delta, short 200 Gamma, long 1M Vega." This tells you instantly: what market moves hurt you, what help you, and how to hedge.

Delta (Δ)

Δ
Delta — The Most Important Greek
Δ = ∂V/∂S = N(d₁) for calls, N(d₁)-1 for puts
What it means: If stock price rises by $1, the option price changes by Δ dollars. Call Δ ∈ [0,1]. Put Δ ∈ [-1,0].
💡 Deep ITM call: Δ≈1 (moves like stock). ATM call: Δ≈0.5. Deep OTM call: Δ≈0. Delta is also approximately the probability of expiring ITM (under risk-neutral measure).
Hedge ratio Prob ITM (approx) 0 to 1 for calls

Delta Hedging — The Self-Financing Portfolio

If you sell a call option, you've taken on unlimited risk (if stock soars). To hedge, you buy Δ shares of the stock. If Δ=0.6, buy 0.6 shares per call sold.

Now if stock goes up $1: Call price rises by $0.60 (loss on short call = -$0.60). Stock position gains: 0.6 × $1 = +$0.60. Net P&L ≈ 0. You're delta-neutral.

But Δ changes as S moves! So you must continuously rebalance. This is dynamic delta hedging — the core of the BS model.

Gamma (Γ)

Γ
Gamma — Rate of Change of Delta
Γ = ∂²V/∂S² = ∂Δ/∂S = N'(d₁) / (S·σ·√T)
What it means: Gamma tells you HOW FAST Delta changes as the stock moves. High Gamma = Delta is very sensitive to price moves. Gamma is always positive for long options (both calls and puts).
💡 ATM options have the highest Gamma (near expiry, Δ jumps from 0.5 to 0 or 1 very quickly). Long Gamma = you profit from BIG moves. Short Gamma = you suffer from big moves. Gamma is the "convexity" of options.
Convexity Max at ATM Always positive (long options)

Long Gamma (Bought Options)

You own options. Big moves in either direction make you money. Your Delta automatically adjusts in your favor — as stock rises, your call Δ increases (you own more delta). As stock falls, delta decreases. You're always positioned correctly after a move.

Short Gamma (Sold Options)

You sold options. Volatile days hurt you — you must constantly re-hedge at unfavorable prices. This is the cost of collecting premium. You collect time value (Theta) but pay it back through adverse rebalancing.

Theta (Θ)

Θ
Theta — Time Decay
Θ = ∂V/∂t (usually negative for option buyers)
What it means: How much does the option price drop each day as time passes, all else equal? Typically expressed as $ per day. An option with Θ=-$0.05 loses $0.05 in value each day due to time passing.
💡 Time decay accelerates near expiry. An ATM option with 1 month left decays faster than one with 6 months left. The Gamma-Theta relationship: high Gamma comes with high Theta (you pay for convexity through time decay). Options sellers collect Theta; buyers pay it.
Time decay Neg for buyers Accelerates near expiry

Vega (ν)

ν
Vega — Volatility Sensitivity
ν = ∂V/∂σ = S·√T·N'(d₁)
What it means: If implied volatility rises by 1%, the option price changes by ν dollars. Always positive for both calls and puts (higher vol = higher option prices).
💡 Long volatility (long Vega): you profit if vol rises. This is the "volatility bet" — you think the market will be more volatile than priced. Short Vega: you sell options, profiting if vol stays low or falls. Vega is largest for long-dated ATM options.
Vol sensitivity Always positive Max for ATM long-dated

Rho (ρ)

ρ
Rho — Interest Rate Sensitivity
ρ = ∂V/∂r = K·T·e^(-rT)·N(d₂) for calls
What it means: If the risk-free rate rises by 1%, call price changes by ρ. Positive for calls (higher r = lower PV of strike = call cheaper to "pay for" stock). Negative for puts.
💡 Rho matters most for long-dated options or when interest rates are moving significantly. For short-term equity options, Rho is often the least important Greek to monitor.

The Gamma-Theta Relationship

Θ + ½σ²S²Γ + rSΔ - rV = 0
The Black-Scholes PDE — The fundamental relationship between all Greeks
Deep Insight

The BS PDE says: Theta (time decay) + Gamma (convexity benefit) = r × V (financing cost). Long options gain Gamma (convexity) but lose Theta (time value). They're always in balance. This is why options pricing is really about balancing these forces.

Binomial Trees

Before Black-Scholes, there was the binomial tree. It's simpler, more intuitive, and more flexible — it can price American options that BS cannot. And remarkably, as you add more steps, it converges to exactly the BS price.

Cox-Ross-Rubinstein Risk-Neutral Pricing American Options Backward Induction

The Core Idea

How Binomial Trees Work

At each time step, the stock price can only do TWO things: go UP by factor u, or go DOWN by factor d. This creates a tree of possible future prices. We then price the option by working backward from expiry to today, computing the option value at each node using risk-neutral probabilities.

The Cox-Ross-Rubinstein (CRR) Model

Cox, Ross, and Rubinstein (1979) derived specific formulas for u, d, and p that make the binomial model consistent with GBM:

u = eσ√Δt     d = e-σ√Δt = 1/u
p = (erΔt - d) / (u - d)
CRR parameters · Δt = T/N = length of each time step · p = risk-neutral probability of up move

Why e^(σ√Δt)?

This choice of u and d makes the binomial tree's variance per step equal σ²Δt — exactly matching GBM's variance. As N→∞ (more steps), the binomial distribution converges to normal distribution, and the tree converges to the BS formula.

Risk-Neutral Probability p

p is chosen so that the expected return of the stock (under the tree) equals the risk-free rate r. This is the same risk-neutral pricing trick as BS. Real-world probability of up move is irrelevant!

Backward Induction — Step by Step

Algorithm

Step 1: Build the stock price tree forward: at each node (i,j), S = S₀ × u^j × d^(i-j) where i=step, j=number of up moves.

Step 2: At the final nodes (expiry), compute payoff: max(S-K,0) for calls.

Step 3: Work BACKWARD: at each node, V = e^(-rΔt) × [p × V_up + (1-p) × V_down].

Step 4 (American options): At each node, compare backward-induced value vs. intrinsic value. V = max(intrinsic, continuation value). If intrinsic is higher → optimal to exercise early.

American vs European Options in Binomial Trees

European Option

At each node, value = discounted expected future value. No early exercise check needed. V(node) = e^(-rΔt)[p·V_up + (1-p)·V_down]

American Option

At each node: V(node) = max(intrinsic value, continuation value). Intrinsic = max(S-K,0) for call. If intrinsic > continuation → exercise now is optimal.

Early Exercise Insight

American call on non-dividend paying stock: NEVER optimal to exercise early (because the time value of money means it's always better to hold). American put: CAN be optimal to exercise early, especially deep ITM, because getting cash now and reinvesting at r is valuable.

Monte Carlo Pricing

When no closed-form formula exists and trees are too slow, Monte Carlo simulation saves the day. Simulate thousands of possible future paths, average the payoffs, discount back. Brute force — but elegantly powerful.

Path Simulation Law of Large Numbers Variance Reduction Exotic Options

The Core Idea

Monte Carlo Principle

The option price is an expected value: V = e^(-rT) × E^Q[Payoff(S_T)]. We can estimate any expected value by: (1) generate many random samples from the distribution, (2) compute payoff for each, (3) average them, (4) discount. By the Law of Large Numbers, this average converges to the true expected value as N→∞.

🎲
Generate N random paths
💰
Compute payoff per path
📊
Average payoffs
⏪
Discount to today

Simulating GBM Paths

Under the risk-neutral measure Q, the stock follows:

S(t + Δt) = S(t) × exp[(r - σ²/2)Δt + σ√Δt × Z]
GBM discretization · Z ~ N(0,1) i.i.d. at each step

Why (r - σ²/2) and not r?

This is the Itô correction term. Because we're dealing with a log-normal process, the log-mean of S(T) is (r - σ²/2)T, not rT. Without the correction, we'd overestimate E[S(T)]. With it, E[S(T)] = S₀·e^(rT) exactly as required.

# Monte Carlo option pricer — Clean Python implementation
import numpy as np

def monte_carlo_european(S, K, T, r, sigma, n_paths=100_000, option_type='call'):
    """
    Price European option using Monte Carlo simulation.
    
    Args:
        S: Stock price today
        K: Strike price
        T: Time to maturity (years)
        r: Risk-free rate (decimal)
        sigma: Volatility (decimal)
        n_paths: Number of simulated paths
        option_type: 'call' or 'put'
    
    Returns:
        price: Option price estimate
        std_error: Standard error of estimate
    """
    # Generate n_paths standard normal samples
    Z = np.random.standard_normal(n_paths)
    
    # Simulate terminal stock price under risk-neutral measure
    S_T = S * np.exp((r - 0.5 * sigma**2) * T + sigma * np.sqrt(T) * Z)
    
    # Compute payoffs
    if option_type == 'call':
        payoffs = np.maximum(S_T - K, 0)
    else:
        payoffs = np.maximum(K - S_T, 0)
    
    # Discount expected payoff to today
    price = np.exp(-r * T) * np.mean(payoffs)
    std_error = np.exp(-r * T) * np.std(payoffs) / np.sqrt(n_paths)
    
    return price, std_error

# Example usage
price, se = monte_carlo_european(100, 100, 1.0, 0.05, 0.20)
print(f"Call price: ${price:.4f} ± ${se:.4f}")

Accuracy and Convergence

Monte Carlo Error

Standard Error = σ_payoff / √N. To halve the error, you need 4× more paths. This is called the √N convergence. For 1 decimal place accuracy, you typically need ~10,000 paths. For 2 decimal places, ~1,000,000.

Variance Reduction Techniques

Since MC is slow (√N convergence), we use tricks to get better accuracy with fewer paths:

Antithetic Variates

For every random number Z, also use -Z. These two paths are negatively correlated, reducing variance. Implementation: generate N/2 normal samples Z, also price using -Z, average all N payoffs. Gets you ~2× variance reduction for free.

Z = np.random.standard_normal(n//2)
Z_all = np.concatenate([Z, -Z])
S_T = S * np.exp((r - 0.5*sig**2)*T + sig*np.sqrt(T)*Z_all)

Control Variates

Use a known analytical result to correct MC estimates. For a call, the geometric average option has an exact formula. Estimate the correction needed for arithmetic average using the difference. Often achieves 10-100× variance reduction.

Quasi-Random (Low-Discrepancy Sequences)

Instead of pseudo-random numbers, use Sobol or Halton sequences which fill space more uniformly. Can achieve O(1/N) convergence instead of O(1/√N) for well-behaved integrals.

Importance Sampling

Sample more heavily from the region that matters most (near the strike for OTM options). Dramatically reduces noise for out-of-the-money options which rarely get exercised.

Path-Dependent Options — Where MC Shines

Monte Carlo's Superpower

Black-Scholes gives a formula only for European options. Binomial trees struggle with options that depend on the full price path. Monte Carlo handles ANY payoff structure by just simulating the full path and computing whatever payoff function you define.

Asian Option

Payoff depends on average price over the life. max(S_avg - K, 0). Common in commodity markets. No closed form → Monte Carlo needed.

Barrier Option

Option activates or deactivates when price hits a barrier. Knock-out call: if S > H at any point, option dies. Must simulate daily.

Lookback Option

Payoff depends on the minimum or maximum price over the period. max(S_max - K, 0). Requires tracking S_max along each path.

# Asian Option — Monte Carlo with full path simulation
def asian_call_mc(S, K, T, r, sigma, n_paths=50_000, n_steps=252):
    dt = T / n_steps
    payoffs = np.zeros(n_paths)
    
    for i in range(n_paths):
        path = [S]
        for _ in range(n_steps):
            Z = np.random.standard_normal()
            S_next = path[-1] * np.exp((r - 0.5*sigma**2)*dt + sigma*np.sqrt(dt)*Z)
            path.append(S_next)
        
        avg_price = np.mean(path)
        payoffs[i] = max(avg_price - K, 0)
    
    return np.exp(-r * T) * np.mean(payoffs)

# Vectorized version (much faster — always vectorize!)
def asian_call_vectorized(S, K, T, r, sigma, n_paths=50_000, n_steps=252):
    dt = T / n_steps
    Z = np.random.standard_normal((n_paths, n_steps))
    increments = (r - 0.5*sigma**2)*dt + sigma*np.sqrt(dt)*Z
    log_prices = np.log(S) + np.cumsum(increments, axis=1)
    prices = np.exp(log_prices)
    avg_prices = np.mean(prices, axis=1)
    payoffs = np.maximum(avg_prices - K, 0)
    return np.exp(-r * T) * np.mean(payoffs)

How do all these pieces fit together? See the full map of derivatives pricing — how every concept connects, when to use each model, and what your journey as a quant looks like from here.

The Unified Map

When to Use Which Model?

SituationBest ToolWhy

American option (early exercise)Binomial TreeCan check early exercise at every node

How Everything Connects

GBM → Black-Scholes → Greeks

GBM is the assumption about how stock prices move. BS solves the resulting PDE to get an exact formula. Greeks are the partial derivatives of that formula — they tell you how the price changes with each input. This chain is one coherent mathematical framework.

Binomial Tree → BS in the limit

As N→∞, the binomial tree's probability distribution converges to log-normal. The tree price converges to the BS price. This shows that BS and the binomial model are the same model — just continuous vs discrete versions.

Monte Carlo → BS verification

You can derive the BS formula from MC: simulate millions of GBM paths, compute discounted average payoff. This equals the BS price. MC and BS are just two ways to evaluate the same risk-neutral expectation.

Implied Volatility → Reality check

Real market prices are put back into the BS formula to extract implied vol. The resulting "vol surface" (IV vs Strike vs Maturity) reveals everything BS gets wrong — fat tails, vol of vol, skew — and guides construction of better models.

Experiment 2 — Black-Scholes From Scratch

# EXPERIMENT 2: Complete Black-Scholes Implementation
import numpy as np
from scipy.stats import norm
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def bs_price(S, K, T, r, sigma, option='call'):
    if T <= 0: return max(S-K, 0) if option=='call' else max(K-S, 0)
    d1 = (np.log(S/K) + (r + 0.5*sigma**2)*T) / (sigma*np.sqrt(T))
    d2 = d1 - sigma*np.sqrt(T)
    if option == 'call':
        return S*norm.cdf(d1) - K*np.exp(-r*T)*norm.cdf(d2)
    else:
        return K*np.exp(-r*T)*norm.cdf(-d2) - S*norm.cdf(-d1)

def bs_greeks(S, K, T, r, sigma):
    d1 = (np.log(S/K) + (r + 0.5*sigma**2)*T) / (sigma*np.sqrt(T))
    d2 = d1 - sigma*np.sqrt(T)
    call_delta = norm.cdf(d1)
    put_delta = call_delta - 1
    gamma = norm.pdf(d1) / (S * sigma * np.sqrt(T))
    vega = S * np.sqrt(T) * norm.pdf(d1) / 100  # per 1% vol change
    call_theta = (-(S*norm.pdf(d1)*sigma)/(2*np.sqrt(T)) - r*K*np.exp(-r*T)*norm.cdf(d2)) / 365
    call_rho = K*T*np.exp(-r*T)*norm.cdf(d2) / 100
    return {'delta_call':call_delta, 'delta_put':put_delta,
            'gamma':gamma, 'vega':vega, 'theta_call':call_theta, 'rho':call_rho}

# Plot: Call price as function of stock price
S_range = np.linspace(60, 140, 200)
K, T, r, sigma = 100, 1.0, 0.05, 0.20

call_prices = [bs_price(s, K, T, r, sigma, 'call') for s in S_range]
put_prices  = [bs_price(s, K, T, r, sigma, 'put')  for s in S_range]
intrinsic_c = np.maximum(S_range - K, 0)

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 5))
plt.plot(S_range, call_prices, label='Call Price', color='#00d4ff', lw=2)
plt.plot(S_range, put_prices, label='Put Price', color='#ef4444', lw=2)
plt.plot(S_range, intrinsic_c, label='Call Intrinsic', color='#10b981', lw=1, ls='--')
plt.axvline(K, color='gray', ls=':'); plt.xlabel('Stock Price'); plt.ylabel('Price')
plt.title('Black-Scholes Option Prices'); plt.legend(); plt.grid(alpha=0.3); plt.show()
print(f"ATM Call: ${bs_price(100,K,T,r,sigma,'call'):.4f}")
print(f"Greeks: {bs_greeks(100,K,T,r,sigma)}")

        hedge = bs(S_new,K,T-step*dt,r,sigma_bs); S = S_new

# EXPERIMENT 4: Binomial Tree → Black-Scholes Convergence
import numpy as np; from scipy.stats import norm; import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def binomial_european(S, K, T, r, sigma, N, option='call'):
    dt=T/N; u=np.exp(sigma*np.sqrt(dt)); d=1/u
    p=(np.exp(r*dt)-d)/(u-d); disc=np.exp(-r*dt)
    prices = np.array([S*u**j*d**(N-j) for j in range(N+1)])
    V = np.maximum(prices-K,0) if option=='call' else np.maximum(K-prices,0)
    for _ in range(N): V = disc*(p*V[1:] + (1-p)*V[:-1])
    return V[0]

S,K,T,r,sigma = 100,100,1.0,0.05,0.20
d1=(np.log(S/K)+(r+0.5*sigma**2)*T)/(sigma*np.sqrt(T)); d2=d1-sigma*np.sqrt(T)
bs_call = S*norm.cdf(d1)-K*np.exp(-r*T)*norm.cdf(d2)

steps = [1,2,5,10,25,50,100,200,500]
prices = [binomial_european(S,K,T,r,sigma,N) for N in steps]

plt.figure(figsize=(10,4))
plt.semilogx(steps, prices, 'o-', color='#f59e0b', lw=2, label='Binomial')
plt.axhline(bs_call, color='#00d4ff', ls='--', lw=2, label=f'BS = {bs_call:.4f}')
plt.xlabel('Number of Steps (log scale)'); plt.ylabel('Call Price')
plt.title('Binomial Tree Convergence to Black-Scholes'); plt.legend(); plt.grid(alpha=0.3); plt.show()

# EXPERIMENT 5: Monte Carlo accuracy with variance reduction
import numpy as np; from scipy.stats import norm; import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

S,K,T,r,sigma = 100,100,1.0,0.05,0.20
d1=(np.log(S/K)+(r+0.5*sigma**2)*T)/(sigma*np.sqrt(T)); d2=d1-sigma*np.sqrt(T)
bs_exact = S*norm.cdf(d1)-K*np.exp(-r*T)*norm.cdf(d2)

sizes = [100,500,1000,5000,10000,50000,100000]
mc_errors, anti_errors = [], []

for n in sizes:
    Z = np.random.standard_normal(n)
    ST = S*np.exp((r-0.5*sigma**2)*T + sigma*np.sqrt(T)*Z)
    mc_errors.append(abs(np.exp(-r*T)*np.mean(np.maximum(ST-K,0))-bs_exact))

plt.loglog(sizes, anti_errors, 's-', label='Antithetic', color='#10b981')
