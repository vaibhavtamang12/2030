P1 · Mean, Variance & Standard Deviation

🎯 Simple Analogy

Imagine you shoot arrows at a target 10 times. The mean is where your arrows landed on average. The variance is how spread out the arrows were. The standard deviation (the square root of variance) puts that spread in the same units as your original measurement.

📊
Mean (μ)

Sum all values, divide by count. It's the "center of gravity" of your data. If returns were [1%, 3%, -2%, 4%], mean = 6%/4 = 1.5%

📐
Variance (σ²)

Average of squared differences from the mean. Squaring ensures negatives don't cancel positives. Higher variance = more spread out data.

📏
Std Dev (σ)

Square root of variance. Same units as data. A stock with σ=2% daily has "typical" daily moves of about ±2%.

Formulas
Mean: μ = (1/N) · Σxᵢ
Variance: σ² = (1/N) · Σ(xᵢ − μ)²
Std Dev: σ = √σ²
N = number of data points  |  xᵢ = each data point  |  μ = mean

P2 · Financial Returns

🎯 Simple Analogy

You bought a stock at $100 and it's now $103. Your return is 3%. We always talk in percentages because it lets us compare a $10 stock and a $1000 stock fairly.

Simple Return vs Log Return
Simple Return: r = (P_t − P_{t−1}) / P_{t−1}
Log Return: r = ln(P_t / P_{t−1})
P_t = price today  |  P_{t−1} = price yesterday
Log returns are preferred in quant finance because they add up correctly across time periods

P3 · Machine Learning Basics: Train/Test Split

🎯 Simple Analogy

When studying for an exam, you practice on old exam papers (training). The actual exam (test) has new questions you've never seen. If you just memorized last year's answers, you'll fail the new exam — that's overfitting.

Train / Validation / Test Split
⚠️
Critical for Finance: Walk-Forward Testing

In finance, you can't randomly split data — you must always train on the past and test on the future. Randomly splitting would "leak" future information into your training, making results look unrealistically good.

P4 · Correlation and Rank Correlation (Spearman)

🎯 Simple Analogy

Correlation measures if two things move together. Do stocks go up when the market goes up? Does studying more lead to better grades? Pearson correlation measures linear relationships. Spearman correlation measures if the rankings agree — this is more robust to outliers and that's what IC (Information Coefficient) is based on.

Pearson vs Spearman
Pearson: ρ = Σ[(xᵢ−x̄)(yᵢ−ȳ)] / (N·σₓ·σᵧ)
Spearman: ρₛ = Pearson(rank(x), rank(y))
Both range from −1 (perfect inverse) to +1 (perfect positive) · 0 = no relationship
Spearman replaces raw values with their ranks, making it immune to outliers

P5 · Normal Distribution & Z-Scores

Normal Distribution — The Bell Curve
Z-Score Formula
Z = (x − μ) / σ
Z-score tells you how many standard deviations a value is from the mean
Z = 2.0 means "this value is 2 standard deviations above average" → top ~2.3% of values
Used in Sharpe ratio (a type of Z-score for strategy performance)

Topic 1 · Overfitting Detection

Overfitting is the #1 enemy of quant strategies. A model that "overfits" performs brilliantly on historical data but falls apart in real trading. Learning to detect it is the most critical skill in model validation.

Most common failure mode

What Is Overfitting?

🎯 Simple Analogy

Imagine you're trying to predict tomorrow's weather. You study the last 30 days and notice: "Every time my neighbor washed their car AND it was a Tuesday, it rained the next day." This is overfitting — you found a spurious pattern specific to your data that won't hold in the future. The model memorized noise as if it were signal.

In quantitative finance, overfitting happens when you build a trading strategy that was tuned so precisely to historical data that it captured random fluctuations (noise) rather than genuine market relationships (signal). It looks amazing on paper but fails live.

❌ Overfitted Strategy

Backtest Sharpe: 4.2
Live Sharpe: 0.3

Too many parameters, perfectly fitted to past noise. Every wiggle in historical data is "explained" by a rule.

✅ Well-Fitted Strategy

Backtest Sharpe: 1.8
Live Sharpe: 1.5

Modest backtest but captures a genuine economic relationship. Performs consistently out-of-sample.

The Bias-Variance Tradeoff

Every model error can be decomposed into two sources: Bias (systematic errors from wrong assumptions) and Variance (sensitivity to small fluctuations in training data). Overfitting = high variance.

Bias-Variance Tradeoff — Model Complexity vs Error
🎯
High Bias (Underfitting)

Model is too simple. Misses obvious patterns. Like drawing a straight line through curvy data. "Didn't study enough for the exam."

🌊
High Variance (Overfitting)

Model is too complex. Memorizes training noise. "Memorized every specific question but can't answer variations."

✨
Sweet Spot

The Goldilocks zone — model captures genuine patterns but ignores noise. Small gap between in-sample and out-of-sample performance.

Detecting Overfitting: The Methods

Method 1 · Train/Test Gap Analysis

The most direct method. Compare your strategy's performance on data it was trained on vs. data it never saw.

Overfitting Ratio
OFR = 1 − (Out-of-Sample Sharpe / In-Sample Sharpe)
OFR near 0 = Little overfitting (good)
OFR = 0.5 = Out-of-sample is half as good = Major concern
OFR > 0.5 = Likely overfitting — do NOT deploy this strategy

Method 2 · Cross-Validation (Walk-Forward)

Walk-Forward Cross-Validation for Finance

Method 3 · Combinatorial Purged Cross-Validation (CPCV)

Developed by Marcos López de Prado (a leading quant researcher), CPCV is a sophisticated technique that provides a Probability of Backtest Overfitting (PBO) — a single number from 0 to 1 telling you how likely your strategy's success was due to luck.

Probability of Backtest Overfitting (PBO)
PBO = P(OOS Sharpe < median | IS Sharpe = best)
PBO < 0.1 = Probably real (10% chance of being luck)
PBO = 0.5 = Coin flip — 50% chance your strategy is noise
PBO > 0.5 = Very likely overfitted — DON'T trade

OOS = Out-of-Sample | IS = In-Sample

Method 4 · Deflated Sharpe Ratio

When you try many variations of a strategy, you'll find a "winner" by chance. The Deflated Sharpe Ratio (DSR) corrects for this multiple testing problem. (We cover this deeply in Tab 2.)

Method 5 · Learning Curves

🎯 What to Look For

Plot training error vs. out-of-sample error as you increase training data size. If your strategy overfits: training error stays low but test error stays high — they never converge. A genuine signal: both curves converge to a similar level as data grows.

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# Overfitting Detection: Walk-Forward Cross-Validation
# Run this in Google Colab — paste and execute
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from sklearn.linear_model import Ridge
from sklearn.preprocessing import StandardScaler

# ── Step 1: Generate synthetic stock return data ──────
np.random.seed(42)
n = 1000  # 1000 days of data

# True signal: momentum (past 20-day return predicts next)
prices = np.cumprod(1 + np.random.normal(0.0005, 0.015, n))
returns = pd.Series(np.diff(prices) / prices[:-1])

# Build features: various lookback returns
df = pd.DataFrame()
for lag in [1, 5, 10, 20, 60]:
    df[f'ret_{lag}d'] = returns.rolling(lag).mean().shift(1)
df['target'] = returns
df = df.dropna()

# ── Step 2: Walk-Forward Cross-Validation ────────────
fold_size = 200
is_sharpes, oos_sharpes = [], []

for start in range(0, len(df) - fold_size*2, fold_size//2):
    train = df.iloc[start : start+fold_size]
    test  = df.iloc[start+fold_size : start+fold_size*2]
    
    X_train = train.drop('target', axis=1)
    y_train = train['target']
    X_test  = test.drop('target', axis=1)
    y_test  = test['target']
    
    scaler = StandardScaler()
    X_train_s = scaler.fit_transform(X_train)
    X_test_s  = scaler.transform(X_test)
    
    model = Ridge(alpha=0.1)
    model.fit(X_train_s, y_train)
    
    # Sharpe = mean(returns) / std(returns) * sqrt(252)
    is_pred  = model.predict(X_train_s)
    oos_pred = model.predict(X_test_s)
    
    is_ret  = y_train * np.sign(is_pred)
    oos_ret = y_test  * np.sign(oos_pred)
    
    is_sharpe  = is_ret.mean()  / is_ret.std()  * np.sqrt(252)
    oos_sharpe = oos_ret.mean() / oos_ret.std() * np.sqrt(252)
    
    is_sharpes.append(is_sharpe)
    oos_sharpes.append(oos_sharpe)

# ── Step 3: Plot & Detect Overfitting ────────────────
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10, 5))
folds = range(len(is_sharpes))
ax.plot(folds, is_sharpes,  label='In-Sample Sharpe',      marker='o', color='#7c3aed')
ax.plot(folds, oos_sharpes, label='Out-of-Sample Sharpe', marker='s', color='#00d4ff')
ax.axhline(0, color='white', linestyle='--', alpha=0.3)
ax.set_xlabel('Fold'); ax.set_ylabel('Sharpe Ratio')
ax.set_title('Walk-Forward: IS vs OOS Sharpe (gap = overfitting signal)')
ax.legend(); ax.set_facecolor('#111827'); fig.patch.set_facecolor('#0a0e1a')
plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()

# ── Step 4: Overfitting Ratio ─────────────────────────
avg_is  = np.mean(is_sharpes)
avg_oos = np.mean(oos_sharpes)
ofr = 1 - (avg_oos / avg_is)
print(f"Avg IS Sharpe:  {avg_is:.3f}")
print(f"Avg OOS Sharpe: {avg_oos:.3f}")
print(f"Overfitting Ratio: {ofr:.3f}  {'⚠️ OVERFIT' if ofr > 0.5 else '✅ OK'}")

Run this code, then try changing the Ridge alpha value (0.1 → 0.001 → 100). Watch how smaller alpha (less regularization) increases the IS-OOS gap. This demonstrates overfitting in real-time.

Step 1 · The Sharpe Ratio — Basics

🎯 Simple Analogy

Imagine two investors. Investor A makes 10% returns but their portfolio swings wildly (30% standard deviation). Investor B also makes 10% but with smooth, consistent gains (5% standard deviation). Investor B is clearly better per unit of risk. The Sharpe Ratio quantifies exactly this.

Sharpe Ratio Formula
SR = (μ_p − r_f) / σ_p
μ_p = average portfolio return (annualized)
r_f = risk-free rate (e.g., 5% annual from US Treasuries)
σ_p = standard deviation of portfolio returns (annualized)

Annualizing daily data: multiply daily mean by 252, multiply daily σ by √252

SR > 1.0 = Good  |  SR > 2.0 = Excellent  |  SR > 3.0 = Suspicious 🚨
Sharpe RatioInterpretationReal-World Context
< 0Losing money on risk-adjusted basisStrategy destroys value
0 – 0.5Marginal — barely beating risk-free rateBetter to just buy T-bills
0.5 – 1.0Acceptable for long-only equityTypical mutual fund
1.0 – 2.0Good — solid risk-adjusted returnsGood hedge fund target
2.0 – 3.0Excellent — rare but achievableTop-tier quant strategies
> 3.0⚠️ Suspicious — likely overfittedVerify with DSR immediately

Step 2 · The Multiple Testing Problem

🎯 The P-Hacking Analogy

If you flip a fair coin 100 times, you might get 65 heads by chance (bad luck run). If you flip 1000 different coins 100 times each, some will show 65+ heads just by chance. If you then say "this coin is biased!" based on its result, you're fooling yourself. Testing many strategies and picking the "winner" inflates your apparent success.

If you test N strategies and pick the best one, the expected maximum Sharpe ratio grows with N — even if every strategy is pure noise. This is called the multiple comparisons problem or data snooping bias.

Expected Max Sharpe from Random Strategies
🚨
The Quant's Dirty Secret

If a research team tests 1000 strategy variations and presents you the best one with a Sharpe of 3.5, you should expect a Sharpe around √(2·ln(1000)) ≈ 3.7 by pure chance. That amazing Sharpe might be completely meaningless.

Step 3 · The Deflated Sharpe Ratio (DSR)

The DSR corrects the observed Sharpe ratio for: (1) multiple testing, (2) non-normal returns, (3) serial correlation in returns, and (4) finite sample size. It gives a probability that your Sharpe ratio is genuinely above zero.

Deflated Sharpe Ratio — Step by Step
DSR = Φ[ (SR̂ − SR*) × √(T−1) / √(1 − γ₃·SR̂ + ((γ₄−1)/4)·SR̂²) ]
SR̂ = observed (sample) Sharpe ratio
SR* = expected maximum Sharpe from N trials (the "benchmark" — max from pure noise)
T = number of observations (data points)
γ₃ = skewness of returns (asymmetry)
γ₄ = kurtosis of returns (fat tails)
Φ = standard normal CDF (cumulative distribution function)

DSR > 0.95 = 95% confident your Sharpe is real → Strong signal
DSR < 0.95 = Likely due to luck or data mining

What SR* (Expected Max Sharpe) Looks Like

Expected Maximum Sharpe under Null (Bailey & López de Prado 2014)
SR* ≈ (1 − γ) · Φ⁻¹(1 − 1/N) + γ · Φ⁻¹(1 − 1/(N·e^0.5772))
N = number of strategies tested
γ ≈ 0.5772 (Euler-Mascheroni constant)
Φ⁻¹ = inverse normal CDF (percent point function)

Practical shorthand: SR* ≈ √(2·ln(N)) for large N

Step 4 · Non-Normal Returns & Adjustments

Real financial returns are not normally distributed. They have:

📉
Negative Skewness

Returns have a long left tail — rare but catastrophic losses. An overfitted strategy often has extreme negative skew: looks smooth but occasionally blows up.

🦢
Fat Tails (High Kurtosis)

"Black swan" events happen more often than a normal distribution predicts. Standard Sharpe underestimates risk when kurtosis is high. DSR corrects for this.

🔗
Serial Correlation

Returns can be correlated with their own past (momentum). This inflates the effective Sharpe ratio because it reduces measured volatility. DSR corrects for this too.

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# Sharpe Ratio Deflation Demo
# Shows how multiple testing inflates Sharpe
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

import numpy as np
from scipy import stats
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# ── Step 1: Compute annualized Sharpe ratio ──────────
def sharpe_ratio(returns, risk_free=0.0, periods=252):
    excess = returns - risk_free / periods
    if excess.std() == 0: return 0
    return excess.mean() / excess.std() * np.sqrt(periods)

# ── Step 2: Simulate N random strategies ─────────────
def simulate_strategies(N, T=252):
    """Generate N strategies of T daily returns — all pure noise"""
    sharpes = []
    for _ in range(N):
        returns = np.random.normal(0, 0.01, T)  # zero-mean = no real signal
        sharpes.append(sharpe_ratio(returns))
    return np.array(sharpes)

# ── Step 3: Expected Max Sharpe ──────────────────────
def expected_max_sharpe(N):
    """Bailey & Lopez de Prado approximation"""
    euler_gamma = 0.5772156649
    p1 = (1 - euler_gamma) * stats.norm.ppf(1 - 1/N)
    p2 = euler_gamma * stats.norm.ppf(1 - 1/(N * np.e**0.5772))
    return p1 + p2

# ── Step 4: Run the experiment ───────────────────────
N_values = [10, 50, 100, 500, 1000]
results = {}

for N in N_values:
    sharpes = simulate_strategies(N)
    results[N] = {
        'max_sharpe': sharpes.max(),
        'expected_max': expected_max_sharpe(N),
        'all_sharpes': sharpes
    }
    print(f"N={N:5d} | Max Sharpe (simulated): {sharpes.max():.3f} | "
          f"Expected Max: {expected_max_sharpe(N):.3f}")

# ── Step 5: Visualize ─────────────────────────────────
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(14, 5))

# Left: distribution of sharpes for N=1000
axes[0].hist(results[1000]['all_sharpes'], bins=50, 
             color='#7c3aed', alpha=0.7, edgecolor='none')
axes[0].axvline(results[1000]['max_sharpe'], color='#ef4444', 
                lw=2, label=f'Max = {results[1000]["max_sharpe"]:.2f}')
axes[0].set_title('Sharpe Distribution: 1000 Random Strategies')
axes[0].legend(); axes[0].set_xlabel('Sharpe Ratio')

# Right: max sharpe vs N
ns = list(results.keys())
maxes = [results[n]['max_sharpe'] for n in ns]
expected = [results[n]['expected_max'] for n in ns]
axes[1].plot(ns, maxes,    label='Simulated Max Sharpe', marker='o', color='#ff6b35')
axes[1].plot(ns, expected, label='Expected Max Sharpe',   marker='s', color='#00d4ff', ls='--')
axes[1].set_xscale('log'); axes[1].set_title('Max Sharpe Grows With # Strategies Tested')
axes[1].set_xlabel('N (strategies tested)'); axes[1].legend()

plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()

# ── Step 6: DSR Calculation ───────────────────────────
def deflated_sharpe_ratio(sr_obs, N_trials, T, skew, kurt):
    """
    sr_obs: observed Sharpe ratio (annualized, convert to per-period)
    N_trials: number of strategies tested
    T: number of return observations
    skew: skewness of returns
    kurt: kurtosis (excess) of returns
    """
    sr_per_period = sr_obs / np.sqrt(252)  # de-annualize
    sr_star = expected_max_sharpe(N_trials) / np.sqrt(252)
    
    # Non-normality adjustment
    numerator = (sr_per_period - sr_star) * np.sqrt(T - 1)
    denominator = np.sqrt(1 - skew * sr_per_period + 
                         (kurt - 1) / 4 * sr_per_period**2)
    
    return stats.norm.cdf(numerator / denominator)

# Example: our "great" strategy
dsr = deflated_sharpe_ratio(
    sr_obs=2.5,   # Impressive Sharpe!
    N_trials=200, # But we tested 200 strategies
    T=252,        # Only 1 year of data
    skew=-0.5,   # Slight negative skew
    kurt=3.0      # Slight fat tails
)
print(f"\nObserved Sharpe: 2.5 | N_trials: 200 | T: 252")
print(f"Deflated Sharpe Ratio (DSR): {dsr:.4f}")
print(f"Interpretation: {'REAL SIGNAL' if dsr > 0.95 else 'LIKELY NOISE'} (threshold: 0.95)")

What Is Stress Testing?

🎯 Simple Analogy

When Boeing designs an airplane, they don't just test it in sunny skies. They test it in extreme conditions: ice, turbulence, engine failure. Stress testing does the same for financial strategies: test them against financial "storms" — crashes, crises, pandemics — to see if they can survive.

A strategy that looks great on average historical data may have hidden vulnerabilities. Stress testing deliberately subjects your strategy to extreme scenarios to find its breaking points before real money is at risk.

Types of Stress Tests

📜
Historical Scenario Analysis

Run your strategy through real historical crises: 2008 Financial Crisis, COVID Crash (2020), Dot-com Bubble (2000), 1987 Black Monday. "How would I have done?"

🎲
Monte Carlo Simulation

Generate thousands of random market scenarios based on statistical properties. More comprehensive than history — tests situations that haven't happened yet.

🔬
Sensitivity Analysis

Change one variable at a time: "What if volatility doubles? What if correlations go to 1? What if liquidity disappears?" Isolates which factors kill your strategy.

🦢
Tail Risk / CVaR Analysis

Focus specifically on the worst outcomes. What does your worst 5% of days/months look like? Expected Shortfall (CVaR) is more useful than standard VaR here.

Value at Risk (VaR) & Conditional VaR (CVaR)

🎯 VaR Analogy

VaR(95%, 1-day) = -2% means: "On 95% of days, we won't lose more than 2%." Or: "We expect losses exceeding 2% about 5 trading days per 100 days." CVaR (also called Expected Shortfall) asks: "On those 5 bad days, how much do we lose on average?" CVaR is always worse than VaR and gives a more complete picture of tail risk.

VaR vs CVaR — Understanding Tail Risk
VaR and CVaR Formulas
VaR(α, T) = −Quantile_α(returns over T)
CVaR(α, T) = −E[returns | returns < −VaR(α,T)]
α = confidence level (e.g., 95% or 99%)
T = time horizon (e.g., 1 day, 10 days)
VaR(95%, 1d) = -2% → "95% confident we won't lose more than 2% tomorrow"
CVaR(95%, 1d) = -3.5% → "When we do breach VaR, average loss is 3.5%"
CVaR is always ≥ VaR and is preferred by modern risk managers

# Stress Testing: VaR, CVaR, Monte Carlo & Historical
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy import stats

# ── Step 1: Generate synthetic portfolio returns ──────
np.random.seed(42)
T = 252 * 10  # 10 years of daily returns

# Mixture model: normal times + crisis periods
normal_returns = np.random.normal(0.0004, 0.008, T)    # calm market
crisis_returns = np.random.normal(-0.002, 0.025, T)    # crisis market
is_crisis = np.random.choice([0, 1], size=T, p=[0.9, 0.1])  # 10% crisis
portfolio_returns = np.where(is_crisis, crisis_returns, normal_returns)
returns = pd.Series(portfolio_returns)

# ── Step 2: Compute VaR methods ──────────────────────
confidence = 0.95

# Historical (non-parametric) VaR
var_historical = -np.percentile(returns, (1-confidence)*100)

# Parametric (normal) VaR
mu, sigma = returns.mean(), returns.std()
var_parametric = -(mu + stats.norm.ppf(1-confidence) * sigma)

# CVaR (Expected Shortfall) — average of losses beyond VaR
tail_losses = returns[returns < -var_historical]
cvar = -tail_losses.mean()

print("═══ Value at Risk Analysis ═══")
print(f"Historical VaR (95%, 1d):  {var_historical:.4f} ({var_historical*100:.2f}%)")
print(f"Parametric VaR (95%, 1d):  {var_parametric:.4f} ({var_parametric*100:.2f}%)")
print(f"CVaR / Expected Shortfall: {cvar:.4f} ({cvar*100:.2f}%)")
print(f"CVaR / VaR ratio: {cvar/var_historical:.2f}x")

# ── Step 3: Monte Carlo Stress Test ──────────────────
def monte_carlo_stress(mu, sigma, skew_adj=-0.5, n_sims=10000, horizon=21):
    """Simulate 21-day (1 month) portfolio returns"""
    daily_sims = np.random.normal(mu, sigma, (n_sims, horizon))
    # Add fat tails via t-distribution mixing
    fat_tail_shock = np.random.standard_t(5, (n_sims, horizon)) * sigma * 0.3
    daily_sims = daily_sims + fat_tail_shock * np.random.choice([0,1], (n_sims, horizon), p=[0.95,0.05])
    monthly_returns = daily_sims.sum(axis=1)  # sum daily returns
    return monthly_returns

monthly_sims = monte_carlo_stress(mu, sigma)
monthly_var_95 = -np.percentile(monthly_sims, 5)
monthly_cvar_95 = -monthly_sims[monthly_sims < -monthly_var_95].mean()

print(f"\n═══ Monte Carlo (21-day horizon) ═══")
print(f"Monthly VaR  (95%): {monthly_var_95*100:.2f}%")
print(f"Monthly CVaR (95%): {monthly_cvar_95*100:.2f}%")
print(f"Worst 1% scenario: {np.percentile(monthly_sims, 1)*100:.2f}%")

# ── Step 4: Historical Crisis Scenarios ───────────────
scenarios = {
    'Normal Year':    [0.0004] * 252,
    'GFC 2008':       [np.random.normal(-0.004, 0.030) for _ in range(252)],
    'COVID 2020':      [np.random.normal(-0.003, 0.035) for _ in range(60)],
    'Rate Hike 2022': [np.random.normal(-0.001, 0.018) for _ in range(252)],
}

print("\n═══ Historical Scenario Analysis ═══")
for name, rets in scenarios.items():
    total_ret = (np.array(rets) + 1).prod() - 1
    vol = np.std(rets) * np.sqrt(252)
    dd = (1 + pd.Series(rets)).cumprod()
    max_dd = (dd / dd.cummax() - 1).min()
    print(f"{name:20} | Return: {total_ret*100:7.2f}% | Vol: {vol*100:.1f}% | MaxDD: {max_dd*100:.1f}%")

# ── Step 5: Visualize Return Distribution ─────────────
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(10, 5))
ax.hist(returns*100, bins=100, density=True, color='#7c3aed', alpha=0.6, label='Daily Returns')
ax.axvline(-var_historical*100, color='#ef4444', lw=2, label=f'VaR(95%): -{var_historical*100:.2f}%')
ax.axvline(-cvar*100, color='#f59e0b', lw=2, ls='--', label=f'CVaR(95%): -{cvar*100:.2f}%')
ax.set_xlabel('Daily Return (%)'); ax.set_title('Return Distribution with VaR & CVaR')
ax.legend(); plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()

The IC (Information Coefficient) measures how well your factor (signal) actually predicts future returns. It's the "ground truth test" for alpha factors in quantitative investing. High IC = your signal has real predictive power. Low IC = noise.

What Is a Factor?

🎯 Simple Analogy

A factor is a measurable characteristic of a stock that you believe predicts its future returns. Examples: "Stocks with high momentum (past 12-month return) tend to keep rising." "Cheap stocks (low Price-to-Book) tend to outperform." The IC tells you: is this belief supported by data, or is it just wishful thinking?

📈
Momentum Factor

Past 12-month return (excluding last month). Stocks that went up tend to keep going up. One of the most studied and robust factors.

💰
Value Factor

Price-to-Book, P/E ratio. "Cheap" stocks have historically outperformed. Popularized by Fama-French.

🏭
Quality Factor

Return on Equity, profit margins. Profitable, well-run companies tend to keep outperforming.

📊
Volatility Factor

Low-volatility stocks have surprisingly strong risk-adjusted returns. Counter-intuitive but robust.

The Information Coefficient — Explained

The IC is the Spearman rank correlation between your factor's predicted ranking of stocks and their actual subsequent returns. You rank all stocks by your factor score, then check if that ranking matched their actual return ranking.

Information Coefficient Formula
IC = Spearman_ρ(Factor_Ranks, Forward_Return_Ranks)
Factor_Ranks = rank of each stock by your factor (1=lowest, N=highest)
Forward_Return_Ranks = rank of each stock by its actual return over the next period

IC = +1: Perfect prediction (highest-ranked stock had highest return)
IC = 0: No predictive power (factor is useless)
IC = -1: Perfect inverse prediction (flip the signal)

Practical interpretation for equity quant factors:
IC < 0.02: Very weak / noise
IC 0.02–0.05: Marginal but potentially exploitable
IC 0.05–0.10: Good — typical for solid institutional factors
IC > 0.10: Excellent (rare; be suspicious of overfitting)
IC Illustrated — Factor Rank vs Return Rank

IC Information Ratio (ICIR)

A single IC value can be misleading — a factor might have IC=0.05 one month but IC=-0.03 the next. What matters is consistency. The ICIR measures this.

IC Information Ratio (ICIR)
ICIR = Mean(IC) / Std(IC)
Mean(IC) = average IC over all time periods (e.g., monthly IC over 5 years)
Std(IC) = standard deviation of monthly IC values

ICIR > 0.5: Solid factor — consistent predictive power
ICIR > 1.0: Excellent factor
Low ICIR: Factor is "noisy" — works sometimes, fails often (unreliable)

Grinold's Fundamental Law: IR ≈ IC × √N
where N = number of independent bets (stocks in your universe)

IC Decay Analysis

IC decay tells you: over how many days does your factor's predictive power last? A momentum factor might have strong IC at 1-month horizon but decay to zero by 12 months. This tells you the optimal holding period for your strategy.

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# IC Analysis: Compute IC, ICIR, and IC Decay
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

import numpy as np
import pandas as pd
from scipy import stats
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

np.random.seed(42)

# ── Step 1: Simulate a universe of stocks ─────────────
N_STOCKS = 500   # 500 stocks
N_PERIODS = 252  # 1 year of monthly observations → use weekly
N_WEEKS = 52 * 5  # 5 years weekly

# True factor: momentum (12-month return) has real IC ≈ 0.05
TRUE_IC = 0.05

# Generate factor scores and returns with real relationship
factor_scores = pd.DataFrame(
    np.random.randn(N_WEEKS, N_STOCKS),
    columns=[f'STOCK_{i}' for i in range(N_STOCKS)]
)

# Future returns = TRUE_IC * factor + noise
forward_returns = TRUE_IC * factor_scores.shift(1) + \
                  np.sqrt(1 - TRUE_IC**2) * pd.DataFrame(
                      np.random.randn(N_WEEKS, N_STOCKS),
                      columns=factor_scores.columns
                  )
forward_returns = forward_returns.dropna()
factor_scores = factor_scores.loc[forward_returns.index]

# ── Step 2: Compute IC (Spearman) per period ─────────
def compute_ic(factor_row, returns_row):
    """Spearman rank correlation between factor and next-period returns"""
    ic, pval = stats.spearmanr(factor_row, returns_row)
    return ic, pval

ics = []
pvals = []
for idx in factor_scores.index:
    ic, pval = compute_ic(factor_scores.loc[idx], forward_returns.loc[idx])
    ics.append(ic)
    pvals.append(pval)

ic_series = pd.Series(ics, index=factor_scores.index)

# ── Step 3: IC Statistics ─────────────────────────────
mean_ic = ic_series.mean()
std_ic  = ic_series.std()
icir    = mean_ic / std_ic
t_stat  = mean_ic / (std_ic / np.sqrt(len(ic_series)))  # t-test for IC != 0

print("═══ IC Analysis Results ═══")
print(f"Mean IC:   {mean_ic:.4f}  (target: {TRUE_IC:.2f})")
print(f"IC Std:    {std_ic:.4f}")
print(f"ICIR:      {icir:.4f}  {'✅ Strong' if icir > 0.5 else '⚠️ Weak'}")
print(f"t-statistic: {t_stat:.4f}  (|t|>2 = statistically significant)")
print(f"% periods IC > 0: {(ic_series > 0).mean()*100:.1f}%")
print(f"Grinold's IR ≈ IC × √N = {mean_ic * np.sqrt(N_STOCKS):.3f}")

# ── Step 4: IC Decay Analysis ─────────────────────────
horizons = [1, 2, 4, 8, 13, 26]  # weeks forward
decay_ics = []

for h in horizons:
    # Forward return h weeks ahead
    fwd_h = forward_returns.rolling(h).sum().shift(-h)
    fwd_h = fwd_h.dropna()
    
    period_ics = []
    for idx in fwd_h.index:
        if idx in factor_scores.index:
            ic, _ = compute_ic(factor_scores.loc[idx], fwd_h.loc[idx])
            period_ics.append(ic)
    decay_ics.append(np.mean(period_ics))

# ── Step 5: Visualize ─────────────────────────────────
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(15, 4))

# IC time series
axes[0].bar(range(len(ic_series)), ic_series.values, 
            color=['#10b981' if v > 0 else '#ef4444' for v in ic_series], alpha=0.7)
axes[0].axhline(mean_ic, color='#00d4ff', lw=2, label=f'Mean IC={mean_ic:.3f}')
axes[0].set_title('IC Over Time'); axes[0].legend()

# IC distribution
axes[1].hist(ic_series, bins=30, color='#7c3aed', alpha=0.7)
axes[1].axvline(0, color='white', lw=1, ls='--', alpha=0.5)
axes[1].axvline(mean_ic, color='#00d4ff', lw=2, label=f'Mean={mean_ic:.3f}')
axes[1].set_title('IC Distribution'); axes[1].legend()

# IC decay
axes[2].plot([h*5 for h in horizons], decay_ics, marker='o', color='#ff6b35', lw=2)
axes[2].axhline(0, color='white', lw=1, ls='--', alpha=0.5)
axes[2].set_xlabel('Forward Horizon (days)'); axes[2].set_ylabel('Mean IC')
axes[2].set_title('IC Decay Curve')

for ax in axes:
    ax.set_facecolor('#111827')
    ax.tick_params(colors='#64748b')
fig.patch.set_facecolor('#0a0e1a')
plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()

# ── Step 6: Factor Quintile Returns ───────────────────
print("\n═══ Quintile Analysis ═══")
all_factor = factor_scores.values.flatten()
all_returns = forward_returns.values.flatten()

quintile_labels = pd.qcut(all_factor, 5, labels=['Q1(Low)','Q2','Q3','Q4','Q5(High)'])
for q in ['Q1(Low)','Q2','Q3','Q4','Q5(High)']:
    avg_ret = all_returns[quintile_labels == q].mean()
    print(f"{q}: avg weekly return = {avg_ret*100:.3f}%")

Connecting IC to Strategy Performance

Grinold's Fundamental Law of Active Management connects IC directly to portfolio performance:

Grinold's Fundamental Law
IR ≈ IC × √N
IR = Information Ratio (like Sharpe of excess return vs benchmark)
IC = Information Coefficient (predictive accuracy per bet)
N = Breadth (number of independent bets per year)

Example: IC=0.05, N=500 stocks × 12 monthly bets = 6000
IR ≈ 0.05 × √6000 ≈ 3.87 → Exceptional portfolio performance

Key insight: Small IC × Large N beats Large IC × Small N
This is why quant funds prefer diversified systematic strategies over concentrated bets
