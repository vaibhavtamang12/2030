P1. What is a Time Series?

A time series is simply a sequence of numbers collected over time, in order. Think of it like a diary: every day you write down one number, and after a year you have 365 numbers in sequence. That's a time series.

🌡️ Real World Analogy
Your daily body temperature recorded at 8am every morning for a year is a time series. Stock prices, GDP, rainfall, heart rate — all time series. The key is: order matters. Shuffling the values destroys the meaning.
Figure P1 — A simple time series visualized

P2. Mean, Variance, and Standard Deviation

These are the three most fundamental summary statistics. If you know these cold, you can understand everything else.

Mean (μ)

The average of all values. Add them up, divide by how many there are.

μ = (x₁ + x₂ + ... + xₙ) / n
= Sum of all values / number of values

Example: Prices [10, 12, 11, 13, 9] → Mean = 55/5 = 11

Variance (σ²)

How "spread out" the values are from the mean. Big variance = wild swings.

σ² = Σ(xᵢ - μ)² / n
Average of squared deviations

Std Dev (σ): Square root of variance. Same units as your data.

P3. Probability & the Normal Distribution

Many statistical tests assume data follows a normal (Gaussian) distribution — the famous bell curve. This bell is symmetric around the mean, and ~68% of values fall within 1 standard deviation of the mean.

Figure P2 — Normal Distribution Bell Curve
📊 Why This Matters for Quant Finance
Many statistical tests in time series (like the ADF test for stationarity) use p-values derived from normal-like distributions. Understanding what a p-value means is essential.

p-value: The probability of seeing your data (or something more extreme) if the null hypothesis were true. Small p-value (< 0.05) → reject null hypothesis.

P4. Correlation

Correlation measures how two variables move together. It ranges from -1 to +1.

📈📈

+1 Perfect Positive

When X goes up, Y goes up by the same proportion. Example: Celsius & Fahrenheit temperatures.

🔀

0 No Correlation

X and Y move independently. Knowing X tells you nothing about Y.

📈📉

-1 Perfect Negative

When X goes up, Y goes down proportionally. Example: Bond prices vs interest rates.

Pearson Correlation: r = Σ[(xᵢ-μₓ)(yᵢ-μᵧ)] / (n · σₓ · σᵧ)
Ranges from -1 (perfect negative) to +1 (perfect positive)

P5. Random Walk — The Most Important Concept in Finance

A random walk is a process where each new value is the previous value plus a random shock (noise). It's like a drunk person walking — each step goes in a random direction.

Xₜ = Xₜ₋₁ + εₜ
where εₜ is random noise (white noise), drawn from N(0, σ²)
🚶 The Drunk Walker Analogy
Imagine someone standing at position 0 on a number line. Every second, they flip a fair coin: heads = step right (+1), tails = step left (-1). After 1000 steps, they could be anywhere. This is how stock prices behave — their future position is unpredictable from where they are now.
Figure P3 — Random Walk vs Stationary Series
Red = Random Walk (non-stationary) · Blue = Stationary series (wiggles around a fixed mean)

P6. White Noise

White noise is a sequence of random values that are: (1) independent of each other, (2) have zero mean, and (3) have constant variance. It's the simplest stationary process and serves as the "residual" or "error term" in almost all time series models.

εₜ ~ WN(0, σ²)
Independently and identically distributed (i.i.d.) with mean 0 and variance σ²

P7. Regression Basics

Linear regression fits a line through data: Y = β₀ + β₁X + ε. In time series, we often regress one series on another. The residuals (ε) are the leftover variation not explained by X. These residuals play a huge role in cointegration.

What Does Stationary Mean?

A time series is stationary if its statistical properties — particularly its mean and variance — do not change over time. Think of it as a series that has a stable "personality."

🌊 Ocean Analogy
Imagine ocean waves at a beach. The waves go up and down, but over time the average height of waves stays the same, and the height range (variance) stays similar. That's stationary. Now imagine a tsunami growing bigger and bigger — that's non-stationary: its properties change over time.

The 3 Conditions for (Weak) Stationarity

In practice, we use weak (covariance) stationarity, which requires three things:

1. Constant Mean

E[Xₜ] = μ for all t

The series wiggles around a fixed horizontal level. It doesn't trend upward or downward.

2. Constant Variance

Var[Xₜ] = σ² for all t

The swings in the series don't get bigger or smaller over time. No "explosive" behavior.

3. Autocovariance depends only on lag

Cov[Xₜ, Xₜ₋ₖ] = γ(k)

How correlated values are k steps apart is the same regardless of when you measure it.

Figure 1.1 — Stationary vs Non-Stationary Series
■ Stationary: wiggles around constant mean  |  ■ Trending (non-stationary): mean increases  |  ■ Random Walk (non-stationary): variance grows

Why Stationarity Matters So Much

⚡ Critical Insight
Almost every classical statistical model (regression, ARIMA, etc.) assumes stationarity. If you apply these models to a non-stationary series, you get spurious results — results that look statistically significant but are completely meaningless.

Famous example: Regressing US cheese consumption against the number of people who died by getting tangled in bed sheets — both trended upward, so R² ≈ 0.95, but this is nonsense. This is called spurious regression.

The Unit Root — The Heart of Non-Stationarity

Consider the AR(1) model: Xₜ = φ · Xₜ₋₁ + εₜ

  • If |φ| < 1: Series is stationary. Shocks decay over time.
  • If φ = 1: Series is a random walk — has a "unit root." Shocks persist forever.
  • If φ > 1: Series explodes. Gets bigger and bigger (almost never in practice).

0.80
→ Stationary (shocks decay)

Making a Series Stationary: Differencing

The most common fix for a unit root is first differencing: instead of modeling Xₜ, model the changes ΔXₜ = Xₜ - Xₜ₋₁.

📈 Stock Price Analogy
Stock price levels are non-stationary (they trend upward over time). But daily returns (percentage changes) are approximately stationary — they wiggle around zero. This is why quant models usually work with log returns, not price levels.
If Xₜ is non-stationary (I(1)):
ΔXₜ = Xₜ - Xₜ₋₁ → usually stationary

Notation: I(1) means "integrated of order 1" — needs 1 difference to become stationary
Figure 1.2 — Stock Price vs Log Returns
Top: Non-stationary price level (random walk) · Bottom: Stationary first differences (returns)

Testing for Stationarity

The Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) Test

The most widely used test. It tests the null hypothesis that a unit root exists (i.e., the series is non-stationary).

H₀: Series has a unit root (non-stationary)
H₁: Series is stationary

Decision: If p-value < 0.05 → Reject H₀ → Series is stationary
Counterintuitive: you WANT a small p-value (means the series IS stationary)
⚠️ Common Mistake
In the ADF test, a large p-value means the series is non-stationary. This confuses beginners because normally "significant result" means small p-value. Here: small p-value = stationary (good!).
Python from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import adfuller import pandas as pd # Load a stock price series prices = pd.Series([100, 101.2, 102.5, 100.8, 103.1, ...]) # Run ADF test on price levels result = adfuller(prices, autolag='AIC') print(f"ADF Statistic: {result[0]:.4f}") print(f"p-value: {result[1]:.4f}") print("Critical Values:", result[4]) # Interpretation if result[1] < 0.05: print("✅ Stationary (reject unit root)") else: print("❌ Non-stationary (fail to reject unit root)") # Now test on first differences (returns) returns = prices.diff().dropna() result2 = adfuller(returns) print(f"\nAfter differencing, p-value: {result2[1]:.4f}")

The KPSS Test (Alternative)

The KPSS test flips the hypotheses: H₀ is stationarity, and a small p-value means non-stationary. Use both ADF and KPSS together for robustness.

TestH₀ (Null)Small p-value meansLarge p-value means
ADFNon-stationary (unit root)✅ Stationary❌ Non-stationary
KPSSStationary❌ Non-stationary✅ Stationary

Order of Integration I(d)

A series is I(d) if you need to difference it d times to make it stationary:

  • I(0): Already stationary. Most economic rates, returns.
  • I(1): Need 1 difference. Most price levels, GDP levels, CPI.
  • I(2): Need 2 differences. Rare. Some price indices during hyperinflation.
💡 Quant Finance Connection
Stock log-prices are I(1). Log-returns are I(0). This is why pairs trading is built on cointegration (coming in Tab 4) — two I(1) series can combine to form an I(0) series, which is mean-reverting and tradeable.

What is Autocorrelation?

Regular correlation measures how two different variables move together. Autocorrelation (also called serial correlation) measures how a time series correlates with itself at different time lags.

🌡️ Weather Analogy
If today is hot, tomorrow is probably also hot. If it rained today, there's a higher chance it rains tomorrow. This is autocorrelation — yesterday's temperature predicts today's temperature. The "lag" is the time gap (1 day, 2 days, 1 week, etc.).

The Autocorrelation Function (ACF)

The ACF computes the correlation between the series and its lagged version, for lags k = 0, 1, 2, 3, ...

ACF(k) = Corr(Xₜ, Xₜ₋ₖ)
= Cov(Xₜ, Xₜ₋ₖ) / Var(Xₜ)

At k=0: ACF = 1 always (a series is perfectly correlated with itself)
At k=1: Correlation with yesterday's value
At k=7: Correlation with last week's value (for daily data)
Figure 2.1 — ACF Plot (Correlogram) for Different Series
■ Bars = autocorrelation at each lag  |  Dashed lines = 95% confidence bounds (±1.96/√n)  |  Bars outside bounds are statistically significant

The Partial Autocorrelation Function (PACF)

The PACF is trickier: it shows the direct correlation between Xₜ and Xₜ₋ₖ, after removing the influence of all intermediate lags.

🔗 Chain Analogy
Suppose today's temperature correlates with 3 days ago. But maybe that's only because: today → yesterday → day before → 3 days ago. The PACF removes this indirect chain effect and shows only the direct connection. If PACF at lag 3 is near zero after removing lags 1 and 2, the apparent correlation at lag 3 was spurious.

Reading ACF/PACF Plots — The Pattern Decoder

This is the most important skill for building ARIMA models. Learn these patterns cold:

Ljung-Box Test: Is There Significant Autocorrelation?

The Ljung-Box test checks whether there is any significant autocorrelation across multiple lags jointly. Useful for checking model residuals.

H₀: No autocorrelation at lags 1 through m (residuals are white noise)
H₁: At least one lag has significant autocorrelation

If p-value < 0.05 → significant autocorrelation remains → model needs improvement
Python import numpy as np import matplotlib.pyplot as plt from statsmodels.graphics.tsaplots import plot_acf, plot_pacf from statsmodels.stats.diagnostic import acorr_ljungbox # Generate example series (AR(1) with phi=0.7) np.random.seed(42) n = 300 x = np.zeros(n) x[0] = np.random.randn() for t in range(1, n): x[t] = 0.7 * x[t-1] + np.random.randn() # Plot ACF and PACF fig, (ax1, ax2) = plt.subplots(2, 1, figsize=(12, 6)) plot_acf(x, lags=30, ax=ax1, title='ACF — AR(1) with φ=0.7') plot_pacf(x, lags=30, ax=ax2, title='PACF — AR(1) with φ=0.7') plt.tight_layout() plt.show() # Ljung-Box test for first 20 lags lb_test = acorr_ljungbox(x, lags=[5, 10, 20], return_df=True) print(lb_test) # p-values should be small — confirming autocorrelation exists

Autocovariance Function

Related to ACF, the autocovariance function (ACVF) γ(k) measures covariance (not normalized correlation) at lag k. ACF is just the normalized version: ρ(k) = γ(k)/γ(0).

Durbin-Watson Statistic

A simpler test specifically for lag-1 autocorrelation in regression residuals. Ranges from 0 to 4:

  • ≈ 2: No autocorrelation ✅
  • < 2: Positive autocorrelation (consecutive residuals are similar)
  • > 2: Negative autocorrelation (residuals alternate in sign)

Why Autocorrelation Matters for Quants

📊 Model Building

ACF/PACF plots tell you what AR and MA orders to use in ARIMA. Without them, you're guessing blindly.

🔍 Model Diagnosis

After fitting a model, residuals should be white noise (no autocorrelation). If ACF of residuals has significant spikes, the model is incomplete.

💰 Trading Signals

Positive autocorrelation at short lags → momentum strategy. Negative autocorrelation → mean-reversion strategy.

⚠️ Regression Problems

Autocorrelated errors in OLS regression make standard errors wrong, invalidating t-tests and confidence intervals.

Building Blocks: AR and MA

ARIMA is built from three components: AR (autoregressive), I (integrated/differencing), and MA (moving average). Let's understand each.

AR — AutoRegressive Models

An AR(p) model says: the current value is a linear function of the past p values, plus noise.

AR(p): Xₜ = c + φ₁Xₜ₋₁ + φ₂Xₜ₋₂ + ... + φₚXₜ₋ₚ + εₜ

c = constant (intercept)
φ₁...φₚ = AR coefficients (how much weight to give each past value)
εₜ = white noise shock
🎯 Momentum Trading Analogy
AR models are like momentum strategies. If you buy a stock because it went up yesterday AND the day before, you're implicitly using AR(2) logic: Xₜ = φ₁Xₜ₋₁ + φ₂Xₜ₋₂ + noise.
Python # Simulate AR(2) process: Xₜ = 0.6*Xₜ₋₁ + 0.2*Xₜ₋₂ + εₜ import numpy as np np.random.seed(42) n, phi1, phi2 = 500, 0.6, 0.2 x = np.zeros(n) eps = np.random.randn(n) for t in range(2, n): x[t] = phi1 * x[t-1] + phi2 * x[t-2] + eps[t] # PACF will show significant spikes at lags 1 and 2, then cut off

MA — Moving Average Models

An MA(q) model says: the current value depends on the current shock plus a weighted sum of past shocks (not past values of X itself!)

MA(q): Xₜ = μ + εₜ + θ₁εₜ₋₁ + θ₂εₜ₋₂ + ... + θqεₜ₋q

μ = mean of the series
θ₁...θq = MA coefficients
εₜ, εₜ₋₁... = current and past shocks (unobservable noise)
📰 News Shock Analogy
Imagine a CEO's press statement that caused a stock to spike. The MA model says: today's price is affected by today's news shock PLUS some residual effect from yesterday's news shock AND last week's news shock. The effect of news fades over time according to the θ weights.

Combining: ARMA(p,q)

Put them together and you get ARMA(p,q):

ARMA(p,q): Xₜ = c + φ₁Xₜ₋₁ + ... + φₚXₜ₋ₚ + εₜ + θ₁εₜ₋₁ + ... + θqεₜ₋q

The "I" in ARIMA: Integration

The I(d) part handles non-stationarity. If a series needs to be differenced d times to become stationary, we include that in the model. For most economic series, d=1.

ARIMA(p, d, q)

p = order of AR part
d = number of differences (integration order)
q = order of MA part

ARIMA(0,1,0) = Random Walk
ARIMA(1,0,0) = AR(1) on stationary series
ARIMA(1,1,1) = AR(1)+MA(1) on first-differenced series
Figure 3.1 — ARIMA Model Structure

The Box-Jenkins Methodology — ARIMA in 4 Steps

George Box and Gwilym Jenkins developed a systematic procedure for ARIMA model building:

  1. Identification: Plot the series. Use ADF/KPSS to check stationarity. Difference if needed (determines d). Plot ACF/PACF of stationary series to guess p and q.
  2. Estimation: Fit the model using MLE (Maximum Likelihood Estimation) or conditional least squares.
  3. Diagnostics: Check residuals. They should look like white noise: no autocorrelation (ACF/PACF all near zero), no patterns, Ljung-Box p-value > 0.05.
  4. Forecasting: Use the fitted model to forecast future values with confidence intervals.

Model Selection: AIC and BIC

When unsure between multiple candidate models, use information criteria. Lower is better.

AIC = 2k - 2·ln(L)
BIC = k·ln(n) - 2·ln(L)

k = number of model parameters, n = sample size, L = likelihood
AIC penalizes parameters less; BIC penalizes more (prefers simpler models)
Python import pandas as pd import numpy as np from statsmodels.tsa.arima.model import ARIMA from statsmodels.graphics.tsaplots import plot_acf, plot_pacf import matplotlib.pyplot as plt # Step 1: Generate sample non-stationary data (ARIMA(1,1,1) process) np.random.seed(42) n = 300 shocks = np.random.randn(n) x_stat = np.zeros(n) x_stat[0] = shocks[0] for t in range(1, n): x_stat[t] = 0.5 * x_stat[t-1] + shocks[t] + 0.3 * shocks[t-1] x_nonstat = np.cumsum(x_stat) # Integrate once → I(1) series series = pd.Series(x_nonstat) # Step 2: Check stationarity of original from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import adfuller adf = adfuller(series) print(f"Original series ADF p-value: {adf[1]:.4f}") # Should be > 0.05 # Step 3: Fit ARIMA model model = ARIMA(series, order=(1, 1, 1)) result = model.fit() print(result.summary()) # Step 4: Diagnostic check residuals = result.resid adf_resid = adfuller(residuals.dropna()) print(f"\nResiduals ADF p-value: {adf_resid[1]:.4f}") # Should be < 0.05 # Step 5: Forecast 20 steps ahead forecast = result.forecast(steps=20) conf_int = result.get_forecast(steps=20).conf_int() print("\nForecast:", forecast.values[:5])

For quick model selection, pmdarima's auto_arima searches over (p,d,q) combinations and picks the best by AIC/BIC.

SARIMA — Seasonal Extension

SARIMA(p,d,q)(P,D,Q)[m] adds seasonal AR, differencing, and MA terms for series with repeating patterns (monthly seasonality m=12, quarterly m=4, etc.).

SARIMA(p,d,q)(P,D,Q)[m]

Lowercase (p,d,q) = non-seasonal component
Uppercase (P,D,Q) = seasonal component
m = seasonal period (12 for monthly, 4 for quarterly)

Model Diagnostics — What Good Residuals Look Like

✅ Good Residuals

  • ACF: All bars within confidence bounds
  • Histogram: Approximately normal
  • QQ-plot: Points near diagonal
  • Ljung-Box: p-value > 0.05
  • No visible patterns over time

❌ Bad Residuals Signal

  • Significant ACF spikes → increase p or q
  • PACF spike at lag 1 → add AR term
  • ACF spike at lag m → add seasonal term
  • Increasing variance → try log transform
  • Outliers → investigate data quality

The magic behind pairs trading. Two non-stationary series that move together in a stable long-run relationship.

The Puzzle That Motivates Cointegration

We know that two random walks (non-stationary series) shouldn't be regressed on each other — it gives spurious results. But here's the puzzle: what if two random walks move so closely together that their difference is stationary?

🐕 The Dog on a Leash Analogy
Imagine a drunk owner (random walk 1) and their dog on a leash (random walk 2). Both wander randomly across the park — they're non-stationary. But they can't stray too far from each other because of the leash. The distance between them (the spread) is bounded and mean-reverting — that's stationary! The leash is the cointegrating relationship.

This is cointegration: two or more I(1) series that share a common stochastic trend, such that a linear combination of them is I(0) (stationary).

Formal Definition

Two series Xₜ and Yₜ are cointegrated if:

1. Both are I(1) (non-stationary individually)
2. There exists β such that: Zₜ = Yₜ - β·Xₜ is I(0) (stationary)

Zₜ is called the cointegrating residual or spread
β is the cointegrating coefficient
Figure 4.1 — Cointegrated Pair vs Non-Cointegrated Pair
Left: Cointegrated pair (spread mean-reverts) · Right: Non-cointegrated pair (spread drifts away)

Why This Matters in Finance: Pairs Trading

Pairs trading is one of the most famous stat-arb strategies, built entirely on cointegration:

  1. Find two stocks that are cointegrated (e.g., Coca-Cola & Pepsi, Gold ETF & Gold Futures)
  2. Estimate the cointegrating vector β (from regression: Y = β·X + ε)
  3. Compute the spread: Zₜ = Yₜ - β·Xₜ. This should be mean-reverting.
  4. When spread is too high (above mean + 2σ): Short Y, Long X
  5. When spread is too low (below mean - 2σ): Long Y, Short X
  6. Close positions when spread returns to mean
💰 The Intuition
If Coke and Pepsi are cointegrated and Coke suddenly becomes much more expensive relative to Pepsi (spread is high), the market will likely correct this imbalance. You profit by shorting the overpriced one and buying the underpriced one, betting on mean-reversion.

Testing for Cointegration

Engle-Granger 2-Step Test

The simplest approach for two series:

  1. Step 1: Regress Y on X: Yₜ = α + β·Xₜ + eₜ. Save the residuals eₜ.
  2. Step 2: Apply ADF test to residuals eₜ. If residuals are stationary (ADF p < 0.05), the series are cointegrated.
⚠️ Important Caveat
The Engle-Granger test uses special critical values (not standard ADF ones) because the residuals are from a regression. Use the coint() function in statsmodels which handles this correctly.
Python import numpy as np import pandas as pd from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import coint, adfuller import matplotlib.pyplot as plt np.random.seed(42) n = 500 # Simulate a cointegrated pair common_trend = np.cumsum(np.random.randn(n)) # Shared I(1) trend X = common_trend + np.random.randn(n) * 0.5 # X tracks the trend Y = 2 * common_trend + np.random.randn(n) * 0.5 # Y = 2*trend + noise # True cointegrating vector: Y - 2X should be stationary # Engle-Granger test using statsmodels score, pvalue, crit_values = coint(X, Y) print(f"Cointegration test score: {score:.4f}") print(f"p-value: {pvalue:.4f}") print(f"Critical values (1%, 5%, 10%): {crit_values}") if pvalue < 0.05: print("✅ Series ARE cointegrated") else: print("❌ Series are NOT cointegrated") # Step 2: Compute the spread for trading from numpy.linalg import lstsq beta = lstsq(np.column_stack([X, np.ones(n)]), Y, rcond=None)[0][0] spread = Y - beta * X print(f"\nEstimated β: {beta:.4f}") # Verify spread is stationary adf_spread = adfuller(spread) print(f"Spread ADF p-value: {adf_spread[1]:.4f}") # Should be < 0.05 # Generate trading signals spread_mean = spread.mean() spread_std = spread.std() z_score = (spread - spread_mean) / spread_std entry_long = z_score < -2 # Buy spread when 2σ below mean entry_short = z_score > 2 # Sell spread when 2σ above mean print(f"\nLong entry signals: {entry_long.sum()}") print(f"Short entry signals: {entry_short.sum()}")

Johansen Test (for Multiple Series)

When testing for cointegration among 3+ series, use the Johansen test. It also tells you how many cointegrating relationships exist (the "cointegration rank").

Johansen Test:
H₀: At most r cointegrating vectors
H₁: More than r cointegrating vectors

Tests are done sequentially: r=0, then r=1, etc.
Two test statistics: Trace statistic and Max-eigenvalue statistic
Python from statsmodels.tsa.vector_ar.vecm import coint_johansen # Stack multiple I(1) series into a matrix data = np.column_stack([X, Y]) # Johansen test (det_order=-1: no constant, k_ar_diff=1: one lag) result = coint_johansen(data, det_order=0, k_ar_diff=1) print("Eigenvalues:", result.eig) print("Trace statistics:", result.lr1) print("Critical values (90%, 95%, 99%):\n", result.cvt) print("Cointegrating vectors:\n", result.evec)

Error Correction Models (ECM)

When series are cointegrated, you should use an Error Correction Model instead of a VAR in differences. The ECM captures both short-run dynamics and long-run equilibrium.

ΔYₜ = α + γ·Zₜ₋₁ + β₁·ΔXₜ₋₁ + εₜ

γ = error correction coefficient (how fast Y corrects back to equilibrium)
Zₜ₋₁ = lagged spread (error from long-run relationship)
-1 < γ < 0: Negative γ creates mean-reversion pull back to equilibrium
⚖️ Equilibrium Analogy
The ECM is like a rubber band. When the spread (Zₜ₋₁) is stretched above equilibrium, the negative γ pulls Y back down. When the spread is compressed below equilibrium, it pulls Y back up. The larger |γ|, the faster the adjustment.

5. Granger Causality

What is Granger Causality?

Granger causality is a statistical concept of causality, not true philosophical causality. It was developed by Nobel laureate Clive Granger in 1969.

🔑 The Core Idea
X Granger-causes Y if: past values of X contain information that helps predict future values of Y, above and beyond the information in Y's own past.

This is predictive causality — "X precedes Y and adds predictive value." It does NOT mean X truly causes Y in a scientific sense.
📺 TV Ratings Analogy
Suppose Super Bowl ad spending goes up, and 2 weeks later product sales go up. Does ad spending Granger-cause sales? The Granger test would check: knowing last month's ad spending, can we better predict this month's sales than using only past sales alone? If yes → Granger causality (but beware: both might be driven by a third factor like the economy!).

The Formal Test: Two Regressions

Granger causality is tested by comparing two models:

Model 1: Restricted

Predict Y using only Y's own past lags:

Yₜ = α + Σβᵢ·Yₜ₋ᵢ + εₜ

This is the baseline — how well can Y predict itself?

Model 2: Unrestricted

Predict Y using Y's past AND X's past lags:

Yₜ = α + Σβᵢ·Yₜ₋ᵢ + Σγᵢ·Xₜ₋ᵢ + εₜ

Does adding X's past significantly reduce the error?

Null Hypothesis H₀: γ₁ = γ₂ = ... = γₖ = 0
(X's past lags add NO predictive value — X does NOT Granger-cause Y)

Test: F-statistic (or Chi-squared)
If p-value < 0.05 → Reject H₀ → X Granger-causes Y
Figure 5.1 — Granger Causality Framework

Critical Assumptions

⚠️ Assumptions You Must Check Before Running Granger Test
  1. Both series must be stationary. If they're I(1), difference them first (unless cointegrated — then use VECM).
  2. Correct lag selection. Use AIC/BIC to choose the number of lags k. Too few lags = miss the relationship; too many = noisy test.
  3. Granger causality ≠ real causality. A rooster crowing Granger-causes sunrise — but roosters don't make the sun rise. Common third variables can drive both series.

Granger Causality in Python

Python import numpy as np import pandas as pd from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import grangercausalitytests from statsmodels.tsa.stattools import adfuller np.random.seed(42) n = 400 # Simulate X that causes Y with a 2-period lag X = np.random.randn(n) Y = np.zeros(n) Y[0], Y[1] = np.random.randn(2) for t in range(2, n): Y[t] = 0.4 * Y[t-1] + 0.6 * X[t-2] + np.random.randn() * 0.3 # Check stationarity first! for name, series in [("X", X), ("Y", Y)]: pval = adfuller(series)[1] print(f"{name} ADF p-value: {pval:.4f} → {'Stationary ✅' if pval < 0.05 else 'Non-stationary ❌'}") # Stack into dataframe: grangercausalitytests needs [Y, X] order df = pd.DataFrame({'Y': Y, 'X': X}) print("\n=== Granger Causality Test: Does X cause Y? ===") gc_results = grangercausalitytests(df[['Y', 'X']], maxlag=5, verbose=True) # Interpret: check if lag 2 gives small p-value (we know true lag=2) print("\n=== Reverse: Does Y cause X? (should be NO) ===") gc_results_rev = grangercausalitytests(df[['X', 'Y']], maxlag=5, verbose=False) # Extract F-test p-values for each lag print("\nLag | p-value (X→Y) | p-value (Y→X)") print("-" * 40) for lag in range(1, 6): p1 = gc_results[lag][0]['ssr_ftest'][1] p2 = gc_results_rev[lag][0]['ssr_ftest'][1] print(f" {lag} | {p1:.4f} | {p2:.4f}")

Granger Causality in a VAR System

For systems with more than 2 variables, Granger causality is typically tested within a Vector Autoregression (VAR) model. VAR extends ARIMA to multiple time series simultaneously.

VAR(p) System (2 variables):

Yₜ = A₁Yₜ₋₁ + A₂Yₜ₋₂ + ... + AₚYₜ₋ₚ + εₜ

where Yₜ = [y₁ₜ, y₂ₜ, ...]' is a vector of all variables
A₁...Aₚ are coefficient matrices
Granger test: are the cross-variable coefficients jointly zero?
Python from statsmodels.tsa.api import VAR # Fit VAR model to data df_var = pd.DataFrame({'y1': Y, 'y2': X}) model = VAR(df_var) # Select lag order using information criteria lag_order = model.select_order(maxlags=10) print(lag_order.summary()) # Fit chosen order results = model.fit(lag_order.aic) print(results.summary()) # Test Granger causality within VAR gc_test = results.test_causality('y1', causing='y2', kind='f') print(f"\nDoes y2 Granger-cause y1?") print(gc_test.summary())

Granger Causality vs Cointegration

These concepts are related but distinct. Here's the key relationship:

FeatureGranger CausalityCointegration
What it testsPredictive relationshipLong-run equilibrium
Series requirementMust be stationaryBoth must be I(1)
DirectionOne-way or bidirectionalSymmetric relationship
Time horizonShort-run dynamicsLong-run relationship
Main useCausal inference, predictionPairs trading, ECM
Model contextVAR on stationary dataVECM on I(1) cointegrated data
🔗 The Connection
If two series are cointegrated, there must be Granger causality in at least one direction (Granger Representation Theorem). The ECM's error correction term captures the long-run Granger causal relationship.

Does gold price Granger-cause gold miners' stock prices? Does oil Granger-cause the Canadian dollar (petrocurrency)? Used in cross-asset strategies.

Topic 4: Cointegration Exercises

🟢 Easy — Conceptual
  1. Explain cointegration using the "drunk owner and dog on leash" analogy. Why must both series be I(1) for cointegration to make sense?
  2. What is the difference between a spurious regression and a cointegrating regression? Both involve regressing one non-stationary series on another — what determines which is which?
  3. In pairs trading, what does the "spread" represent? Why should it be mean-reverting if the two stocks are cointegrated?
  4. What is the Engle-Granger two-step procedure? Describe each step without code.
  5. What is the Error Correction Model (ECM)? What does the error correction coefficient γ represent? What happens if γ = 0?
🟡 Medium — Applied
  1. Test for cointegration between: (a) Brent and WTI crude oil prices, (b) EUR/USD and GBP/USD exchange rates, (c) Gold spot price and GLD ETF, (d) S&P 500 and NASDAQ. Download data using yfinance, run both Engle-Granger and Johansen tests. Do they agree?

  3. Simulate the Error Correction Model: generate cointegrated data, fit the ECM, and verify that γ is negative and significant. Visualize how fast the system corrects back to equilibrium for different values of γ.
  4. Investigate how stable the cointegrating relationship is over time. Use rolling windows of 1 year to estimate the hedge ratio β for a cointegrated pair. Plot β over time — is it stable? What happens to your trading strategy if β is unstable?
  5. Compare two hedge ratio estimation methods: (a) OLS regression, (b) Johansen cointegrating vector. Generate data with known β=1.5, estimate using both methods, and compare bias and variance across 500 simulations.

Topic 5: Granger Causality Exercises

🟢 Easy — Conceptual
  1. "The rooster's crow Granger-causes the sunrise." Explain what this means and why Granger causality ≠ true causality. Give your own example of spurious Granger causality driven by a common third factor.
  2. In the Granger causality test, what are the restricted and unrestricted models? What is the null hypothesis? How is the F-statistic computed?
  3. Why must you ensure stationarity before running a Granger test? What could go wrong if you test non-stationary series?
  4. What is the difference between unidirectional and bidirectional Granger causality? Give a financial example of each.
  5. How does the choice of lag length k affect the Granger causality test? What happens with too few lags? Too many lags?
🟡 Medium — Applied
  1. Test for Granger causality between: (a) Gold prices and USD index, (b) VIX (fear index) and S&P 500 returns, (c) Oil prices and airline stock returns, (d) Bitcoin and Ethereum returns. Use proper lag selection (AIC) and report both directions of causality.

(b) Generate 1000 series with true Granger causality at lag 2 — what % correctly detect it? How does power change with n?
  3. Fit a bivariate VAR model to S&P 500 returns and VIX changes. Test Granger causality in both directions within the VAR framework. Produce impulse response functions (IRFs) to show how a shock to VIX affects future S&P returns.
