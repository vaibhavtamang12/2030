⚠ What You Need First

All five topics below are used throughout MoM and MLE. Take 5–10 minutes per section. You don't need mastery — just enough familiarity to follow the main content comfortably.

1. Probability Distributions & Parameters

A probability distribution is a mathematical recipe that describes the randomness of a variable. Most distributions are characterised by one or a few numbers called parameters — the unknowns we want to estimate.

Parameter (θ)
A fixed but unknown number that controls the shape of a distribution. For a coin, θ = p (probability of heads). For a Normal, θ = (μ, σ). MoM and MLE are two methods to estimate θ from data.
Parametric Model
A family of distributions indexed by parameters. Example: X ~ Normal(μ, σ²) — the family is "all normal distributions." Choosing μ=0, σ=1 pins down one member of that family.

The Recipe Analogy

Think of a distribution family as a recipe template (e.g., "chocolate cake"), and parameters as the specific quantities (e.g., "2 cups flour, 1 cup sugar"). The recipe tells you the shape; the parameters fill in the exact amounts. Estimation is reading measurements from a finished cake (data) and guessing what quantities were used.

Same Family, Different Parameters
Normal(μ, σ²) with varying μ and σ
Exponential(λ) — changing λ
Different rates λ → different shapes

2. Expected Value & Moments

The k-th moment of a distribution is E[Xᵏ] — the expected value of X raised to the power k. Moments capture shape properties of a distribution.

Moments
μₖ = E[Xᵏ] = ∫ xᵏ · f(x; θ) dx
1st moment (k=1): E[X] = mean. 2nd moment (k=2): E[X²]. Central 2nd moment: E[(X−μ)²] = variance.
MomentDefinitionWhat It Tells YouExample: Normal(μ,σ²)
1st (mean)E[X]Centre of distributionμ
2nd centralE[(X−μ)²]Spread / varianceσ²
3rd centralE[(X−μ)³]/σ³Skewness (asymmetry)0 (symmetric)
4th centralE[(X−μ)⁴]/σ⁴Kurtosis (tail weight)3 (normal baseline)

3. Likelihood — What Does It Mean?

Given data x₁,...,xₙ and a model with parameter θ, the likelihood L(θ) answers: "How probable was this data, if θ were the true parameter?"

Likelihood
L(θ; x₁,...,xₙ) = ∏ᵢ f(xᵢ; θ)
For i.i.d. data, joint probability = product of individual probabilities. L(θ) is a function of θ, not of the data.

The Likelihood Intuition — Reverse Detective

Imagine you observe three coin flips: Heads, Heads, Tails. If the coin were fair (θ=0.5): L(0.5) = 0.5 × 0.5 × 0.5 = 0.125. If the coin were biased to 70% heads (θ=0.7): L(0.7) = 0.7 × 0.7 × 0.3 = 0.147. The biased coin makes this data more likely to have occurred. MLE asks: which θ maximises L(θ)?

Likelihood as a Function of θ — Coin Example
Data: 7 Heads, 3 Tails. L(p) = p⁷(1-p)³. Which p is most likely?

4. Estimation vs Truth

An estimator θ̂ is a function of the data that produces a guess for the true parameter θ. It has two key properties:

Bias
E[θ̂] − θ. An unbiased estimator has E[θ̂] = θ on average. Bias is the systematic error in one direction.
Variance
How spread out different estimates are across different samples. Low variance = consistent estimates.
Mean Squared Error (MSE)
MSE = Bias² + Variance. The overall error metric. A biased but low-variance estimator can beat an unbiased high-variance one.

5. Logarithm Rules (for Log-Likelihood)

MLE almost always uses the log-likelihood ℓ(θ) = log L(θ) because products become sums and optimisation is easier. Essential rules:

Log Rules
log(ab) = log(a) + log(b)
log(aᵏ) = k·log(a)
log(∏ᵢ xᵢ) = Σᵢ log(xᵢ)
Since log is monotone increasing, argmax L(θ) = argmax log L(θ). Always use log-likelihood for computation.

✅ Ready to proceed! With distributions, moments, likelihood, estimator properties, and log rules in hand — you have everything needed to master Method of Moments and Maximum Likelihood Estimation.

Topic 1 of 2

Method of Moments

The oldest, most intuitive estimation technique: match what the distribution theoretically predicts with what you actually observe in your data.

🎯 Why Quants Need This

Method of Moments is often the first pass at parameter estimation — fast, algebraic, and analytically tractable. It gives closed-form estimates for many distributions, making it ideal for initialising more complex optimisations like MLE, and for understanding the structure of the estimation problem.

The Core Idea — Matching Moments

The key insight is beautifully simple. Every distribution has theoretical moments — formulas like "E[X] = μ" and "E[X²] = σ² + μ²" — that are functions of the unknown parameters. And from data, we can compute sample moments — the actual averages like (1/n)ΣXᵢ and (1/n)ΣXᵢ².

Method of Moments says: set the theoretical moments equal to the sample moments and solve for the parameters.

The Fingerprint Matching Analogy

Suppose a criminal left behind two fingerprint features: finger length and ridge count. Each criminal has unique known values for these features. Method of Moments is like measuring those two features at the crime scene, then finding the criminal whose known features match. We have k unknowns (parameters), so we match k equations (moments) to solve uniquely.

The Formal Procedure

Method of Moments Recipe
Step 1: Compute theoretical moments μₖ(θ) = E[Xᵏ; θ]
Step 2: Compute sample moments m̂ₖ = (1/n)Σᵢ Xᵢᵏ
Step 3: Set μₖ(θ) = m̂ₖ for k = 1, 2, ..., p
Step 4: Solve the system of equations for θ̂
Need p equations for p parameters. Use the first p moments: E[X], E[X²], ..., E[Xᵖ].
  1. Count the parameters

    How many unknowns θ₁, θ₂, ... does your distribution have? That's how many moment equations you need.

  2. Write theoretical moment formulas

    Derive E[X] = g₁(θ) and E[X²] = g₂(θ) etc. These are known functions of the parameters from the distribution's theory.

  3. Compute sample moments from data

    m̂₁ = (1/n)ΣXᵢ (sample mean), m̂₂ = (1/n)ΣXᵢ² (sample second moment), etc.

  4. Solve the system

    Set g₁(θ) = m̂₁ and g₂(θ) = m̂₂ and solve algebraically. The solutions are the MoM estimators θ̂.

Worked Example 1 — Normal Distribution

Data: x₁,...,xₙ ~ Normal(μ, σ²). Two parameters: μ and σ². Need two moment equations.

Normal MoM Derivation
Theoretical: E[X] = μ → set equal to m̂₁ = X̄
Theoretical: E[X²] = σ² + μ² → set equal to m̂₂ = (1/n)ΣXᵢ²

From equation 1: μ̂ = X̄ = (1/n)ΣXᵢ
Substitute into equation 2:
σ̂² + X̄² = (1/n)ΣXᵢ²
σ̂² = (1/n)ΣXᵢ² − X̄² = (1/n)Σ(Xᵢ − X̄)²
Result: μ̂ = sample mean, σ̂² = biased sample variance (divides by n, not n−1). MoM gives the biased version.

📌 Note: MoM Variance is Biased

The MoM estimator σ̂² = (1/n)Σ(Xᵢ−X̄)² has E[σ̂²] = ((n−1)/n)σ² ≠ σ². It's slightly biased downward. The unbiased version uses (n−1) in the denominator — this is why your calculator divides by n−1 for sample variance.

Worked Example 2 — Exponential Distribution

Data: x₁,...,xₙ ~ Exponential(λ). One parameter λ. Need only the first moment.

Exponential MoM
Theoretical: E[X] = 1/λ
Sample: m̂₁ = X̄ = (1/n)ΣXᵢ

Set equal: 1/λ = X̄
Solve: λ̂ = 1/X̄
The MoM estimator of the rate λ is the reciprocal of the sample mean. Intuitive: if average waiting time is 5 minutes, rate = 1/5 = 0.2 events per minute.

Worked Example 3 — Beta Distribution (two parameters)

Data: x₁,...,xₙ ~ Beta(α, β). Two parameters. First two moments:

Beta MoM
Theoretical: E[X] = α/(α+β) = m̂₁
Theoretical: Var[X] = αβ/((α+β)²(α+β+1)) = m̂₂ − m̂₁²

Let m = m̂₁, v = m̂₂ − m̂₁² (sample mean and variance)
α̂ = m·(m(1−m)/v − 1)
β̂ = (1−m)·(m(1−m)/v − 1)
Beta distribution used in Bayesian analysis for modelling probabilities. MoM gives explicit formulas — MLE does not have closed-form solutions for Beta.

🧪 Colab Experiment 1 — Method of Moments from Scratch

experiment_1_method_of_moments.py ▶ Open Colab 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy import stats

np.random.seed(42)

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# EXPERIMENT 1A: MoM for Normal Distribution
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
print("=== MoM for Normal Distribution ===")

# True parameters
mu_true, sigma_true = 3.5, 1.2
n = 200
data = np.random.normal(mu_true, sigma_true, n)

# Step 1: Compute sample moments
m1_hat = data.mean()          # (1/n) * sum(Xi)
m2_hat = (data**2).mean()    # (1/n) * sum(Xi^2)

# Step 2: Solve for parameters
mu_hat    = m1_hat                           # from E[X] = mu
sigma_sq_hat = m2_hat - m1_hat**2           # from E[X^2] = sigma^2 + mu^2
sigma_hat = np.sqrt(sigma_sq_hat)

print(f"True: μ={mu_true}, σ={sigma_true}")
print(f"MoM:  μ̂={mu_hat:.4f}, σ̂={sigma_hat:.4f}")
print(f"Note: MoM σ̂ divides by n (biased). Unbiased uses n-1: {data.std(ddof=1):.4f}")

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# EXPERIMENT 1B: MoM for Exponential Distribution
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
print("\n=== MoM for Exponential Distribution ===")

lambda_true = 0.5  # mean = 1/lambda = 2
data_exp = np.random.exponential(1/lambda_true, n)

m1_exp = data_exp.mean()
lambda_hat = 1 / m1_exp  # E[X] = 1/lambda => lambda = 1/X̄

print(f"True λ={lambda_true}, mean should be {1/lambda_true}")
print(f"Sample mean: {m1_exp:.4f}")
print(f"MoM λ̂ = 1/X̄ = {lambda_hat:.4f}")

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# EXPERIMENT 1C: Visualise MoM — Fitted vs True Distribution
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(15, 5))
distributions = [
    ('Normal',   np.random.normal(3.5, 1.2, 300),   stats.norm),
    ('Exponential', np.random.exponential(2, 300),  stats.expon),
    ('Gamma',    np.random.gamma(2, 1.5, 300),    stats.gamma),
]

for ax, (name, data_i, dist_cls) in zip(axes, distributions):
    # Plot histogram
    ax.hist(data_i, bins=35, density=True, alpha=0.55,
            color='#b94a1a', label='Data')

    # MoM fit
    m1 = data_i.mean()
    m2 = (data_i**2).mean()
    var = m2 - m1**2

    xs = np.linspace(data_i.min(), data_i.max(), 200)
    if name == 'Normal':
        ax.plot(xs, stats.norm.pdf(xs, m1, np.sqrt(var)),
                'b-', linewidth=2.5, label=f'MoM fit: μ={m1:.2f}, σ={np.sqrt(var):.2f}')
    elif name == 'Exponential':
        lam = 1/m1
        ax.plot(xs, stats.expon.pdf(xs, scale=1/lam),
                'b-', linewidth=2.5, label=f'MoM fit: λ={lam:.3f}')
    elif name == 'Gamma':
        alpha_hat = m1**2/var
        beta_hat  = var/m1
        ax.plot(xs, stats.gamma.pdf(xs, a=alpha_hat, scale=beta_hat),
                'b-', linewidth=2.5, label=f'MoM fit: α={alpha_hat:.2f}, β={beta_hat:.2f}')

    ax.set_title(f'MoM Fit: {name}', fontweight='bold')
    ax.legend(fontsize=9)

plt.suptitle('Method of Moments: Fitted Distributions vs Data', fontsize=14)
plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# EXPERIMENT 1D: Sampling Distribution of MoM Estimator
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
print("\n=== Sampling Distribution of MoM Estimator ===")
REPS = 2000
n_vals = [20, 100, 500]

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(14, 4))
for ax, n in zip(axes, n_vals):
    mom_ests = []
    for _ in range(REPS):
        d = np.random.exponential(2, n)
        mom_ests.append(1/d.mean())
    ax.hist(mom_ests, bins=50, density=True, color='#0e6b6b', alpha=0.7)
    ax.axvline(0.5, color='red', linewidth=2, linestyle='--', label='True λ=0.5')
    ax.axvline(np.mean(mom_ests), color='gold', linewidth=2, label=f'Mean λ̂={np.mean(mom_ests):.4f}')
    ax.set_title(f'n={n}: Bias={np.mean(mom_ests)-0.5:.4f}, SE={np.std(mom_ests):.4f}')
    ax.legend(fontsize=8)

plt.suptitle('Sampling Distribution of MoM λ̂ = 1/X̄ for Exp(0.5)', fontsize=13)

Topic 2 of 2

Maximum Likelihood

Find the parameter values that make your observed data as probable as possible. The gold standard of statistical estimation — efficient, principled, and universally applicable.

🔗 Builds on Prerequisites

MLE requires understanding the likelihood function from the prerequisites, plus logarithm rules (since we maximise log-likelihood) and basic calculus (taking derivatives and setting to zero to find maxima).

The Core Philosophy

You've observed data x₁,...,xₙ. The data has already happened — it's fixed. But we don't know which parameter θ generated it. MLE's answer: choose the θ that would have made this specific data most probable to occur.

The Archery Analogy

You walk into a room and see 10 arrows all clustered near the bullseye. You want to estimate where the archer was aiming. MLE says: "The archer must have been aiming at the bullseye — because that's the aim that makes the observed shot pattern most probable." The parameter (aim point) is chosen to maximise the probability of the observed evidence (arrow positions).

MoM would say: "Take the average position of the arrows and call that the aim." MLE is more principled — it uses the full shape of the likelihood surface.

The Formal Definition

MLE Definition
θ̂_MLE = argmax_θ L(θ; x) = argmax_θ ∏ᵢ f(xᵢ; θ)
= argmax_θ ℓ(θ; x) = argmax_θ Σᵢ log f(xᵢ; θ)
Since log is monotone, maximising the log-likelihood ℓ(θ) is equivalent to maximising L(θ). Always use log-likelihood in practice.

The Standard MLE Procedure

  1. Write the likelihood

    L(θ) = ∏ᵢ f(xᵢ; θ). For i.i.d. data this is the product of individual densities/probabilities evaluated at each observed xᵢ.

  2. Take the log → log-likelihood

    ℓ(θ) = Σᵢ log f(xᵢ; θ). Products become sums. Much easier to differentiate.

  3. Differentiate w.r.t. θ

    Compute ∂ℓ/∂θ. Set equal to zero: this is the "score equation" or "likelihood equation."

  4. Solve for θ

    If the score equation has an analytic solution, that's your MLE. Otherwise, use numerical optimisation (gradient ascent, Newton-Raphson, scipy.optimize).

  5. Verify it's a maximum

    Check ∂²ℓ/∂θ² < 0 (concave down). Or verify the solution is not a boundary or saddle point.

Worked Example 1 — Bernoulli / Binomial MLE

Observe n coin flips: k heads, n−k tails. X₁,...,Xₙ ~ Bernoulli(p). Find p̂_MLE.

Bernoulli MLE — Full Derivation
L(p) = pᵏ(1−p)ⁿ⁻ᵏ

ℓ(p) = k·log(p) + (n−k)·log(1−p)

∂ℓ/∂p = k/p − (n−k)/(1−p) = 0

k(1−p) = (n−k)p
k − kp = np − kp
k = np

p̂_MLE = k/n = (number of heads) / (total flips)
The MLE is just the sample proportion — intuitively obvious, but now formally justified as the principled maximum likelihood solution.

Worked Example 2 — Normal Distribution MLE

Observe x₁,...,xₙ ~ Normal(μ, σ²). Find μ̂_MLE and σ̂²_MLE.

Normal MLE Derivation
f(xᵢ; μ, σ²) = (1/√(2πσ²)) exp(−(xᵢ−μ)²/(2σ²))

ℓ(μ,σ²) = −(n/2)log(2π) − (n/2)log(σ²) − Σᵢ(xᵢ−μ)²/(2σ²)

∂ℓ/∂μ = Σᵢ(xᵢ−μ)/σ² = 0 → μ̂ = X̄

∂ℓ/∂σ² = −n/(2σ²) + Σᵢ(xᵢ−μ)²/(2σ⁴) = 0
→ σ̂² = (1/n)Σᵢ(xᵢ − X̄)²
MLE and MoM agree for Normal! Both give μ̂ = X̄ and σ̂² = (1/n)Σ(xᵢ−X̄)² (biased). This is a lucky coincidence — not universal.

Worked Example 3 — Exponential MLE

Exponential MLE
f(xᵢ; λ) = λ·exp(−λxᵢ) for xᵢ > 0

ℓ(λ) = n·log(λ) − λ·Σᵢ xᵢ

∂ℓ/∂λ = n/λ − Σᵢ xᵢ = 0

λ̂_MLE = n / Σᵢ xᵢ = 1/X̄
Same as MoM for Exponential! Again, a coincidence. For many distributions, MoM ≠ MLE.

The Information Matrix & Fisher Information

MLE comes with built-in uncertainty quantification via Fisher Information — a measure of how much information the data carries about θ.

Fisher Information
I(θ) = E[−∂²ℓ/∂θ² | θ] = E[(∂ℓ/∂θ)²]
Var(θ̂_MLE) ≈ 1/(n·I(θ)) [Cramér-Rao Lower Bound]
The MLE achieves the CRLB asymptotically — it is the most efficient unbiased estimator for large n. No estimator can have lower variance.

The Fisher Information Intuition

Fisher Information measures how "peaked" the log-likelihood is at θ. A sharply peaked likelihood → high information → small uncertainty → tight confidence intervals. A flat likelihood → low information → parameter is hard to pin down → wide confidence intervals.

Think of it as: "How sensitive is the log-likelihood to changes in θ near the true value?" High sensitivity = high information = better estimates.

Sharp Likelihood = Low Uncertainty
High Fisher Information → peaked → tight CI
Flat Likelihood = High Uncertainty
Low Fisher Information → flat → wide CI

Asymptotic Properties of MLE

Under mild regularity conditions, the MLE has three powerful asymptotic properties:

MLE Asymptotic Normality
√n · (θ̂_MLE − θ₀) → N(0, 1/I(θ₀)) as n → ∞
θ₀ = true parameter. This allows Wald confidence intervals: θ̂ ± z_{α/2}/√(n·Î(θ̂)).
PropertyStatementPractical Use
Consistencyθ̂_MLE → θ₀ as n → ∞Large samples converge to truth
Asymp. Normality√n(θ̂ − θ) → N(0, I⁻¹)Confidence intervals, hypothesis tests
EfficiencyVar(θ̂) → CRLB = 1/(nI)Best possible estimator for large n
InvarianceMLE of g(θ) is g(θ̂_MLE)Estimate transformations without re-fitting

📌 MLE Invariance Property — Very Useful!

If θ̂ is the MLE of θ, then g(θ̂) is the MLE of g(θ) for any function g. Example: if λ̂_MLE = 0.5 is the MLE of the rate, then 1/λ̂ = 2 is the MLE of the mean. You never need to re-solve the optimisation for transformed parameters.

Numerical MLE — When No Closed Form Exists

Many distributions (Beta, Gamma with both parameters, Weibull, etc.) have no closed-form MLE. We use numerical optimisation:

Numerical MLE Strategy
1. Define negative log-likelihood: nll(θ) = −Σᵢ log f(xᵢ; θ)
2. Minimise nll using scipy.optimize.minimize
3. Use gradient methods (L-BFGS-B) or trust-region
4. Hessian at solution → Fisher information → CIs

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy import stats, optimize
from scipy.stats import norm, expon, gamma

np.random.seed(0)

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# EXPERIMENT 2A: Visualise Likelihood Surface
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
n = 30
p_true = 0.35
data = np.random.binomial(1, p_true, n)
k = data.sum()

p_vals = np.linspace(0.001, 0.999, 300)
log_likelihood = k * np.log(p_vals) + (n-k) * np.log(1 - p_vals)
likelihood     = np.exp(log_likelihood - log_likelihood.max())  # normalise for plot

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(13, 5))
axes[0].plot(p_vals, likelihood, color='#b94a1a', linewidth=2.5, label='Likelihood (normalised)')
axes[0].axvline(k/n, color='navy', linestyle='--', linewidth=2, label=f'MLE: p̂={k/n:.3f}')
axes[0].axvline(p_true, color='green', linestyle=':', linewidth=2, label=f'True: p={p_true}')
axes[0].set_xlabel('p'); axes[0].set_title('Likelihood Function L(p) — Bernoulli')
axes[0].legend()

axes[1].plot(p_vals, log_likelihood, color='#0e6b6b', linewidth=2.5, label='Log-Likelihood ℓ(p)')
axes[1].axvline(k/n, color='navy', linestyle='--', linewidth=2, label=f'MLE: p̂={k/n:.3f}')
axes[1].set_xlabel('p'); axes[1].set_title('Log-Likelihood ℓ(p) — same maximum!')
axes[1].legend()
plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# EXPERIMENT 2B: Numerical MLE for Gamma Distribution
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
print("\n=== Numerical MLE for Gamma(α, β) ===")
alpha_true, beta_true = 3.0, 2.0
data_gamma = np.random.gamma(alpha_true, beta_true, 200)

def neg_log_lik_gamma(params):
    alpha, beta = params
    if alpha <= 0 or beta <= 0: return 1e10
    return -np.sum(stats.gamma.logpdf(data_gamma, a=alpha, scale=beta))

# Initial guess from MoM
m1, m2 = data_gamma.mean(), (data_gamma**2).mean()
var_hat = m2 - m1**2
alpha0 = m1**2/var_hat
beta0  = var_hat/m1
print(f"MoM initial guess: α₀={alpha0:.3f}, β₀={beta0:.3f}")

result = optimize.minimize(neg_log_lik_gamma, [alpha0, beta0],
                           method='L-BFGS-B', bounds=[(0.001,None),(0.001,None)])
alpha_mle, beta_mle = result.x
print(f"True:  α={alpha_true}, β={beta_true}")
print(f"MLE:   α̂={alpha_mle:.4f}, β̂={beta_mle:.4f}")
print(f"MLE converged: {result.success}, log-lik = {-result.fun:.2f}")

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# EXPERIMENT 2C: Asymptotic Normality of MLE
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
REPS = 3000
n_vals = [20, 100, 500]
p0 = 0.4

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(14, 4))
for ax, n in zip(axes, n_vals):
    mle_ests = [np.random.binomial(1, p0, n).mean() for _ in range(REPS)]
    mle_std = np.array(mle_ests).std()
    ax.hist(mle_ests, bins=40, density=True, alpha=0.65, color='#b94a1a')
    # Overlay N(p0, p0(1-p0)/n) — theoretical asymptotic distribution
    xs = np.linspace(p0-4/np.sqrt(n), p0+4/np.sqrt(n), 200)
    theo_std = np.sqrt(p0*(1-p0)/n)
    ax.plot(xs, norm.pdf(xs, p0, theo_std), 'k-', linewidth=2, label=f'N({p0}, I⁻¹/n)')
    ax.axvline(p0, color='green', linestyle='--', label=f'True p={p0}')
    ax.set_title(f'n={n}: MLE ~ N(p, p(1-p)/n)')
    ax.legend(fontsize=8)

plt.suptitle('MLE Asymptotic Normality: Bernoulli MLE Converges to Normal', fontsize=13)
plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# EXPERIMENT 2D: Likelihood Ratio Test
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
print("\n=== Likelihood Ratio Test: H0: p=0.5 vs H1: p≠0.5 ===")
data_lrt = np.random.binomial(1, 0.6, 50)  # true p=0.6
k_obs = data_lrt.sum()
n_obs = len(data_lrt)
p_null = 0.5
p_hat  = k_obs/n_obs

ll_null = k_obs*np.log(p_null) + (n_obs-k_obs)*np.log(1-p_null)
ll_alt  = k_obs*np.log(p_hat)  + (n_obs-k_obs)*np.log(1-p_hat)
LR_stat = 2 * (ll_alt - ll_null)  # ~ chi-squared(1) under H0
p_val = 1 - stats.chi2.cdf(LR_stat, df=1)

print(f"Observed k={k_obs} out of n={n_obs}, p̂={p_hat:.3f}")
print(f"LR statistic: {LR_stat:.4f} (chi2(1) under H0)")
print(f"p-value: {p_val:.4f}")
print(f"Reject H0: p=0.5? {'YES' if p_val < 0.05 else 'NO'}")

The Big Picture

MoM vs MLE

Two roads to the same destination — but they take very different routes. Knowing when to use each is the mark of a practised statistician.

Side-by-Side Comparison

Method of Moments

Often has closed-form analytical solution

Algebraic — no optimisation needed

Sometimes biased (e.g., variance estimate)

Consistent (converges to truth as n→∞)

Usually less efficient than MLE

Excellent as initialiser for numerical MLE

May give invalid parameter values

Does not use full distributional information

Maximum Likelihood

Sometimes closed-form; often requires numerical optimisation

Principled — maximises data probability

Sometimes biased (same variance for Normal)

Consistent + asymptotically efficient

Achieves Cramér-Rao bound asymptotically

Natural framework for hypothesis testing (LRT)

Built-in uncertainty via Fisher information

Uses full distributional structure

When Do They Agree?

For some distributions, MoM and MLE produce exactly the same estimator — a lucky coincidence.

DistributionMoM EstimatorMLE EstimatorSame?
Normal(μ, σ²)μ̂=X̄, σ̂²=(1/n)Σ(Xᵢ−X̄)²μ̂=X̄, σ̂²=(1/n)Σ(Xᵢ−X̄)²✓ Yes
Exponential(λ)λ̂=1/X̄λ̂=1/X̄✓ Yes
Poisson(λ)λ̂=X̄λ̂=X̄✓ Yes

Uniform(0, θ)θ̂=2X̄θ̂=max(X₁,...,Xₙ)✗ Very different!

The Uniform(0,θ) Case — Dramatic Difference

MoM: θ̂ = 2X̄. Uses only the mean, doesn't use the fact that all observations must be ≤ θ.

MLE: θ̂ = max(X₁,...,Xₙ). Logically perfect — the largest observation is the best lower bound for θ. Any θ below the max would make those observations impossible!

MLE is dramatically better here: it's consistent, uses data more cleverly, and has smaller error. MoM θ̂ = 2X̄ could actually be SMALLER than the largest observation — a clear impossibility for the true θ.

📌 Asymptotic Relative Efficiency (ARE)

ARE(MoM, MLE) = Var(θ̂_MLE) / Var(θ̂_MoM). If ARE = 0.80, MoM needs 25% more data to achieve the same precision as MLE. For many distributions, ARE is close to 1 — but for some (like the uniform) the gap is enormous.

Real-World Impact

Quant Applications

MoM and MLE are the engines behind volatility estimation, risk modelling, derivative pricing calibration, and factor model fitting.

1. Volatility Estimation (Normal MLE)

Under Black-Scholes, log-returns rₜ ~ Normal(μ, σ²). MLE gives the standard formula for historical volatility:

Historical Volatility MLE
σ̂²_daily = (1/n) Σᵢ (rᵢ − r̄)²
σ̂_annual = σ̂_daily × √252
This is the textbook volatility estimator used by every trading desk. It IS the MLE. The choice of n (lookback window) is a model choice, not an estimation choice.

2. Fat-Tail Modelling — Student-t MLE

Market returns have heavier tails than Normal. The Student-t distribution with ν degrees of freedom is fitted via numerical MLE:

Student-t Log-Likelihood
ℓ(ν,μ,σ) = n·[log Γ((ν+1)/2) − log Γ(ν/2) − log(√(νπ)σ)]
− ((ν+1)/2) Σᵢ log(1 + (xᵢ−μ)²/(νσ²))
No closed form for ν (degrees of freedom). MoM provides initial guess; numerical MLE refines it. Lower ν = heavier tails = more crisis risk.

3. Implied Volatility — MLE in Reverse

In options markets, we observe market prices of options and "invert" the Black-Scholes formula to find the implied volatility σ_IV. This is a single-parameter MLE problem:

Implied Vol as MLE
σ̂_IV = argmin_σ Σᵢ (C_market(i) − C_BS(σ; Sᵢ,Kᵢ,rᵢ,Tᵢ))²
Minimising squared pricing errors is equivalent to MLE under Gaussian errors. Solved by numerical methods (Newton-Raphson on vega).

4. Credit Risk — Fitting Loss Distributions

Banks fit loss distributions (Gamma, Log-Normal, or Beta) to historical loan default rates using MLE, then compute regulatory capital requirements (VaR at 99.9%).

5. Factor Models — Linear Regression as MLE

Under Gaussian errors, OLS (Ordinary Least Squares) regression is identical to MLE. The factor loadings β in the CAPM/APT model are found by MLE:

Linear Regression = Gaussian MLE
rᵢ = α + βᵢ·rₘ + εᵢ, εᵢ ~ N(0, σ²)
β̂_MLE = β̂_OLS = (XᵀX)⁻¹Xᵀy
This is why OLS works: it is MLE under Gaussian errors. Gauss-Markov theorem guarantees BLUE (Best Linear Unbiased Estimator).

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy import stats, optimize
import warnings; warnings.filterwarnings('ignore')

np.random.seed(42)

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# EXPERIMENT 3A: Fit Normal vs Student-t to Returns
#   Compare: which distribution is a better fit?
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════

# Simulate "realistic" stock returns with fat tails
nu_true = 5  # 5 degrees of freedom — heavy tails
mu_true, sigma_true = 0.0005, 0.015
n = 252  # one year of daily returns
returns = mu_true + sigma_true * stats.t.rvs(df=nu_true, size=n)

# Fit 1: Normal MLE (closed form)
mu_norm = returns.mean()
sigma_norm = returns.std(ddof=1)
ll_norm = stats.norm.logpdf(returns, mu_norm, sigma_norm).sum()
print(f"Normal MLE: μ={mu_norm:.5f}, σ={sigma_norm:.5f}")
print(f"Normal log-likelihood: {ll_norm:.2f}")

# Fit 2: Student-t MLE (numerical)
def neg_ll_t(params):
    nu, mu, sigma = params
    if nu <= 2 or sigma <= 0: return 1e10
    return -stats.t.logpdf(returns, df=nu, loc=mu, scale=sigma).sum()

res = optimize.minimize(neg_ll_t, [10, mu_norm, sigma_norm],
                         method='L-BFGS-B',
                         bounds=[(2.001,None),(None,None),(0.001,None)])
nu_mle, mu_mle, sigma_mle = res.x
ll_t = -res.fun
print(f"\nStudent-t MLE: ν={nu_mle:.2f}, μ={mu_mle:.5f}, σ={sigma_mle:.5f}")
print(f"Student-t log-likelihood: {ll_t:.2f}")
print(f"LR test stat: {2*(ll_t - ll_norm):.2f} (df=1, critical=3.84)")
print(f"Student-t significantly better? {2*(ll_t - ll_norm) > 3.84}")

# Plot
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(13, 5))
xs = np.linspace(returns.min(), returns.max(), 200)
axes[0].hist(returns, bins=40, density=True, alpha=0.5, label='Data', color='gray')
axes[0].plot(xs, stats.norm.pdf(xs, mu_norm, sigma_norm),
        'r-', linewidth=2.5, label=f'Normal MLE')
axes[0].plot(xs, stats.t.pdf(xs, nu_mle, mu_mle, sigma_mle),
        'b-', linewidth=2.5, label=f'Student-t MLE (ν={nu_mle:.1f})')
axes[0].legend(); axes[0].set_title('Normal vs Student-t MLE Fit')

# QQ plot: Normal vs t-distribution fit
(osm, osr), (slope, intercept, _) = stats.probplot(returns, dist='norm')
axes[1].plot(osm, osr, 'ro', alpha=0.4, ms=4, label='vs Normal')
axes[1].plot(osm, slope*np.array(osm)+intercept, 'r-', linewidth=2)
axes[1].set_title('QQ Plot: Heavy tails visible as deviations at extremes')
axes[1].set_xlabel('Theoretical Normal Quantiles')
plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# EXPERIMENT 3B: 99% VaR — Normal vs t-distribution
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
print("\n=== Risk Implications: VaR Comparison ===")
portfolio_value = 1_000_000
VaR_norm = portfolio_value * stats.norm.ppf(0.01, mu_norm, sigma_norm) * (-1)
VaR_t    = portfolio_value * stats.t.ppf(0.01, nu_mle, mu_mle, sigma_mle) * (-1)
print(f"Normal model 1-day 99% VaR: ${VaR_norm:,.0f}")
print(f"Student-t model 1-day 99% VaR: ${VaR_t:,.0f}")
print(f"t-model VaR is {(VaR_t/VaR_norm-1)*100:.1f}% larger")
print(f"→ Underestimating tail risk by using Normal!")

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
# EXPERIMENT 3C: Implied Volatility via MLE / Root-Finding
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════════════
from scipy.stats import norm as N

def bs_call(S, K, r, T, sigma):
    d1 = (np.log(S/K) + (r + 0.5*sigma**2)*T) / (sigma*np.sqrt(T))
    d2 = d1 - sigma*np.sqrt(T)
    return S*N.cdf(d1) - K*np.exp(-r*T)*N.cdf(d2)

# Market prices and option specs
S, K, r, T = 100, 105, 0.05, 0.25
C_market = 3.80  # observed market price
# Find implied vol: which sigma makes BS price = market price
def iv_target(sigma): return bs_call(S, K, r, T, sigma) - C_market

sigma_iv = optimize.brentq(iv_target, 0.001, 5.0)
print(f"\n=== Implied Volatility ===")
print(f"Market price: ${C_market:.2f}")
print(f"Implied Vol:  {sigma_iv:.4f} ({sigma_iv*100:.2f}%)")
print(f"Verify BS({sigma_iv:.4f}) = ${bs_call(S,K,r,T,sigma_iv):.4f}")

The Deepest Insight: MoM and MLE are not just estimation methods — they are philosophies for connecting theory to data. MoM says "match the summaries." MLE says "find the world that makes your data most likely." Both are valid. Together they give you a complete toolkit for building the statistical backbone of every model you will ever use as a quant engineer. The skill is not in memorising formulas — it is in understanding when to use which, what their limitations are, and how to interpret the uncertainty they reveal.
