P1 · Population vs Sample

🌍 Population

The entire group you care about. Every single element. In quant finance, think: all daily returns of a stock since it was listed.

You almost never have access to the full population. It's either too expensive, too large, or impossible to collect.

🔬 Sample

A subset of the population that you actually observe. You use the sample to infer things about the population.

Example: 90 days of return data to understand how a strategy behaves.

💡
Quant Finance Connection
When you backtest a strategy over 2 years, those 2 years are your sample. You're trying to infer how the strategy would perform across all possible market regimes — the population. This is why backtesting has limitations!

P2 · Mean, Variance, Standard Deviation

Mean (μ or x̄)

The average. Sum all values, divide by count. The "center" or "expected value" of your data.

Population Mean μ = (1/N) · Σ xᵢ
Sample Mean x̄ = (1/n) · Σ xᵢ

Variance (σ² or s²)

How far, on average, each data point deviates from the mean. Squared so negatives don't cancel out positives.

Sample Variance s² = (1/(n−1)) · Σ (xᵢ − x̄)²

Why (n−1) and not n? Called Bessel's correction — corrects for the fact that we're estimating from a sample, not the full population.

Standard Deviation (σ or s)

Square root of variance. Same units as your data. In finance: volatility is just standard deviation of returns.

s = √s²
Standard Deviation Visualized

P3 · The Normal Distribution

The bell curve. When you collect enough random data, it often follows this shape. Defined by just two numbers: mean (μ) and standard deviation (σ).

Normal Distribution — The Bell Curve

The 68-95-99.7 Rule

68% of data falls within 1σ of the mean

95% of data falls within 2σ of the mean

99.7% of data falls within 3σ of the mean

Why It Matters for Quants

Asset returns are often approximately normal (though not perfectly). Most statistical tests assume normality. Understanding the normal distribution is non-negotiable.

P4 · Central Limit Theorem (CLT)

✨
The Most Important Theorem in Statistics
CLT says: If you take many samples from any distribution and calculate the mean of each sample, those sample means will follow a Normal distribution — regardless of what the original distribution looks like. This is why the normal distribution is everywhere.

CLT In Plain English

Imagine rolling a die 1000 times. Each roll is uniform (1–6, not normal). But if you take many groups of 30 rolls and average each group, those averages form a bell curve. This means our statistical tests work even when data isn't perfectly normal, as long as we have enough samples.

Standard Error (SE)

The CLT also tells us how spread out sample means are:

SE = σ / √n

Larger sample → smaller SE → more precise estimate. This is why more data = better inference.

Topic 01 · Hypotheses

Null & Alternative Hypotheses

Before you can test anything statistically, you need to formalize what you're testing. Hypotheses are the foundation of every statistical test you'll ever run.

The Core Idea — Scientific Skepticism

🔬
The Fundamental Logic
Statistics doesn't prove things. It measures how surprised we should be by our data, assuming nothing interesting is happening. We start by assuming there's no effect (boring world), collect data, and ask: "How likely is this data in the boring world?" If it's very unlikely → we have evidence something IS happening.

Null Hypothesis (H₀)

H₀ — The "Nothing Interesting" Hypothesis

The null hypothesis is the default position — the claim that there is no effect, no difference, no relationship. It is what you assume to be true until evidence convinces you otherwise.

Think of it like a court of law: the accused is innocent until proven guilty. H₀ is "innocent."

Examples of H₀

ScenarioNull Hypothesis (H₀)
Does a new drug lower blood pressure?"The drug has NO effect on blood pressure"
Is our trading strategy profitable?"The strategy's mean return = 0 (no alpha)"
Does company size affect returns?"Company size has NO relationship with returns"
Is this coin fair?"P(heads) = 0.5"

Alternative Hypothesis (H₁ or Hₐ)

H₁ — The "Something Interesting" Hypothesis

This is what you believe might be true — the claim you're trying to find evidence for. If data strongly contradicts H₀, you "reject H₀ in favor of H₁."

Examples of H₁

ScenarioAlternative Hypothesis (H₁)
Does a new drug lower blood pressure?"The drug DOES lower blood pressure" (one-tailed)
Is our trading strategy profitable?"Mean return > 0 (positive alpha)" (one-tailed)
Does company size affect returns?"Company size IS related to returns" (two-tailed)
Is this coin fair?"P(heads) ≠ 0.5" (two-tailed)

One-Tailed vs Two-Tailed Tests

Two-Tailed Test ≠

H₁ says the parameter is different from H₀ — either larger OR smaller.

Use when you have no prior reason to expect direction.

Example: H₀: μ = 0  →  H₁: μ ≠ 0

One-Tailed Test > or <

H₁ says the parameter is larger OR smaller — you already expect a direction.

Use when theory or prior evidence gives you direction.

Example: H₀: μ = 0  →  H₁: μ > 0

One-Tailed vs Two-Tailed Rejection Regions

Significance Level (α) — The Decision Threshold

Alpha (α) — How Much Doubt is Enough?

Before collecting data, you choose how strong the evidence needs to be to reject H₀. This is α (alpha), the significance level.

Common choices:

α = 0.05 → "I'll only reject H₀ if there's less than 5% chance this data happens when H₀ is true."

α = 0.01 → More strict. Used in medical/safety-critical decisions.

α = 0.10 → More lenient. Sometimes used in exploratory quant research.

⚠️
Critical Rule
You MUST choose α before collecting data. Choosing it after you see results is called p-hacking — a form of statistical fraud common in bad research.

Topic 02 · p-Values

p-Values — The Most Misunderstood Number in Science

The p-value is widely used and widely misunderstood. Once you truly understand what it says — and what it doesn't — you'll be ahead of most practitioners.

What Exactly IS a p-Value?

🎯
The Precise Definition
The p-value is the probability of observing data at least as extreme as what you actually observed, assuming the null hypothesis is true.

p-value = P(data this extreme | H₀ is true)

The Coin Flip Analogy

Step-by-Step Intuition

Scenario: You suspect a coin is biased toward heads. You flip it 20 times and get 16 heads.

H₀: The coin is fair — P(heads) = 0.5

H₁: The coin is biased — P(heads) > 0.5

Question: If H₀ is true (fair coin), how likely is it to get 16 or more heads out of 20?

Answer: About 0.6% → p ≈ 0.006

Interpretation: If the coin were fair, seeing 16+ heads would happen only 0.6% of the time. That's very unlikely. We reject H₀ at α = 0.05.

What p-Value IS and IS NOT

✅ p-value IS...

The probability your data (or more extreme) would occur if H₀ were true.

A measure of how surprising your data is under H₀.

A basis for a binary decision: reject or not reject H₀.

❌ p-value is NOT...

The probability that H₀ is true.

The probability that H₁ is true.

The probability you made an error.

A measure of the size or importance of an effect.

🚨
Common Misconception to Burn Into Memory
"p = 0.03" does NOT mean "there's a 3% chance H₀ is true." It means "if H₀ WERE true, 3% of the time we'd see data this extreme." These are completely different statements. Confusing them is one of the most common errors in published research.

The Test Statistic — How We Calculate p

Test Statistics Turn Data Into a p-Value

A test statistic measures how far your sample result is from the H₀ value, in units of standard error. Common ones:

Test StatisticWhen to UseFormula
z-scoreLarge samples (n > 30), σ knownz = (x̄ − μ₀) / (σ/√n)
t-statisticSmall samples, σ unknownt = (x̄ − μ₀) / (s/√n)
chi-square (χ²)Categorical dataΣ (O−E)²/E
F-statisticComparing variances/multiple groupsvariance between / variance within

Once you have the test statistic, you look up (or compute) how extreme it is on the relevant distribution — that probability is your p-value.

Significance vs Practical Importance

⚠️
Statistical Significance ≠ Practical Significance
With a huge sample (n = 1,000,000), even a tiny, meaningless effect (return of 0.0001%) can give p < 0.001. Always ask: "Is the effect size actually meaningful for my use case?" In quant finance, a statistically significant alpha might be economically insignificant after transaction costs.

Topic 03 · Confidence Intervals

Confidence Intervals

Instead of a single number estimate, a CI gives you a range. It's more informative and more honest about uncertainty than a point estimate alone.

The Problem with Point Estimates

Why a Single Number Isn't Enough

Say you backtest a strategy and the mean daily return is 0.05%. Is that reliable? You've only seen one sample from a universe of possible markets. If you repeated the test on different 90-day windows, you'd get different numbers. How much would they vary?

A confidence interval acknowledges this: instead of "the return is 0.05%", it says "the return is between 0.01% and 0.09% — and I'm 95% confident this range contains the true value."

The Precise Definition (Read Carefully)

📐
What "95% Confident" Actually Means
A 95% CI does NOT mean "there's a 95% chance the true parameter is in this interval." The true parameter is fixed — it's either in there or it isn't.

What it DOES mean: If you repeated this experiment many times and built a CI from each sample, 95% of those intervals would contain the true parameter. The 95% refers to the long-run performance of the procedure, not one specific interval.

The Formula

95% Confidence Interval for a Mean

Confidence Interval CI = x̄ ± z* · (s / √n)
ComponentMeaning
x̄Sample mean — your best single estimate
z*Critical value (1.96 for 95%, 2.576 for 99%)
sSample standard deviation
√nSquare root of sample size
s / √nStandard Error (SE) — uncertainty in x̄
z* · SEMargin of Error
💡
Where does 1.96 come from?
On a standard normal distribution, 95% of the area falls between -1.96 and +1.96. So if we go 1.96 standard errors in each direction, we capture the middle 95% of sampling outcomes.
Visualizing the Confidence Interval Construction

What Affects the Width of a CI?

📈 Wider CI (Less Precise) When...

• Higher confidence level (99% vs 95%)

• Smaller sample size (n is small)

• Higher variance in data (σ is large)

📉 Narrower CI (More Precise) When...

• Lower confidence level (90% vs 95%)

• Larger sample size (n is large)

• Lower variance in data (σ is small)

The CI—Hypothesis Test Connection

🔗
Key Link — CIs and p-Values are Two Sides of the Same Coin
A 95% CI corresponds exactly to a two-tailed hypothesis test at α = 0.05.

Rule: If the H₀ value (e.g., 0 for "no effect") falls outside the 95% CI → you would reject H₀ at α = 0.05 (p < 0.05).
If it falls inside the CI → you fail to reject H₀ (p > 0.05).

CIs are often preferred because they show you both the decision AND the effect size range.

CIs in Quant Finance

Practical Usage

Sharpe Ratio CI: "Our strategy has Sharpe 1.2, but the 95% CI is [0.4, 2.0]. We're not confident it's above 0 yet." (Wide CI → need more data)

Mean Return CI: If the 95% CI for daily return is [−0.1%, +0.2%] — it includes 0, so we can't confidently say the strategy is profitable.

Beta Estimation: Reporting a stock's beta as 1.3 ± 0.15 (CI) is more honest than just 1.3.

Topic 04 · Decision Errors

Type I & Type II Errors

Every statistical decision can be wrong in two different ways. Understanding these errors — and the tradeoff between them — is critical for any quantitative practitioner.

The Reality of Statistical Decisions

Two Worlds, Two Possible Truths

When you run a hypothesis test, one of two things is true in reality: either H₀ is true, or H₁ is true. And you make one of two decisions: either reject H₀ or don't. This gives us a 2×2 table:

H₀ Is Actually TRUE H₀ Is Actually FALSE (H₁ true)
You REJECT H₀ ❌ Type I Error (False Positive)
Probability = α		 ✅ Correct (True Positive)
Probability = Power = 1−β
You FAIL TO reject H₀ ✅ Correct (True Negative)
Probability = 1−α		 ❌ Type II Error (False Negative)
Probability = β

🔴 Type I Error (α) — False Positive

What it is: You reject H₀ when H₀ is actually true. You find a signal when there isn't one.

Controlled by: Your chosen significance level α. If α = 0.05, you have a 5% chance of Type I error when H₀ is true.

Finance example: You think your strategy has alpha, but it's actually random luck. You deploy capital based on a fluke.

Medical analogy: A healthy patient tests positive for a disease they don't have.

🟡 Type II Error (β) — False Negative

What it is: You fail to reject H₀ when H₁ is actually true. You miss a real signal.

Controlled by: β (beta). Not directly set — depends on sample size, effect size, and α.

Finance example: A genuinely profitable strategy looks like noise in your test, so you pass on it.

Medical analogy: A sick patient gets a negative test — the disease goes undetected.

Statistical Power = 1 − β

⚡
Power — The Ability to Detect Real Effects
Power = 1 − β = the probability of correctly rejecting H₀ when H₁ is true.

High power = low chance of missing real effects. We want power ≥ 0.80 (i.e., β ≤ 0.20) in most research.

Power is increased by: larger sample size, larger effect size (easier to detect), and higher α (less strict threshold). But higher α increases Type I errors — there's a tradeoff!
Type I vs Type II Error — Overlapping Distributions

The Fundamental Tradeoff

You Cannot Simultaneously Minimize Both Errors

Think about this: to reduce Type I errors, you make your test stricter (lower α, e.g. 0.01). But this means the critical value moves further from H₀ → the H₁ curve has even more of its area on the "fail to reject" side → Type II errors increase.

The Tradeoff Visualized

How to Choose α in Practice

ContextTypical αReasoning
Drug safety / medical0.001 – 0.01False positive could harm people
Academic research0.05Convention; balance of errors
Exploratory quant research0.10 – 0.20Cost of missing signals is high; want broad screen
Production algo decisions0.01 – 0.05False signal → real capital loss

Sample Size & Power Analysis

How Much Data Do You Need?

Before running an experiment, use power analysis to determine the sample size that gives you sufficient power (usually 80%) to detect an effect of a given size at your chosen α.

Approximate Sample Size (two-sample t-test) n ≈ 2 · ((z_α + z_β)² · σ²) / δ²

Where δ is the minimum effect size you care about detecting, σ is standard deviation, z_α is z-score for α, z_β for β (power).

Rule of thumb: Smaller effect size → need more data. This is why detecting small alpha in finance requires years of data.

Topic 05 · Synthesis

How It All Connects

The real power comes from seeing all four topics as one unified framework. Here's the complete picture.

The Complete Statistical Inference Framework

Full Relationship Map

The Unified Summary

In One Paragraph

You start by stating your Null Hypothesis (H₀) and Alternative Hypothesis (H₁) — what you assume vs. what you suspect. You choose a significance level α that sets your tolerance for false positives (Type I errors). Then you collect data and compute a test statistic, which is converted to a p-value — the probability of seeing your data (or more extreme) under H₀. If p < α, you reject H₀. A Confidence Interval gives you the same decision plus the range of plausible values for the true parameter. Throughout this process, you risk two types of mistakes: Type I (rejecting a true H₀, prob = α) and Type II (failing to reject a false H₀, prob = β). These tradeoff against each other, and increasing sample size reduces both.

Common Misconceptions — Master These

❌ "p < 0.05 means the result is true"

No. It means the data is unlikely under H₀. With many tests (multiple comparisons), some will be < 0.05 by chance alone.

❌ "p = 0.06 means no effect"

No. It just means you didn't reach the 0.05 threshold. The effect may still be real but you need more data (power issue).

❌ "95% CI means 95% chance the true value is inside"

No. The parameter is fixed. The 95% refers to the procedure's long-run coverage probability across repeated samples.

❌ "Significant = important"

No. With large n, trivial effects become statistically significant. Always report effect sizes alongside p-values.

Experiment 1 — CLT in Action

Watch the Central Limit Theorem happen live: sample from a skewed distribution, see the bell curve emerge.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy import stats

np.random.seed(42)

# Exponential distribution (very skewed — NOT normal!)
population = np.random.exponential(scale=2, size=100_000)

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 4, figsize=(16, 4))
sample_sizes = [1, 5, 30, 100]

for ax, n in zip(axes, sample_sizes):
    # Take 2000 samples of size n, compute mean of each
    means = [np.mean(np.random.choice(population, n)) for _ in range(2000)]
    ax.hist(means, bins=40, density=True, color='#00d4ff', alpha=0.6, edgecolor='none')

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
# Notice: at n=1, it's skewed. At n=30, it's almost normal!

Experiment 2 — Hypothesis Test from Scratch

Test whether a simulated trading strategy has a mean return greater than 0 (one-sided t-test).

import numpy as np
from scipy import stats

np.random.seed(7)

# Simulate 60 days of strategy returns (mean=0.04%, slightly positive)
n = 60
true_mean = 0.04   # 0.04% per day
true_std  = 1.2    # 1.2% daily vol — realistic for a stock strategy
returns   = np.random.normal(true_mean, true_std, n)

# H0: mu = 0 (no alpha)     H1: mu > 0 (positive alpha)
alpha = 0.05

# Manual computation
x_bar = np.mean(returns)
s     = np.std(returns, ddof=1)    # sample std (n-1)
se    = s / np.sqrt(n)
t_stat = (x_bar - 0) / se          # test statistic under H0
p_val  = 1 - stats.t.cdf(t_stat, df=n-1)  # one-tailed

print(f"Sample mean:    {x_bar:.4f}%")
print(f"Std dev:        {s:.4f}%")
print(f"Standard error: {se:.4f}%")
print(f"t-statistic:    {t_stat:.4f}")
print(f"p-value:        {p_val:.4f}")
print(f"\nDecision: {'REJECT H0 — evidence of positive alpha!' if p_val < alpha else 'Fail to reject H0 — no significant alpha'}")

# Verify using scipy
t2, p2 = stats.ttest_1samp(returns, popmean=0, alternative='greater')
print(f"\nSciPy confirms — t: {t2:.4f}, p: {p2:.4f}")

Experiment 3 — Build & Visualize Confidence Intervals

Simulate repeated sampling and visually confirm that 95% of CIs capture the true mean.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy import stats

np.random.seed(0)
TRUE_MEAN = 5.0
TRUE_STD  = 2.0
n          = 30
n_experiments = 100
confidence    = 0.95

captured = 0
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(14, 8))

for i in range(n_experiments):
    sample  = np.random.normal(TRUE_MEAN, TRUE_STD, n)
    x_bar   = np.mean(sample)
    se      = stats.sem(sample)
    ci      = stats.t.interval(confidence, df=n-1, loc=x_bar, scale=se)
    covers  = ci[0] <= TRUE_MEAN <= ci[1]
    if covers: captured += 1
    color = '#10b981' if covers else '#ef4444'
    ax.plot([ci[0], ci[1]], [i, i], color=color, alpha=0.6, linewidth=1.4)
    ax.plot(x_bar, i, 'o', color=color, markersize=3)

ax.axvline(TRUE_MEAN, color='#f59e0b', linewidth=2, label=f'True mean = {TRUE_MEAN}')
ax.set_title(f'{n_experiments} x 95% CIs — {captured} capture the true mean ({captured}%)')
ax.legend()
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
print(f"Coverage: {captured}/{n_experiments} = {100*captured/n_experiments:.1f}% (expect ~95%)")

Experiment 4 — Type I Error Rate Simulation

Empirically verify: if you run 1000 tests when H₀ is always true, about 5% reject at α=0.05.

import numpy as np
from scipy import stats
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

np.random.seed(1)
N_TESTS  = 1000
n         = 50
alpha     = 0.05
p_values  = []

for _ in range(N_TESTS):
    # H0 IS TRUE — both groups have identical mean (5.0)
    group_A = np.random.normal(5.0, 2.0, n)
    group_B = np.random.normal(5.0, 2.0, n)
    _, p    = stats.ttest_ind(group_A, group_B)
    p_values.append(p)

type_I_rate = np.mean(np.array(p_values) < alpha)
print(f"Type I Error Rate: {type_I_rate:.3f}  (expected: {alpha})")

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 4))
plt.hist(p_values, bins=50, edgecolor='none', color='#00d4ff', alpha=0.7)
plt.axvline(0.05, color='#ef4444', linewidth=2, label='α = 0.05')
plt.xlabel('p-value')
plt.title('p-value distribution when H₀ is TRUE (uniform — as expected)')
plt.legend()
plt.show()
# p-values should be uniform [0,1] when H0 is true — that's by design!

Experiment 5 — Power Analysis

Find the minimum sample size to detect a given effect with 80% power.

import numpy as np
from scipy import stats
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Quant context: detect if daily return > 0 given volatility
true_mean    = 0.05   # 0.05% per day — small but real alpha
true_std     = 1.0    # 1% daily vol
alpha_thresh = 0.05
target_power = 0.80

sample_sizes = range(50, 5000, 50)
powers       = []

for n in sample_sizes:
    se         = true_std / np.sqrt(n)
    t_crit     = stats.t.ppf(1 - alpha_thresh, df=n-1)
    ncp        = true_mean / se   # non-centrality parameter
    power      = 1 - stats.t.cdf(t_crit, df=n-1, loc=ncp)
    powers.append(power)

n_needed = sample_sizes[np.argmax(np.array(powers) >= target_power)]
print(f"To detect {true_mean}% return with 80% power at α={alpha_thresh}:")
print(f"You need approximately {n_needed} trading days ≈ {n_needed/252:.1f} years of data!")

plt.figure(figsize=(10, 5))
plt.plot(list(sample_sizes), powers, color='#00d4ff', linewidth=2)
plt.axhline(0.8, color='#10b981', linestyle='--', label='80% Power Target')
plt.axvline(n_needed, color='#ef4444', linestyle='--', label=f'n={n_needed}')
plt.xlabel('Sample Size (trading days)')
plt.ylabel('Statistical Power')
plt.title('Power vs Sample Size — Detecting Small Daily Alpha')
plt.legend()
plt.grid(True, alpha=0.2)
plt.show()

A pharmaceutical company tests whether a new painkiller reduces headache duration. State the null and alternative hypotheses in words and in symbols (let μ = mean duration, μ₀ = current mean duration).
H-E2
A quant analyst wants to test if a moving average crossover strategy has positive expected daily returns. Write H₀ and H₁. Is this one-tailed or two-tailed? Explain why.
H-E3
State whether each is a valid null hypothesis and explain: (a) "Stock X will go up tomorrow." (b) "The mean return of Strategy A equals 0." (c) "Strategy A is better than Strategy B."
H-E4
Why do we assume H₀ is true at the start? Why don't we just directly test if H₁ is true? (Hint: think about the legal analogy.)
🟡 MEDIUM — Applied Problems ▼
H-M1
You're testing two strategies: A (mean return 0.08%, std 1%) and B (mean return 0.05%, std 1.2%), both over n=120 days. Set up formal hypotheses to test if Strategy A has higher mean return than B. What test would you use and what are your assumptions?
H-M2
A colleague says: "I tested 50 technical indicators and found 3 are significant at α=0.05. This proves they work." What is the fundamental problem with this reasoning? How many significant results would you expect by chance?
H-M3
You're testing whether volatility on Mondays differs from other days. Should you use a one-tailed or two-tailed test? Write out both hypotheses and justify your choice of tail(s).
🔴 HARD — Quant-Level Challenges ▼

Topic: p-Values

🟢 EASY — Core Concepts ▼
P-E1
A test gives p = 0.03. With α = 0.05, do you reject H₀? What about α = 0.01? What changes between the two decisions?
P-E2
Mark each statement TRUE or FALSE and explain: (a) p = 0.04 means there's a 4% chance H₀ is true. (b) p = 0.04 means there's a 4% chance you made an error. (c) p = 0.04 means if H₀ is true, you'd see data this extreme 4% of the time.
P-E3
You flip a fair coin 10 times and get 9 heads. Qualitatively (no calculation needed): would you expect the p-value to be small or large? Would you reject H₀: p(heads) = 0.5 at α = 0.05?
P-E4
If the null hypothesis is always true, what distribution do p-values follow? Why? Draw or describe the shape.
🟡 MEDIUM — Calculations & Interpretation ▼
P-M1
A strategy has 252 days of returns: x̄ = 0.06%, s = 1.5%. Compute the t-statistic and p-value for H₀: μ = 0 (two-sided). Then compute for H₀: μ = 0, H₁: μ > 0 (one-sided). Why does the one-sided p-value differ? Code this in Python.
P-M2
Two researchers both run the same experiment. Researcher A gets p = 0.049 and publishes "significant result." Researcher B gets p = 0.051 and says "no effect found." Critique this binary thinking. What would be a better way to communicate the results?
P-M3
You run 20 independent backtests. 2 show p < 0.05. Calculate the probability that both are false positives assuming H₀ is true for all. What does this tell you about your backtest results?
🔴 HARD — Advanced Analysis ▼
P-H1
The "p-hacking" problem: explain mathematically why searching through 20 features/parameters and reporting only the one with lowest p-value inflates Type I error. If you test 20 features all with no true effect at α=0.05, what is the true α of your procedure? How does Bonferroni correction address this?
P-H2
A hedge fund backtests a momentum strategy on 10 years of data and reports p = 0.002. A Bayesian colleague argues this p-value is misleading because it doesn't account for the prior probability that the strategy has true alpha, and that the fund has tested hundreds of strategies. Explain the Bayesian critique in mathematical terms using Bayes' theorem. What additional information would you need?

Topic: Confidence Intervals

🟢 EASY — Building Blocks ▼
C-E1
Calculate a 95% CI for the mean: x̄ = 8, s = 2, n = 64. Use z* = 1.96. Give the full interval [lower, upper].
C-E2
Mark TRUE or FALSE: (a) "There is a 95% probability the true mean is in [6.5, 9.5]." (b) "95% of similarly constructed intervals will contain the true mean." (c) "The true mean has a 95% chance of being between 6.5 and 9.5." Explain the distinction carefully.
C-E3
If you increase the confidence level from 95% to 99% (keeping data the same), does the interval get wider or narrower? Why? Give an intuitive explanation.
C-E4
A 95% CI for a strategy's Sharpe ratio is [−0.2, 0.8]. Based on this alone, can you say the strategy is significantly profitable? Explain.
🟡 MEDIUM — Application ▼
C-M1
You run a strategy for 100 days: x̄ = 0.1%, s = 0.8%. (a) Construct 90%, 95%, and 99% CIs for the true mean daily return. (b) For each CI, state whether you'd reject H₀: μ = 0 at the corresponding α level and why.
C-M2
You want a margin of error no larger than 0.02% for your daily return estimate. Your historical σ ≈ 1%. How many days of data do you need for a 95% CI with this precision? Use the formula n = (z* · σ / E)².
C-M3
Strategy A has 95% CI for Sharpe: [0.3, 1.4]. Strategy B: [0.8, 1.2]. (a) Which is more precisely estimated? (b) Can you say Strategy B is better? (c) What does the overlap (or lack thereof) of CIs tell you?
🔴 HARD — Quant Depth ▼
C-H1
The Sharpe ratio CI is more complex than the mean CI because the Sharpe is a ratio statistic. Look up the Jobson-Korkie formula for the standard error of the Sharpe ratio. Implement it in Python: given 252 daily returns, compute the Sharpe ratio and its 95% CI. Then test H₀: SR = 0.
C-H2
Bootstrap Confidence Intervals: instead of the formula-based approach, implement a percentile bootstrap CI in Python. Given 200 return observations: (a) resample with replacement 10,000 times, (b) compute the mean of each resample, (c) take the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles as the 95% CI. Compare to the formula-based CI. When and why might bootstrap CIs be preferred for financial data?

Topic: Type I & II Errors

🟢 EASY — Error Identification ▼
E-E1
Classify each as Type I error, Type II error, or correct decision: (a) A strategy has no real alpha, but your test says it does. You deploy capital. (b) A strategy genuinely has alpha, your test confirms it. (c) A strategy has no alpha, your test correctly shows no significance. (d) A genuine alpha strategy looks like noise in your test, so you don't trade it.
E-E2
What is the probability of a Type I error if you set α = 0.05 and H₀ is true? What controls this probability? Can you reduce it to zero?
E-E3
In a medical context (testing a new cancer treatment): which error is more dangerous — Type I or Type II? In a quant context (screening algo strategies): which might be more costly, and why might the answer differ from the medical case?
E-E4
If you want to reduce Type II errors without changing α, what can you do? Name two specific approaches and explain why each works.
🟡 MEDIUM — Tradeoffs & Calculations ▼
E-M1
A strategy has true daily return μ = 0.05%, σ = 1%. You test H₀: μ = 0 (one-sided, α = 0.05) over n = 252 days. (a) Compute the critical value for x̄. (b) What is the power (probability of correctly rejecting H₀)? Use scipy.stats.norm. (c) What n would you need for 80% power?
E-M2
Complete the 2×2 error table for a study that runs 200 hypothesis tests where 20 are truly alternative (H₁ true) and the rest are null (H₀ true). Assume α=0.05 and power=0.80. How many True Positives, False Positives, True Negatives, and False Negatives do you expect?
E-M3
You lower your significance threshold from α=0.05 to α=0.01 to be more conservative. Show mathematically how this changes your Type I error rate, your Type II error rate (β), and your power — assuming sample size is fixed. Is this always the right tradeoff?
🔴 HARD — Advanced Scenarios ▼
E-H1
The False Discovery Rate (FDR): you're running 500 backtests simultaneously, each at α=0.05. Using the Benjamini-Hochberg procedure, explain how to control the expected proportion of false positives among all rejections (FDR). Implement it in Python: given 500 p-values (450 under H₀, 50 under H₁ with true effect), apply B-H correction at FDR=0.05 and compare to uncorrected results.
E-H2
Walk-Forward Testing and Overfitting: a strategy shows p=0.001 in in-sample testing but p=0.34 out-of-sample. Frame this as a Type I / Type II error problem. Why does in-sample testing systematically inflate Type I error rates? What is the deflated Sharpe ratio (Bailey & Lopez de Prado) and how does it attempt to correct for this? Provide the formula and implement a simplified version.
