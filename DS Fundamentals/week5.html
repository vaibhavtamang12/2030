Before You Begin

Prerequisites & Foundational Concepts

Before diving into financial instruments, you need a solid grasp of a few mathematical and economic concepts. These are the building blocks. Don't skip them — every advanced topic references these ideas directly.

Supply & Demand

🛒 What is Supply & Demand? (The simplest explanation)

Demand: How many people WANT something at a given price. As price drops, more people want it. As price rises, fewer want it.

Supply: How much of something SELLERS are willing to offer. Higher prices → more sellers step in. Lower prices → fewer sellers bother.

Equilibrium: The price where supply equals demand. This is the "market price." In financial markets, this happens thousands of times per second.

Time Value of Money (TVM)

⏰ The Golden Rule of Finance

A dollar today is worth MORE than a dollar tomorrow. Why? Because if you have $1 today, you can invest it and earn returns. So future money is "discounted" to find its present value.

Future Value (FV)
FV = PV × (1 + r)ⁿ

PV = Present Value (money today) r = interest rate per period n = number of periods

✅ Example

You invest $1,000 today at 5% annual interest for 3 years:
FV = 1000 × (1.05)³ = $1,157.63

Present Value (PV) — Discounting
PV = FV ÷ (1 + r)ⁿ

Discounting: finding what a future payment is worth TODAY

📌 Example

What is $1,000 received 3 years from now worth today if r=5%?
PV = 1000 ÷ (1.05)³ = $863.84

Risk & Return

⚖️ The Risk-Return Tradeoff

In finance, higher potential returns always come with higher risk. A savings account gives you 2% but almost zero risk. A startup investment might give 1000% or lose everything. Understanding this tradeoff is the foundation of all investing.

Return

📈 What is Return?

The gain (or loss) you make on an investment as a percentage.

Return = (Ending Value − Starting Value) / Starting Value × 100%

If you buy a stock for $100 and sell for $115, return = 15%.

Risk

📉 What is Risk?

In finance, risk = uncertainty of returns. Measured as standard deviation (σ) of historical returns. High σ = big swings up AND down. This is called volatility.

Diversification

🗂️ Don't Put All Eggs in One Basket

Holding many uncorrelated assets reduces risk without reducing expected return. This is the power of diversification — the only "free lunch" in finance.

Chapter 01

Financial Markets — The Big Picture

A financial market is any place (physical or digital) where buyers and sellers come to trade financial assets. Understanding the structure of markets is essential before studying specific instruments.

What is a Financial Market?

The Simple Explanation

Imagine a giant auction house that never closes. Millions of people — individuals, companies, banks, governments — all come to buy and sell financial instruments (stocks, bonds, currencies, derivatives). The market sets prices based on supply and demand, allocates capital efficiently, and allows people to manage risk.

Primary Market

🆕 Where Assets Are Born

When a company sells NEW shares to investors for the first time (IPO — Initial Public Offering). The company raises capital directly. Examples: Facebook's 2012 IPO, government selling new treasury bonds.

Secondary Market

🔄 Where Trading Happens

After assets are created, investors trade them with EACH OTHER. The company gets no money from these trades. NYSE, NASDAQ — this is where you "buy a stock." 99% of trading volume happens here.

OTC Market

🤝 Over The Counter

Trades that happen directly between two parties, not on a central exchange. Bonds, many derivatives, currencies (FX) — mostly OTC. Less transparent but more flexible. Larger notional volume than exchanges.

How Prices Are Set — Order Matching

📖 The Order Book

Every exchange maintains an order book — a real-time list of all outstanding buy and sell orders for an asset. Buyers place bid orders (max price they'll pay). Sellers place ask orders (min price they'll accept).


When a bid ≥ an ask: a TRADE happens. This is order matching — the exchange automatically executes the trade at the overlapping price.

Chapter 02

Equities — Stocks & Shares

When you own a stock, you own a piece of a real company. Equities are the most visible financial instrument — they're what most people mean when they say "the market." For quants, equities are where most strategies and models begin.

What Is a Stock?

🏢 Ownership Explained Simply

A company needs money to grow. Instead of borrowing (which creates debt), it can sell ownership stakes — these stakes are called shares or stocks. Each share represents a fractional ownership of the company's assets and earnings. If a company is worth $1 billion and issues 1 million shares, each share = $1,000 of company value (at book value). But market prices reflect expected future earnings, not just current book value.

Common Stock

🗳️ Common Shares

Most stocks you hear about. Voting rights (usually 1 vote per share). Receive dividends after preferred shareholders. In bankruptcy, paid LAST. Higher risk, higher potential reward.

Preferred Stock

👑 Preferred Shares

Fixed dividend payments like a bond. Paid before common stockholders in bankruptcy. Usually NO voting rights. Hybrid between stock and bond. Often held by VCs in startups.

Key Equity Concepts for Quants
📊 Market Capitalization
Market Cap Formula
Market Cap = Share Price × Shares Outstanding Example: Apple shares = $190, shares outstanding = 15.4B Market Cap = $190 × 15,400,000,000 = ~$2.93 Trillion
Large-Cap

🏦 >$10B

Apple, Microsoft, Google. Stable, liquid, lower risk. S&P 500 companies.

Mid-Cap

🏢 $2B–$10B

More growth potential, less liquid. Higher volatility than large-caps.

Small-Cap

🏠 $300M–$2B

High growth potential, illiquid, high risk. Research intensive for quants.

💰 Dividends

What are dividends?

When a company earns profit, it can either reinvest it (retained earnings) or distribute it to shareholders. Distributions to shareholders are called dividends. Paid quarterly in the US.

Dividend Yield = Annual Dividend / Share Price
If a stock pays $4/year dividend and trades at $100, dividend yield = 4%.

📐 Equity Valuation — How Do We Price a Stock?
The Fundamental Question: What should you pay for a stock? Finance theory says: the price should equal the present value of all future cash flows the company will generate. This is the core of fundamental analysis.
P/E Ratio

📏 Price-to-Earnings

P/E = Stock Price / Earnings Per Share (EPS)

If stock = $50 and EPS = $5, P/E = 10x. "You pay $10 for every $1 of earnings." S&P 500 historical avg ≈ 16-17x. Tech stocks often 30-50x+ (growth priced in).

DCF

💸 Discounted Cash Flow

Project future free cash flows, then discount them back to today using a discount rate (cost of capital). Intrinsic Value = Σ [FCF_t / (1+r)^t]. If intrinsic value > market price → potentially undervalued.

DDM

🌱 Dividend Discount Model

For dividend-paying stocks: P = D₁ / (r - g)
where D₁ = next dividend, r = required return, g = dividend growth rate. Simple but elegant — assumes dividends grow forever at rate g.

📉 Stock Returns — What Drives Prices?

📊 Return Components for Quants

β Beta — Systematic Risk
Beta Definition
β = Cov(r_stock, r_market) / Var(r_market) β = 1.0 → moves exactly with market β = 1.5 → moves 50% MORE than market β = 0.5 → moves 50% LESS than market β = -1.0→ moves OPPOSITE to market

Why β matters for quants

The CAPM (Capital Asset Pricing Model) says:
E(r) = r_f + β × (E(r_m) − r_f)

Expected return = risk-free rate + beta × equity risk premium

This is the foundation of risk-adjusted return analysis. A quant strategy should deliver alpha (returns above what beta predicts).

α Alpha — The Holy Grail

💡 What is Alpha?

Alpha = Actual Return − Expected Return (from CAPM)

If CAPM predicts your stock should return 12% (based on its beta), but it actually returns 18%, you have alpha = +6%. Alpha represents skill or informational edge — returns not explained by market exposure.

The entire quant finance industry is a hunt for alpha. Most retail investors have zero alpha after costs.

Stock Market Indices

📊 What is an Index?

An index tracks the performance of a basket of stocks, representing a segment of the market. You can't directly buy an index, but you can buy ETFs (Exchange-Traded Funds) that replicate it.

Bonds — Fixed Income Instruments

Bonds are debt instruments. When you buy a bond, you're essentially LENDING money to a government or corporation in exchange for regular interest payments (coupons) and your principal back at the end. Fixed income is actually a LARGER market than equities globally.

The #1 Bond Relationship: Price vs Yield

⚠️ The Most Important Fact About Bonds

Bond prices and yields (interest rates) move in OPPOSITE directions.

This confuses beginners constantly. Here's the intuition:
• You own a bond paying 5% ($50/year) that you bought for $1,000.
• Market interest rates rise to 7%. New bonds pay $70/year.
• Your bond (paying only $50) is now less attractive. Nobody pays $1,000 for it.
• Price FALLS until the yield (return) matches new market rates.

Higher rates → Lower bond prices. Lower rates → Higher bond prices.

📊 Price-Yield Relationship (Convex Curve)

Yield to Maturity — The Key Formula
YTM (Approximate Formula — exact requires iteration)
YTM ≈ [C + (F - P)/n] / [(F + P)/2] Where: C = Annual coupon payment F = Face value ($1,000 typically) P = Current market price n = Years to maturity Example: 5% coupon bond, 10 years, trading at $950 YTM ≈ [50 + (1000-950)/10] / [(1000+950)/2] = [50 + 5] / [975] = 55 / 975 ≈ 5.64%
Duration — Bond's Sensitivity to Rate Changes

⏱️ Duration Intuition

Duration is the weighted average time to receive the bond's cash flows. But more practically for traders: duration ≈ price sensitivity to interest rate changes.

Modified Duration: If duration = 8 years, a 1% rise in interest rates causes approximately an 8% fall in the bond's price. A 0.25% rise causes ≈ 2% price fall.

This is WHY long-term bonds are riskier than short-term bonds: they have higher duration = more sensitive to rate changes.

Modified Duration & Price Change
Modified Duration (D*) = Macaulay Duration / (1 + YTM) ΔP/P ≈ -D* × Δy Example: Bond with D* = 6, yield rises by 0.5% (50bps): ΔP/P ≈ -6 × 0.005 = -0.03 = -3% price drop
Types of Bonds
Government

🏛️ Sovereign Bonds

Issued by national governments. US Treasuries (T-Bills, T-Notes, T-Bonds), UK Gilts, Indian G-Secs. Considered near risk-free (for developed nations). Lowest yield but highest safety.

Corporate

🏢 Corporate Bonds

Issued by companies. Higher yield than government bonds (credit spread) to compensate for default risk. Investment-grade (BBB- and above) vs High-yield / Junk bonds (below BBB-).

Municipal

🌆 Muni Bonds

Issued by local/state governments. Often tax-exempt in US, making effective yield higher for high-tax-bracket investors. Fund schools, roads, infrastructure.

Inflation-Linked

📊 TIPS (US) / Linkers

Principal adjusts with inflation. TIPS = Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities. Protect holders from inflation eroding real returns. Real yield = Nominal yield − Inflation.

The Yield Curve — Most Important Chart in Finance

📈 What is the Yield Curve?

A plot of bond yields across different maturities (3 months to 30 years), all for the same credit quality. It shows how interest rates vary by time horizon. The shape of the yield curve tells us A LOT about the economy.

📊 Yield Curve Shapes

🚨 Yield Curve Inversion: Every US recession since 1950 has been preceded by an inverted yield curve (2-year yield > 10-year yield). The 2s10s spread is one of the most watched macroeconomic indicators. When short-term rates exceed long-term rates, it signals the market expects the Fed to CUT rates in the future (because a recession is coming).

Futures — Standardized Derivative Contracts

A futures contract is an agreement to buy or sell an asset at a predetermined price on a specific future date. Futures are traded on exchanges and are critical tools for hedging and speculation. Learn futures before options — they share core concepts like leverage and margin.

The Intuition Behind Futures

🌾 A Farmer's Story (Original Use Case)

Imagine you're a wheat farmer. It's March, and you won't harvest until August. Current wheat price = $5/bushel. You fear prices might drop to $3 by August, ruining your profits.

A cereal company fears the opposite — prices might rise to $7.

Solution: You both agree TODAY to trade 1,000 bushels at $5 in August. You've locked in your price (hedged). The cereal company has locked in their cost.

This is a futures contract. Both parties eliminate price uncertainty. Today, futures cover everything: stock indices, oil, currencies, interest rates, cryptocurrencies.

Key Futures Concepts
💲 Margin & Leverage

⚠️ Futures Require Margin — NOT Full Payment

You don't pay the full contract value upfront. You post a small initial margin (typically 5-15% of contract value). This creates enormous leverage.

Example: S&P 500 E-mini futures contract ≈ $200,000 value. Initial margin ≈ $13,000 (6.5%). A 1% move in S&P = $2,000 profit or loss on your $13,000 — that's a 15% return or loss from a 1% market move!

Leverage & Margin
Leverage Ratio = Contract Value / Margin Posted Example: Contract Value = $200,000 Initial Margin = $13,000 Leverage Ratio = 200,000/13,000 ≈ 15x Daily P&L = (Price Change) × Contract Multiplier

📌 Margin Call

If losses reduce your account below the maintenance margin level, you receive a margin call — you must deposit more funds immediately or your position is liquidated. Futures traders can lose MORE than their initial deposit.

📅 Daily Mark-to-Market Settlement

🔄 How Futures Settle Daily

Unlike stocks, futures are marked-to-market every day. Each day, the exchange calculates your profit or loss based on the day's settlement price and either credits or debits it directly to your margin account. This daily settlement is what makes futures safe — you can't accumulate large losses that a counterparty can't pay.

📊 Daily Mark-to-Market Example (Long 1 Gold Futures Contract)

⚖️ Futures Pricing — The Cost of Carry Model
Futures Fair Value (Cost of Carry Model)
F = S × e^(r - q) × T [Continuous compounding] F = S × (1 + r - q)^T [Simple approximation] Where: F = Futures price S = Spot (current) price r = Risk-free interest rate q = Dividend yield (for equity futures) or convenience yield T = Time to expiration (in years) Intuition: Futures price = Spot + Cost to "carry" the asset (interest paid) − Benefits received (dividends, convenience)

🔍 Contango vs Backwardation

Contango: Futures price > Spot price. Normal for most commodities (storage costs, interest). Long-term futures more expensive than near-term.

Backwardation: Futures price < Spot price. Happens when current demand is high (like oil shortage). Short-term futures MORE expensive than long-term. Often signals supply shortage.

🛡️ Hedging with Futures — A Real Example

Portfolio Hedging Example

You manage a $10M equity portfolio with β = 1.2. You fear a market downturn.

Number of contracts to sell = (Portfolio Value × β) / (Futures Price × Multiplier)

If E-mini S&P 500 is at 4,500: Contract value = 4,500 × $50 = $225,000
Contracts needed = (10,000,000 × 1.2) / 225,000 = 53.3 ≈ 53 contracts to SHORT

If market falls 10%, your portfolio loses ~$1.2M, but your short futures gain ~$1.2M. Hedged!

Chapter 05

Options — The Right, Not Obligation

Options are the most mathematically rich financial instrument. An option gives the buyer the right (but not the obligation) to buy or sell an asset at a fixed price before a specific date. This optionality has value, and pricing it is where quant finance truly shines.

Call vs Put — The Two Basic Options
CALL OPTION

📈 Right to BUY

Buyer pays a premium to get the right to buy the underlying asset at the strike price on or before expiration.

Profit when: Underlying price > Strike + Premium paid

You buy a call when you're BULLISH. Maximum loss = premium paid. Maximum gain = theoretically unlimited.

PUT OPTION

📉 Right to SELL

Buyer pays a premium to get the right to sell the underlying asset at the strike price on or before expiration.

Profit when: Underlying price < Strike − Premium paid

You buy a put when you're BEARISH. Maximum loss = premium. Maximum gain = Strike price (if stock goes to 0).

Black-Scholes Model — The Cornerstone of Options Pricing
Historical Context: Fischer Black, Myron Scholes, and Robert Merton developed this model in 1973. Scholes and Merton received the Nobel Prize in Economics in 1997 (Black had died in 1995). The BSM model is the foundation of modern quantitative finance and derivatives pricing.
Black-Scholes-Merton Formula
Call Price: C = S·N(d₁) − K·e^(-rT)·N(d₂) Put Price: P = K·e^(-rT)·N(-d₂) − S·N(-d₁) Where: d₁ = [ln(S/K) + (r + σ²/2)·T] / (σ·√T) d₂ = d₁ − σ·√T S = Current stock price K = Strike price r = Risk-free interest rate (continuous) T = Time to expiration (in years) σ = Implied volatility (annualized) N() = Cumulative normal distribution function e = Euler's number (≈ 2.71828)

🧠 BSM Assumptions (and why they're violated in practice)

  • Returns are log-normally distributed (Real markets have fat tails)
  • Volatility is constant (Real: it changes constantly — see Vol Smile)
  • No dividends (Extended models handle dividends)
  • No transaction costs (Real markets have frictions)
  • Can trade continuously (Real: discrete trading)
  • Risk-free rate is known and constant
The Greeks — Sensitivities of Option Prices

🔤 Why "The Greeks"?

The Greeks measure how an option's price changes with respect to different factors. Each Greek is a partial derivative of the option price with respect to one variable. Understanding the Greeks is essential for options trading, delta hedging, and risk management.

Δ Delta

∂C/∂S — Price Sensitivity

How much option price changes per $1 change in stock price.

Call delta: 0 to +1. Put delta: -1 to 0.
ATM call ≈ 0.50 (moves $0.50 per $1 stock move)
Deep ITM call ≈ 1.0 (moves like stock)

Also: probability the option expires ITM!

Γ Gamma

∂²C/∂S² — Delta's Rate of Change

How fast delta changes with stock price. Second-order sensitivity.

Highest for ATM options near expiry. Gamma tells you how much your hedge will "slip." Long gamma = you benefit from large moves (good!). Short gamma = you get hurt by large moves.

Θ Theta

∂C/∂t — Time Decay

How much option price decays per day as time passes. Usually negative for option BUYERS (you're losing time value every day).

"Theta burn": A $5 option expiring in 30 days might lose $0.10/day. Option sellers PROFIT from theta.

Vega

∂C/∂σ — Volatility Sensitivity

How much option price changes per 1% change in implied volatility. (Technically not a Greek letter.)

All options have positive vega — higher vol = higher option price. Buying options = long vega. You profit from vol spikes (like VIX jumping in crashes).

ρ Rho

∂C/∂r — Interest Rate Sensitivity

How much option price changes with interest rates. Less impactful for short-dated options. More important for long-dated options (LEAPS). Call options have positive rho (benefit from rate rises).

Delta Hedging — How Market Makers Stay Neutral

🔄 The Delta Hedging Process

A market maker sells a call option with delta = 0.60. To be delta-neutral (no directional exposure), they BUY 60 shares of stock for every 100 options sold. If the stock moves, delta changes (gamma!), so they rebalance. This continuous rebalancing is called dynamic delta hedging.

The cost of this rebalancing IS the cost of gamma — and it's essentially what the option seller receives as payment for bearing that risk. BSM shows: the fair option price = expected cost of delta hedging.

Implied Volatility & The Volatility Smile

😊 What is the Vol Smile?

If BSM were perfect, implied volatility would be the same across all strikes. But in reality, options with lower strikes (especially puts) have HIGHER implied vol than ATM options. This U-shape (or smirk) is called the volatility smile/smirk.

This tells us markets know BSM is wrong — real return distributions have fat tails (tail risk). Puts are expensive because investors pay up for crash protection. This is the entire business of "volatility trading."

Market Microstructure — How Markets Actually Work

Microstructure is the study of the detailed process and mechanics of how financial assets are traded. For quants (especially HFT and execution researchers), this is where alpha lives. It's the difference between theory and the messy reality of markets.

The Order Book — Deep Dive

📖 What is the Limit Order Book (LOB)?

The central data structure of modern markets. It's a real-time, sorted list of all open limit orders (orders with specified price limits). The LOB determines the current price, available liquidity, and trading costs. For quants, modeling the LOB is a rich research area.

Order Type

📋 Market Order

Execute immediately at the best available price. You get guaranteed execution but UNCERTAIN price. In a fast market, you may get a price far from what you expected ("slippage"). Aggressive — takes liquidity from the book.

Order Type

🎯 Limit Order

Execute only at your specified price or better. You get guaranteed price but UNCERTAIN execution (may never fill if price doesn't reach your limit). Passive — adds liquidity to the book. Earns maker rebates on many exchanges.

Order Type

🛡️ Stop Order

Becomes a market order when price hits a trigger level. Used to limit losses (stop-loss) or enter on breakouts. Risk: in fast markets, stop orders can "gap" — execute far below your trigger (called "slippage").

Market Microstructure Concepts for Quants
💰 The Bid-Ask Spread — Who Captures It & Why
Bid-Ask Spread Components
Bid-Ask Spread = Inventory Risk + Adverse Selection + Order Processing Inventory Risk: MM holds shares that could move against them Adverse Selection: MM might be trading with someone who KNOWS more Order Processing: System costs, regulatory fees, exchange fees Effective Spread ≈ 2 × |Trade Price − Midpoint| Quoted Spread = Ask Price − Bid Price Relative Spread = (Ask − Bid) / Midpoint × 100%
⚡ High-Frequency Trading (HFT)

🤖 What is HFT?

Trading strategies executed by algorithms in microseconds (millionths of a second) to milliseconds. HFT firms use co-location (servers physically next to exchange matching engines), fiber optic and microwave networks, and custom FPGA hardware to gain speed advantages measured in nanoseconds.

Main HFT strategies:
• Market making: Post bid and ask, earn the spread thousands of times per second
• Statistical arbitrage: Exploit tiny price differences between correlated assets
• Latency arbitrage: React faster to news than competitors
• Order flow toxicity detection: Identify informed traders and step aside

📊 Price Impact & Market Impact Models

🌊 What is Market Impact?

When you trade, you MOVE the market against yourself. Buying pushes prices up; selling pushes prices down. For large orders, this impact can be significant and costly.

Temporary impact: Price reverts after your trade (you created temporary imbalance)
Permanent impact: Price stays at new level (your trade conveyed information)

A quant strategy with great backtested returns can fail completely once live because market impact wasn't modeled correctly.

Almgren-Chriss Market Impact (Simplified)
Total Cost = Linear Impact + Nonlinear Impact + Risk Cost Linear (temporary): η × σ × (v/V) Nonlinear (permanent): γ × σ × (X/V)^(3/2) Where: σ = asset volatility v = trading rate (shares/time) V = average daily volume X = total order size η, γ = impact coefficients (calibrated from data) Key insight: SLOWING DOWN reduces impact but increases risk SPEEDING UP reduces risk but increases impact Optimal execution balances both.
🧮 VWAP, TWAP — Execution Algorithms
VWAP

📊 Volume Weighted Avg Price

Execute in proportion to historical volume at each time period. Ensures you don't trade too much when volume is thin (higher impact). The standard benchmark for institutional execution. VWAP = Σ(Price × Volume) / Σ(Volume).

TWAP

⏱️ Time Weighted Avg Price

Split order equally across time slices. Simpler than VWAP. Used when you don't want to predict volume patterns. Trades same size every N minutes. More predictable but potentially worse in low-volume periods.

IS

🎯 Implementation Shortfall

The difference between paper portfolio return and actual return due to trading costs. Measures: bid-ask spread cost + market impact + delay cost + opportunity cost. The TRUE measure of execution quality. IS = Paper P&L − Actual P&L.

🔍 Adverse Selection & Informed Trading

⚠️ The Adverse Selection Problem

A market maker doesn't know if they're trading with an informed trader (who knows the true value) or an uninformed trader (random liquidity need).

If an informed trader knows a stock will rise, they'll BUY from the market maker. The market maker loses money on this trade. To survive, market makers widen spreads to compensate for expected losses to informed traders.

This is Glosten-Milgrom adverse selection — a cornerstone of microstructure theory. PIN (Probability of Informed Trading) is a measure of how much of the order flow is informed.

import numpy as np from scipy.stats import norm import matplotlib.pyplot as plt class BlackScholes: def __init__(self, S, K, T, r, sigma): """ S: Stock price, K: Strike, T: Time to expiry (years) r: Risk-free rate, sigma: Volatility """ self.S, self.K, self.T = S, K, T self.r, self.sigma = r, sigma self.d1 = (np.log(S/K) + (r + 0.5*sigma**2)*T) / (sigma * np.sqrt(T)) self.d2 = self.d1 - sigma * np.sqrt(T) def call_price(self): return self.S * norm.cdf(self.d1) - self.K * np.exp(-self.r*self.T) * norm.cdf(self.d2) def put_price(self): return self.K * np.exp(-self.r*self.T) * norm.cdf(-self.d2) - self.S * norm.cdf(-self.d1) def delta_call(self): return norm.cdf(self.d1) def delta_put(self): return norm.cdf(self.d1) - 1 def gamma(self): return norm.pdf(self.d1) / (self.S * self.sigma * np.sqrt(self.T)) def theta_call(self): t1 = -(self.S * norm.pdf(self.d1) * self.sigma) / (2 * np.sqrt(self.T)) t2 = -self.r * self.K * np.exp(-self.r*self.T) * norm.cdf(self.d2) return (t1 + t2) / 365 # per day def vega(self): return (self.S * norm.pdf(self.d1) * np.sqrt(self.T)) / 100 # per 1% vol # === Example: Price a Call Option === bs = BlackScholes(S=100, K=100, T=0.25, r=0.05, sigma=0.20) print("=== Black-Scholes Option Pricing ===") print(f"Call Price: ${bs.call_price():.4f}") print(f"Put Price: ${bs.put_price():.4f}") print(f"\n--- Greeks ---") print(f"Delta (Call): {bs.delta_call():.4f}") print(f"Delta (Put): {bs.delta_put():.4f}") print(f"Gamma: {bs.gamma():.4f}") print(f"Theta (Call): ${bs.theta_call():.4f} per day") print(f"Vega: ${bs.vega():.4f} per 1% vol change") # === Visualization: Greeks vs Stock Price === stock_prices = np.linspace(60, 140, 200) params = dict(K=100, T=0.25, r=0.05, sigma=0.20) calls = [BlackScholes(S=s, **params).call_price() for s in stock_prices] puts = [BlackScholes(S=s, **params).put_price() for s in stock_prices] deltas = [BlackScholes(S=s, **params).delta_call() for s in stock_prices] gammas = [BlackScholes(S=s, **params).gamma() for s in stock_prices] fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 2, figsize=(14, 10)) fig.suptitle('Black-Scholes Option Sensitivities (K=100, T=3m, σ=20%)', fontsize=14) axes[0,0].plot(stock_prices, calls, 'cyan', linewidth=2, label='Call') axes[0,0].plot(stock_prices, puts, 'salmon', linewidth=2, label='Put') axes[0,0].axvline(100, '--', alpha=0.5) axes[0,0].set_title('Option Prices'); axes[0,0].legend(); axes[0,0].grid(alpha=0.3) axes[0,1].plot(stock_prices, deltas, 'lime', linewidth=2) axes[0,1].axhline(0.5, color='gray', linestyle='--', alpha=0.5, label='ATM Delta ≈ 0.5') axes[0,1].set_title('Call Delta'); axes[0,1].legend(); axes[0,1].grid(alpha=0.3) axes[1,0].plot(stock_prices, gammas, 'orange', linewidth=2) axes[1,0].set_title('Gamma (peaks at ATM)'); axes[1,0].grid(alpha=0.3) # Payoff diagram at expiry payoff_call = np.maximum(stock_prices - 100, 0) - bs.call_price() payoff_put = np.maximum(100 - stock_prices, 0) - bs.put_price() axes[1,1].plot(stock_prices, payoff_call, 'cyan', linewidth=2, label='Long Call P&L') axes[1,1].plot(stock_prices, payoff_put, 'salmon', linewidth=2, label='Long Put P&L') axes[1,1].axhline(0, color='white', linewidth=0.5) axes[1,1].set_title('Payoff at Expiry (Net of Premium)') axes[1,1].legend(); axes[1,1].grid(alpha=0.3) axes[1,1].fill_between(stock_prices, payoff_call, 0, where=payoff_call>0, alpha=0.1, color='cyan') plt.tight_layout() plt.show()

Build a simple limit order book simulator and visualize market impact of large trades — core microstructure concepts.

import numpy as np import matplotlib.pyplot as plt from collections import defaultdict # === Simple Order Book Simulator === class OrderBook: def __init__(self, mid_price=100.0, tick_size=0.01): self.bids = defaultdict(float) # price -> qty self.asks = defaultdict(float) self.mid = mid_price self.tick = tick_size self._populate_book() def _populate_book(self): # Generate realistic LOB with decreasing depth away from mid for i in range(1, 20): qty = np.random.exponential(500) self.bids[round(self.mid - i*self.tick, 2)] = qty self.asks[round(self.mid + i*self.tick, 2)] = qty def best_bid(self): return max(self.bids.keys()) def best_ask(self): return min(self.asks.keys()) def spread(self): return self.best_ask() - self.best_bid() def midpoint(self): return (self.best_bid() + self.best_ask()) / 2 def market_buy(self, qty): """Execute market buy, returns avg fill price and market impact""" remaining = qty total_cost = 0 fills = [] initial_mid = self.midpoint() for price in sorted(self.asks.keys()): if remaining <= 0: break avail = self.asks[price] fill = min(remaining, avail) total_cost += fill * price fills.append((price, fill)) self.asks[price] -= fill if self.asks[price] <= 0: del self.asks[price] remaining -= fill avg_price = total_cost / qty impact = self.midpoint() - initial_mid return avg_price, impact, fills # === Test: Market Impact of Different Order Sizes === order_sizes = [100, 500, 1000, 2000, 5000, 10000] impacts = [] avg_prices = [] for size in order_sizes: ob = OrderBook(100.0, 0.01) initial_ask = ob.best_ask() avg_price, impact, _ = ob.market_buy(size) avg_prices.append(avg_price) impacts.append(impact) print(f"Order: {size:6} shares | Avg Fill: ${avg_price:.4f} | Impact: {impact*100:.4f}%") # === Visualization === ob_viz = OrderBook(100.0, 0.01) fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(14, 5)) # Order Book Visualization bid_prices = sorted(ob_viz.bids.keys(), reverse=True)[:10] ask_prices = sorted(ob_viz.asks.keys())[:10] axes[0].barh([f"${p}" for p in bid_prices], [ob_viz.bids[p] for p in bid_prices], color='lime', alpha=0.7, label='Bids') axes[0].barh([f"${p}" for p in ask_prices], [ob_viz.asks[p] for p in ask_prices], color='salmon', alpha=0.7, label='Asks') axes[0].set_title('Limit Order Book') axes[0].set_xlabel('Quantity') axes[0].legend() # Market Impact vs Order Size axes[1].plot(order_sizes, [i*100 for i in impacts], 'o-', color='cyan', markersize=8) axes[1].set_title('Market Impact vs Order Size') axes[1].set_xlabel('Order Size (shares)') axes[1].set_ylabel('Mid-Price Impact (%)') axes[1].grid(alpha=0.3) plt.tight_layout() plt.show()
