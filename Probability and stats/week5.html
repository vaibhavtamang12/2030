Prerequisites

Before we touch the main theorems, let's make sure five foundational ideas feel comfortable. Each one gets a plain-English explanation right here.

⚠ Who This Section Is For

First-time learners. If terms like "expected value" and "variance" already feel natural to you, skim the formulas and jump straight to Tab 1. Otherwise, spend 10–15 minutes here — it will make the rest click much faster.

1 · Random Variables

A random variable is a number whose value is determined by a random experiment. Before the experiment, the value is uncertain. After it runs, you get one specific number.

Discrete random variable
Takes countable separate values. Example: the number you roll on a die ∈ {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}.
Continuous random variable
Takes any value in a range. Example: today's temperature, a stock's return, a person's height.

Everyday Example

Let X = "number shown on a fair six-sided die." Before rolling, X is unknown. After rolling, you might get X = 4. The distribution of X is the full list of possible values and their probabilities: P(X=1)=P(X=2)=···=P(X=6)=1/6.

2 · Expected Value E[X] — the Long-Run Average

The expected value is the probability-weighted average of all possible values. Ask yourself: "If I ran this experiment a million times and averaged the results, what would the average be?" That answer is E[X].

Definition
E[X] = Σ x · P(X = x)    (discrete)
E[X] = ∫ x · f(x) dx      (continuous)
For a fair die: E[X] = 1·(1/6) + 2·(1/6) + 3·(1/6) + 4·(1/6) + 5·(1/6) + 6·(1/6) = 3.5

The Casino Interpretation

A roulette bet on "red" wins $1 with probability 18/38, loses $1 with probability 20/38. E[payout] = 1·(18/38) + (−1)·(20/38) = −0.053. Every single spin is unpredictable — but the casino knows with near-certainty it will earn ~5.3 cents per dollar bet, because of the Law of Large Numbers (which we'll study next).

3 · Variance and Standard Deviation

Variance measures spread: how far do typical values land from the mean? Standard deviation is its square root — same units as X, easier to interpret.

Variance & Std Dev
Var(X) = E[(X − μ)²] = E[X²] − (E[X])²
σ = √Var(X)
μ = E[X]. High variance means values scatter widely. Low variance means values cluster near the mean.
Low Variance — N(5, 0.25)
σ = 0.5 · Values huddle tightly around μ=5
High Variance — N(5, 4)
σ = 2 · Values spread across a wide range

4 · Independence

Two random variables are independent when knowing one gives zero information about the other. Think of flipping two separate fair coins — the first outcome tells you nothing about the second.

⚠ Critical for LLN & CLT

Both theorems assume observations are i.i.d. — independent and identically distributed. Independence means each observation is unaffected by the others. "Identically distributed" means all observations come from the same population. In real finance, stock returns are neither perfectly independent nor identically distributed — that's exactly why quants must understand these assumptions deeply before applying them.

5 · The Sample Mean — Our Central Object

Take n independent observations X₁, X₂, …, Xₙ from the same distribution. Their average is the sample mean:

Sample Mean
X̄ₙ = (X₁ + X₂ + ··· + Xₙ) / n = (1/n) Σᵢ₌₁ⁿ Xᵢ
X̄ₙ is itself a random variable. Each fresh set of n observations gives a different value of X̄ₙ. The LLN tells us where X̄ₙ is heading. The CLT tells us the shape of its fluctuations around that destination.
X̄ₙ is Random — Different Samples, Different Averages
20 independent estimates of the mean from the same N(0,1) population, n=25 each

Topic 1 of 2

Law of Large Numbers

The most fundamental theorem in all of probability: as you collect more data, your sample average is guaranteed to approach the true mean. This is why data works at all.

Why Quants Need This

Monte Carlo pricing, backtesting, risk estimation, factor models — all rely on averaging large numbers of observations. The LLN is the theorem that justifies using a sample mean to estimate a population mean. Without it, statistics would just be guessing.

The Core Idea in Plain English

Flip a fair coin 10 times. You might get 7 heads (70%) — that feels far from 50%. Flip 100 times: you'll probably land between 45% and 55%. Flip 10,000 times: you'll almost certainly be within 1% of 50%. The Law of Large Numbers says this isn't luck — it's mathematically guaranteed to happen as n grows.

The Insurance Company Proof-of-Concept

An insurer can't predict if you will have a car accident this year. But with 1,000,000 policyholders, they can predict total claims with almost frightening accuracy. Individual randomness is high — but the average randomness shrinks. With enough customers, the average claim becomes practically deterministic. That's the entire insurance business model built on one theorem.

The Two Versions

Weak LLN — Khinchin's Theorem
For any ε > 0:  P( |X̄ₙ − μ| > ε ) → 0  as n → ∞
No matter how small your tolerance ε, the probability that X̄ₙ misses μ by more than ε goes to zero. "Convergence in probability."
Strong LLN — Kolmogorov's Theorem
P( lim_{n→∞} X̄ₙ = μ ) = 1
With probability 1, the sample mean WILL converge to μ — not just occasionally close, but permanently settling there. "Almost sure convergence." The stronger guarantee.
VersionMathematical StatementConvergence TypeCondition
Weak LLNP(|X̄ₙ−μ|>ε) → 0In probabilityFinite mean μ
Strong LLNX̄ₙ → μ almost surelyAlmost sureFinite μ and σ²

Weak vs. Strong — What's Actually Different?

Weak: At any given large n, the chance you're far from μ is tiny. But you might still drift away occasionally and return. Like a ship that's probably near its target course but might deviate briefly.

Strong: The path of X̄ₙ eventually settles at μ and stays there forever. The ship reaches port and docks. A genuinely stronger statement — and the one used in practice.

Why It Works — Proof via Chebyshev

The cleanest proof of the Weak LLN uses Chebyshev's Inequality, which holds for any distribution with finite variance:

Chebyshev's Inequality
P( |X̄ₙ − μ| ≥ ε ) ≤ Var(X̄ₙ) / ε²
  1. Compute Var(X̄ₙ)

    Averaging n i.i.d. variables: Var(X̄ₙ) = Var((X₁+···+Xₙ)/n) = nσ²/n² = σ²/n. Variance shrinks with sample size.

  2. Substitute into Chebyshev

    P(|X̄ₙ − μ| ≥ ε) ≤ σ²/(nε²). The right side goes to 0 as n → ∞ for any fixed ε and σ.

  3. Conclude

    Since P(|X̄ₙ − μ| ≥ ε) is squeezed between 0 and something that → 0, it must → 0. Weak LLN proved. ∎

The Standard Error — How Fast Does It Converge?

Standard Error of the Mean
SE(X̄ₙ) = σ / √n
The spread of X̄ₙ around μ shrinks as 1/√n. To halve your error, you need 4× as many observations. To reach one decimal place more of accuracy, you need 100× as many.

⚠ The Gambler's Fallacy — LLN Does NOT Mean This

After flipping 10 tails in a row, many people think "heads is due." This is wrong. The LLN convergence to 0.5 happens by swamping early deviations with huge numbers of future observations — not by the next flip being "corrected." Every flip is exactly 50/50, always, regardless of history.

The LLN says: after 10,000 more flips, those 10 early tails will be statistically irrelevant. It does NOT say: the coin will try to compensate.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

np.random.seed(42)

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════
# PART A: LLN for Four Different Distributions
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════
n_max = 5000
ns = np.arange(1, n_max + 1)
n_paths = 5

configs = [
    ("Coin Flip (μ=0.5)", lambda: np.random.randint(0, 2, n_max), 0.5),
    ("Exponential (μ=1)", lambda: np.random.exponential(1, n_max), 1.0),
    ("Uniform [0,10] (μ=5)", lambda: np.random.uniform(0, 10, n_max), 5.0),
    ("Standard Normal (μ=0)", lambda: np.random.randn(n_max), 0.0),
]

fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 2, figsize=(14, 9))
fig.suptitle("Law of Large Numbers — 5 Paths per Distribution",
             fontsize=14, fontweight="bold")

for ax, (name, sampler, mu) in zip(axes.ravel(), configs):
    for _ in range(n_paths):
        s = sampler()
        ax.plot(ns, np.cumsum(s) / ns, alpha=0.7, linewidth=1)
    ax.axhline(mu, color="black", lw=2, ls="--", label=f"True μ = {mu}")
    ax.set_title(name, fontweight="bold")
    ax.set_xlabel("n (sample size)"); ax.set_ylabel("X̄ₙ")
    ax.legend()

plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════
# PART B: Standard Error — Empirical vs 1/√n
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════
n_vals = [10, 50, 100, 500, 1000, 5000, 10000]
reps = 3000
empirical_ses = []

for n in n_vals:
    means = [np.random.normal(0, 1, n).mean() for _ in range(reps)]
    empirical_ses.append(np.std(means))

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(8, 5))
ax.loglog(n_vals, empirical_ses, "o-", lw=2, label="Empirical Std(X̄ₙ)")
ax.loglog(n_vals, [1/np.sqrt(n) for n in n_vals], "--", lw=2, label="Theoretical 1/√n")
ax.set_title("SE shrinks as 1/√n (log-log scale)")
ax.set_xlabel("n"); ax.set_ylabel("SE"); ax.legend()
ax.set_facecolor("#faf8f4")
plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════
# PART C: Gambler's Fallacy Debunked
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════
print("After 10 consecutive tails, is heads 'due'?")
hits = 0; trials = 0
for _ in range(200_000):
    streak = 0
    while streak < 10:
        flip = np.random.randint(2)
        streak = streak + 1 if flip == 0 else 0
    hits += np.random.randint(2)
    trials += 1
print(f"P(heads | 10 tails) = {hits/trials:.4f}  ← should be 0.5000")

Central Limit Theorem

Perhaps the most astonishing fact in mathematics: average enough independent things together — from any distribution — and the result is always, always normal.

🔗 Connects Directly to LLN

The LLN told you where X̄ₙ goes: to μ. The CLT tells you how it gets there — the shape of the random fluctuations around μ. Together they give a complete picture. The CLT is the sharper, more powerful companion theorem.

The Core Idea in Plain English

You have n i.i.d. random variables. Their average converges to μ (LLN). But if you zoomed in on the remaining small variation around μ, what shape would it have? The CLT gives the startling answer: always the normal (Gaussian) bell curve, no matter what weird shape the original distribution had.

Why Height is Normal

Your height is determined by thousands of genes and environmental factors — each adds a tiny, roughly independent random effect. No single factor is normally distributed. Yet their sum (and therefore your height) is beautifully normal. The CLT is operating here: sum enough independent, finite-variance pieces and normality emerges automatically, regardless of each piece's distribution.

Same logic: stock daily returns (thousands of trades summing up), exam scores (many questions), measurement errors (many small perturbations). Normality is the universal shape of sums of independent things.

The Formal Statement

Central Limit Theorem
√n · (X̄ₙ − μ) / σ  →  N(0, 1)  in distribution, as n → ∞
Requires: X₁,…,Xₙ i.i.d. with E[Xᵢ]=μ and Var(Xᵢ)=σ²<∞. The standardized sample mean converges to standard normal, regardless of the underlying distribution.
Practical Working Form
X̄ₙ  ≈  N(μ, σ²/n)    for large n
Sₙ = X₁+···+Xₙ  ≈  N(nμ, nσ²)    for large n
These two forms are what you'll use 99% of the time in practice. "Large n" in practice often means n ≥ 30 for symmetric distributions, n ≥ 100 for heavily skewed ones.

Unpacking the Formula — Term by Term

  1. (X̄ₙ − μ) — Center the Error

    Subtract μ so the quantity is centered at 0. By LLN this → 0, but CLT "zooms in" on the randomness that remains.

  2. Multiply by √n — Zoom In

    X̄ₙ−μ shrinks at rate 1/√n. Multiplying by √n stabilizes the variance at σ², letting us see the fluctuation shape clearly.

  3. Divide by σ — Standardize

    Makes the variance equal to 1. Now we have a mean-0, variance-1 quantity — a standard normal.

  4. "In distribution" — Shape Converges

    The CDF of √n(X̄ₙ−μ)/σ approaches Φ(z) at every point. The histogram of many simulated X̄ₙ values looks increasingly like a bell curve.

Key Conditions for CLT

ConditionRequirementWhat Breaks Without It
Finite Varianceσ² = Var(X) < ∞Cauchy distribution — CLT fails entirely
IndependenceX₁,…,Xₙ mutually independentCorrelation → convergence is slower, formula wrong
Same DistributionAll Xᵢ from same populationLindeberg condition handles mixing (advanced topic)

Using CLT — Computing Probabilities

Once you know X̄ₙ ≈ N(μ, σ²/n), compute probabilities by standardizing:

Probability Calculation Recipe
P(X̄ₙ ≤ x) ≈ Φ( (x − μ) / (σ/√n) )
Φ = CDF of standard normal. Standardize → z-score = (x−μ)/(σ/√n) → look up Φ(z). In Python: scipy.stats.norm.cdf(z)

Fully Worked Example — Factory Bolts

A factory produces bolts: true diameter μ = 10 mm, σ = 0.5 mm. Inspector samples n = 36 bolts. What is P(X̄₃₆ > 10.15 mm)?

Step-by-Step
Step 1: X̄₃₆ ~ N(10, 0.5²/36) = N(10, 0.00694)
Step 2: SE = 0.5/√36 = 0.5/6 ≈ 0.0833
Step 3: z = (10.15 − 10) / 0.0833 = 1.80
Step 4: P(X̄ > 10.15) = P(Z > 1.80) = 1 − Φ(1.80) ≈ 1 − 0.9641 = 0.036
Only 3.6% chance of seeing a sample mean above 10.15 mm — this would be a suspicious batch. The CLT made this calculation possible.

Speed of Convergence — Berry-Esseen Theorem

How Good Is the Normal Approximation?

The Berry-Esseen theorem quantifies the approximation error:

sup_z |P(√n(X̄ₙ−μ)/σ ≤ z) − Φ(z)| ≤ C · ρ / (σ³·√n)
ρ = E[|X−μ|³] (third absolute moment). C ≈ 0.4748. Error shrinks at rate 1/√n. Symmetric distributions converge faster; skewed ones need larger n.

colab_exp2_clt.py ▶ Open in Colab 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy import stats

np.random.seed(0)
REPS = 10_000

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════
# PART A: CLT from Three Very Different Shapes
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════
dists = {
    "Exponential(1)\n[heavily skewed]": {
        "gen": lambda r, n: np.random.exponential(1, (r, n)),
        "mu": 1.0, "sig": 1.0,
    },
    "Bernoulli(0.1)\n[very skewed]": {
        "gen": lambda r, n: np.random.binomial(1, 0.1, (r, n)),
        "mu": 0.1, "sig": np.sqrt(0.09),
    },
    "Bimodal Mixture\n[two humps]": {
        "gen": lambda r, n: np.where(
            np.random.rand(r, n) < 0.5,
            np.random.normal(-3, 0.5, (r, n)),
            np.random.normal(3, 0.5, (r, n)),
        ),
        "mu": 0.0, "sig": np.sqrt(9.25),
    },
}

n_vals = [1, 5, 30, 100]
colors = ["#c2522b", "#c09a2e", "#4a7c59", "#2c3e50"]

for dname, d in dists.items():
    fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 4, figsize=(16, 4))
    fig.suptitle(f"CLT: {dname}", fontsize=13, fontweight="bold")
    for ax, n, c in zip(axes, n_vals, colors):
        raw = d["gen"](REPS, n)
        xbar = raw.mean(axis=1)
        z = (xbar - d["mu"]) / (d["sig"] / np.sqrt(n))
        ax.hist(z, bins=60, density=True, color=c, alpha=0.7)
        zx = np.linspace(-4, 4, 200)
        ax.plot(zx, stats.norm.pdf(zx), "k-", lw=2, label="N(0,1)")
        _, pval = stats.shapiro(z[:500])
        ax.set_title(f"n = {n}\nShapiro p = {pval:.3f}")
        ax.set_xlim(-5, 5)
        if n == 1: ax.legend(fontsize=8)
    plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()
    print("n=1 → original shape · n=100 → bell curve regardless of origin")

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════
# PART B: Practical Probability Calculation
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════
from scipy.stats import norm
mu, sigma, n = 10.0, 0.5, 36
x_thresh = 10.15
se = sigma / np.sqrt(n)
z = (x_thresh - mu) / se
prob = 1 - norm.cdf(z)
print(f"\nBolt example: P(X̄_36 > 10.15) = {prob:.4f}")

# Verify by simulation
sim = np.random.normal(mu, sigma, (REPS, n)).mean(axis=1)
print(f"Simulated:    P(X̄_36 > 10.15) ≈ {(sim > x_thresh).mean():.4f}")

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════
# PART C: CLT Failure — Cauchy (infinite variance)
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════
fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(15, 4))
for ax, n in zip(axes, [10, 100, 1000]):
    cauchy_means = stats.cauchy.rvs(size=(REPS, n)).mean(axis=1)
    ax.hist(cauchy_means, bins=100, density=True, color="#c2522b", alpha=0.7)
    zx = np.linspace(-8, 8, 200)
    ax.plot(zx, norm.pdf(zx), "k--", lw=2, label="N(0,1)")
    ax.set_xlim(-8, 8)
    ax.set_title(f"Cauchy X̄ₙ · n={n} · CLT FAILS")
    ax.legend()
fig.suptitle("Cauchy has undefined mean & infinite variance → CLT breaks")
plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()

The Bigger Picture

LLN meets CLT

These theorems aren't rivals — they're partners. Understood together, they completely describe the behaviour of the sample mean. Here's how they fit.

The Two-Theorem Story

Law of Large Numbers

Tells you the limit: X̄ₙ → μ as n → ∞

Makes no claim about the shape of fluctuations

Requires finite mean (Weak) or variance (Strong)

Works for more distributions (incl. heavier tails)

Justifies: using X̄ to estimate μ

Central Limit Theorem

Tells you the shape: errors are approximately normal

Quantifies uncertainty: X̄ₙ ~ N(μ, σ²/n)

Requires finite variance σ² < ∞

Fails for infinite-variance distributions (Cauchy)

Justifies: confidence intervals, hypothesis tests, VaR

Real-World Finance

Quant Applications

LLN and CLT aren't just elegant theory — they're the invisible engines running every major tool in quantitative finance.

1 · Monte Carlo Simulation

Monte Carlo methods estimate complex expectations by simulating many scenarios and averaging results. The LLN guarantees this average converges to the true expectation. The CLT gives the error rate.

Monte Carlo Estimator
E[f(X)] ≈ (1/N) Σᵢ f(Xᵢ)  →  E[f(X)]  by LLN
Error ~ N(0, Var(f(X))/N)  by CLT
To halve the MC error: quadruple simulations. Used for path-dependent options, CVA, risk simulations — anywhere closed-form formulas don't exist.

2 · Value at Risk (VaR) and CVaR

For a portfolio of n i.i.d. positions, the total P&L S = ΣXᵢ ~ N(nμ, nσ²) by CLT. VaR at confidence level α is then the α-th percentile of this normal distribution.

1-Day 99% VaR (CLT-based)
VaR₉₉ = −(nμ + z₀.₀₁ · σ√n)  where  z₀.₀₁ = −2.326
CVaR (Expected Shortfall) = E[Loss | Loss > VaR] — literally a conditional expectation, computable analytically once CLT gives the normal approximation. Fat tails require EVT instead.

3 · Backtesting & the t-Statistic

A quant backtests a strategy on n days, observes X̄. Is it skill or luck? The CLT provides the test statistic:

Backtesting t-Statistic
t = X̄ · √n / s  ~  N(0,1)  under H₀: μ=0
Industry rule: |t| > 2.0 for "significant," |t| > 3.0 to account for data-snooping and multiple testing. With 252 trading days and 1% daily vol, you need true μ ≈ 0.13%/day to get t > 2 — that's an extraordinary Sharpe ratio.

4 · Option Pricing — Black-Scholes via CLT

Under risk-neutral measure, log-returns are i.i.d. over each small interval. By CLT, the sum of log-returns over T days is normal — making the stock price log-normal. The option price is then a conditional expectation computable in closed form.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy.stats import norm

np.random.seed(42)

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════
# PART A: European Call via Monte Carlo
#   Uses LLN: average payoff → true expected payoff
#   Uses CLT: error ~ N(0, Var(payoff)/N)
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════
S0, K, r, sigma, T = 100, 105, 0.05, 0.20, 1.0

# Analytical Black-Scholes (our benchmark)
d1 = (np.log(S0/K) + (r + 0.5*sigma**2)*T) / (sigma*np.sqrt(T))
d2 = d1 - sigma*np.sqrt(T)
BS = S0*norm.cdf(d1) - K*np.exp(-r*T)*norm.cdf(d2)
print(f"Black-Scholes price: ${BS:.4f}")

# Monte Carlo for varying N
N_list = [50, 200, 1000, 5000, 20000, 100000]
mc_prices, mc_errs = [], []

for N in N_list:
    Z = np.random.randn(N)
    ST = S0 * np.exp((r - 0.5*sigma**2)*T + sigma*np.sqrt(T)*Z)
    payoff = np.maximum(ST - K, 0)
    price = np.exp(-r*T) * payoff.mean()         # LLN
    se = payoff.std() * np.exp(-r*T) / np.sqrt(N) # CLT
    mc_prices.append(price); mc_errs.append(1.96*se)
    print(f"N={N:7d}: ${price:.4f} ± {1.96*se:.4f}  (BS=${BS:.4f})")

fig, (ax1, ax2) = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(13, 5))
ax1.semilogx(N_list, mc_prices, "o-", color="#c2522b", lw=2)
ax1.fill_between(N_list, [p-e for p,e in zip(mc_prices,mc_errs)],
                  [p+e for p,e in zip(mc_prices,mc_errs)], alpha=0.25, color="#c2522b")
ax1.axhline(BS, color="#2c3e50", ls="--", lw=2, label=f"BS = ${BS:.4f}")
ax1.set_title("MC Price Converges to BS (LLN)"); ax1.legend()
ax1.set_xlabel("N simulations"); ax1.set_ylabel("Estimated Call Price")

Narr = np.array(N_list)
ax2.loglog(Narr, mc_errs, "o-", color="#c09a2e", lw=2, label="MC 95% error")
ax2.loglog(Narr, mc_errs[0]*np.sqrt(N_list[0]/Narr), "--", color="#4a7c59", lw=2, label="∝ 1/√N (CLT)")
ax2.set_title("Error Shrinks at 1/√N Rate (CLT)")
ax2.set_xlabel("N"); ax2.set_ylabel("Error"); ax2.legend()
plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()

# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════
# PART B: Strategy Significance Test
# ═══════════════════════════════════════════════
print("\n=== Backtesting Significance (CLT) ===")
n_days, mu_true, sig = 252, 0.0, 0.01  # no-skill strategy
ret = np.random.normal(mu_true, sig, n_days)
xbar, s = ret.mean(), ret.std()
t = xbar / (s / np.sqrt(n_days))
pval = 2*(1 - norm.cdf(abs(t)))
print(f"X̄ = {xbar:.4%}, t = {t:.3f}, p-value = {pval:.4f}")
print(f"95% CI: [{xbar-1.96*s/np.sqrt(n_days):.4%}, {xbar+1.96*s/np.sqrt(n_days):.4%}]")
needed = ((2*sig) / (0.0001))**2 / 252
print(f"Years needed to detect true μ=0.01%/day at t>2: {needed:.1f} years!")

Hypothesis Testing & CIs

Direct applications of CLT. Master z-tests, t-tests, p-values, and power analysis. These appear in every quant interview and research context.

3
Delta Method

Extends CLT to nonlinear functions: if X̄ ~ N(μ,σ²/n), then g(X̄) ~ N(g(μ), (g'(μ))²σ²/n). Used for Greeks, Sharpe ratio inference, and log-return distributions.

4
CLT for Dependent Data

Gordin's CLT, ergodic theorem, Newey-West standard errors. Essential for time-series financial data where i.i.d. fails.

5
Extreme Value Theory

When CLT fails (fat tails, infinite variance), EVT provides the Generalized Extreme Value and Generalized Pareto distributions. Critical for tail risk and stress testing.

6
Stochastic Calculus

The continuous-time limit of sums of i.i.d. random variables is Brownian motion — the CLT taken to its logical extreme. Leads to Itô's Lemma and the Black-Scholes PDE.

The LLN says: you can trust averages. The CLT says: and here is precisely how much to trust them. Every confidence interval, every p-value, every VaR estimate, every Monte Carlo simulation you will ever run as a quant engineer rests on these two theorems. Master them not as formulas to apply, but as a complete framework for reasoning about uncertainty under randomness.
