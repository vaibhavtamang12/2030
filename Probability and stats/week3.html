00 Prerequisites

Concepts you need to be comfortable with before diving into PDF, CDF, and Change of Variables.

⚠ Why Prereqs Matter

Think of this as a recipe — if you don't have the right ingredients, the dish won't work. These concepts are the ingredients. We'll keep explanations short and intuitive, just enough for the main topics to make sense.

P1 · Random Variables

A random variable is just a number that depends on the outcome of a random experiment. We write it as X (capital letter, by convention).

Example: Roll a fair die. X = the number that shows up. Before you roll, X could be 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, or 6 — it's "random". After you roll and see "4", that specific outcome 4 is a realisation of X.

In quant finance: X could be "tomorrow's stock price", "bond default time", or "daily return of a portfolio".

Discrete RV

Takes countable values: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6. Die roll, coin flip, number of trades today.

Continuous RV

Takes any value in an interval. Temperature, stock price, return on investment — infinite precision possible.

P2 · Probability Basics

P(event) = the chance that some outcome happens, always between 0 and 1. P = 0 means impossible, P = 1 means certain.

P(X = x) means "the probability that our random variable X takes exactly the value x".

P(a ≤ X ≤ b) means "the probability that X falls in the interval [a, b]".

Total probability rule: Σ P(X = xᵢ) = 1  [discrete]
∫₋∞^∞ f(x) dx = 1  [continuous]All probabilities must add up to 1 — something must happen.

P3 · Integration — Area Under a Curve

Don't be scared of integrals! All they mean here is: "the area under a curve between two points".

Imagine drawing a hill on graph paper. The integral from a to b = the area of that hill between x = a and x = b. That's it.

∫ₐᵇ f(x) dx = Area under f(x) from a to bThe ∫ symbol is a stretched S — think "Sum" of tiny slices.

In probability, the area under a probability curve tells us how likely outcomes in that range are.

P4 · Differentiation — Slope / Rate of Change

The derivative f'(x) = how fast f(x) is changing at point x. Think of it as the slope of a curve at a specific point.

If F(x) is a growing curve (like the CDF will be), then F'(x) = f(x) tells you how steeply it grows at each x — that will be our PDF!

d/dx F(x) = F'(x) = f(x)Differentiation "undoes" integration, and vice versa. This is the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.

P5 · Expected Value (Mean)

The expected value E[X] is the "long-run average" of X if you repeated your experiment infinitely many times. Think of it as the balance point (center of mass) of the distribution.

E[X] = ∫₋∞^∞ x · f(x) dxWeighted average of all x values, weighted by how probable they are.

In finance: E[return] is your expected profit. Risk = variance, which measures how spread out returns are from the mean.

✓ You're Ready If...

You understand what a random variable is, that probabilities add to 1, that integrals compute areas, and derivatives compute slopes. With that, you're ready to tackle PDF → CDF → Change of Variables in that order.

01 Probability Density Function (PDF)

The "shape" of a continuous distribution — how probability is spread across values.

What is a PDF? The Core Intuition

Imagine you have a bag of sand. You spread it out on a table in a pile. The height of the sand pile at any point x is like the PDF, f(x). Where the pile is tall = more probability mass there. Where it's flat = low probability.

A PDF does NOT give you probability directly at a single point. For a continuous distribution, P(X = exactly 3.14159…) = 0. Instead, you get probability by looking at an interval:

P(a ≤ X ≤ b) = ∫ₐᵇ f(x) dx = Area under the curve from a to b

This is exactly why a PDF is a density — just like mass density (kg/m³) tells you how much mass per unit volume, f(x) tells you how much probability per unit of x.

Formal Definition & Properties

A function f(x) is a valid PDF if and only if it satisfies these two conditions:

1. f(x) ≥ 0  for all x     [Non-negativity]
2. ∫₋∞^∞ f(x) dx = 1     [Total probability = 1]

Note: f(x) CAN be greater than 1! It's not a probability itself. Only areas are probabilities, and those must be ≤ 1.

Deep Dive: The Normal (Gaussian) PDF

The most important distribution in all of statistics and quant finance. Two parameters control everything:

f(x) = (1 / (σ√(2π))) · exp(-(x−μ)² / (2σ²))μ = mean (center), σ = standard deviation (spread). σ² = variance.

μ shifts the bell

Changing μ slides the bell curve left or right. The shape stays the same. In finance: μ is your expected return.

σ stretches/squeezes

Larger σ = wider bell (more uncertainty). Smaller σ = narrower bell (more predictable). In finance: σ is volatility.

68-95-99.7 Rule: For N(μ, σ²), approximately 68% of outcomes fall within μ±1σ, 95% within μ±2σ, and 99.7% within μ±3σ. This is fundamental to risk management (e.g., Value-at-Risk).

02 Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF)

The running total of probability — "how much probability is to the left of x?"

What is a CDF? The Simplest Intuition

If the PDF is the height of sand at each point, the CDF is the total amount of sand you've scooped up as you move from left to right.

Formally: F(x) = P(X ≤ x) = "the probability that our random variable is at most x".

F(x) = P(X ≤ x) = ∫₋∞ˣ f(t) dtCDF at point x = area under PDF from −∞ up to x.

As x → −∞: F(x) → 0 (no probability scooped yet).
As x → +∞: F(x) → 1 (all probability scooped).
At any x: F(x) is between 0 and 1, and always non-decreasing (you never scoop probability back out).

Properties of the CDF

PropertyMath StatementIntuition
Range0 ≤ F(x) ≤ 1It's always a probability
Non-decreasingIf x₁ < x₂ then F(x₁) ≤ F(x₂)You can only add probability as x grows
LimitsF(−∞) = 0, F(+∞) = 1Nothing below −∞, everything below +∞
Interval ProbP(a < X ≤ b) = F(b) − F(a)Difference in running totals
ComplementP(X > x) = 1 − F(x)What's above = 1 minus what's below

The Critical Relationship: CDF ↔ PDF

This is perhaps the most important equation to memorise. The PDF and CDF are two sides of the same coin:

F(x) = ∫₋∞ˣ f(t) dt     [PDF → CDF via integration]

f(x) = d/dx F(x) = F'(x)     [CDF → PDF via differentiation]Integration and differentiation are inverse operations (Fundamental Theorem of Calculus).
1
You have a PDF f(x) — a curve describing density To find cumulative probability up to some x, integrate f from −∞ to x → gives F(x)
2
You have a CDF F(x) — a S-shaped curve going 0 to 1 To recover the density at each point, differentiate F(x) → gives f(x) back
3
Finding interval probabilities P(a ≤ X ≤ b) = F(b) − F(a). Subtract CDFs — no integration needed!

Inverse CDF (Quantile Function) — Critical for Quant Work

The inverse CDF, written F⁻¹(p) or Q(p), answers: "below what value x does p fraction of the distribution lie?"

F⁻¹(p) = x such that F(x) = pGiven probability p, find the corresponding x value (quantile).

Value at Risk (VaR)

VaR at 5% = F⁻¹(0.05) for returns. "With 95% confidence, losses won't exceed this amount."

Inverse Transform Sampling

Generate U ~ Uniform(0,1), then X = F⁻¹(U). This gives samples from any distribution! Critical for Monte Carlo.

Percentiles

The 95th percentile = F⁻¹(0.95). Half the data is below the median = F⁻¹(0.5).

The Standard Normal CDF: Φ(x)

For the N(0,1) distribution, the CDF is written Φ(x) (Phi). It has no closed form — you look it up in tables or use a computer.

Φ(x) = (1/√2π) ∫₋∞ˣ exp(-t²/2) dt

For general N(μ,σ²): F(x) = Φ((x-μ)/σ)Standardize first by subtracting mean and dividing by std dev, then use Φ.

In the Black-Scholes option pricing formula, you'll see N(d₁) and N(d₂) — these are exactly Φ(d₁) and Φ(d₂). Understanding the CDF IS understanding Black-Scholes.

03 Change of Variables

What happens to a probability distribution when you transform a random variable?

The Core Problem: Why Do We Need This?

Imagine you know the PDF of daily stock returns X ~ N(μ, σ²). Now someone asks you: "What's the distribution of the stock price, Y = e^X?"

Y is a transformation of X. The distribution of Y is NOT normal. Change of Variables gives you the machinery to find the PDF of Y from the PDF of X.

Finance Example

If X = log-return ~ Normal, then Y = e^X = price ratio ~ Log-Normal. Black-Scholes uses this!

Risk Example

If losses follow distribution X, what's the distribution of portfolio variance X²? Change of variables!

Simulation Example

Transform U ~ Uniform(0,1) to any target distribution using inverse CDF. Monte Carlo at its core.

Method 1: The CDF Method (Most Fundamental)

Step-by-step: find the CDF of Y = g(X) first, then differentiate to get the PDF.

1
Write F_Y(y) = P(Y ≤ y) Express this as a probability about X using the transformation Y = g(X)
2
Substitute Y = g(X) and invert P(g(X) ≤ y) = P(X ≤ g⁻¹(y)) = F_X(g⁻¹(y))  [if g is increasing]
3
Differentiate to get PDF of Y f_Y(y) = d/dy F_Y(y)
Example: Y = g(X) = 2X + 3 (linear shift)

F_Y(y) = P(Y ≤ y) = P(2X+3 ≤ y) = P(X ≤ (y-3)/2) = F_X((y-3)/2)
f_Y(y) = f_X((y-3)/2) · (1/2)The 1/2 comes from the chain rule when differentiating.

Method 2: The Change of Variables Formula (Faster)

For a monotone (always increasing or always decreasing) transformation Y = g(X):

f_Y(y) = f_X(g⁻¹(y)) · |d/dy g⁻¹(y)|

= f_X(x) · |dx/dy|The |dx/dy| term is called the Jacobian. It accounts for stretching/squeezing.

⚠ Why the Jacobian? The Stretching Intuition

Imagine a rubber band laid on the x-axis. If you stretch it (g expands the range), the probability density gets thinner. The Jacobian |dx/dy| is the "stretching factor" — it ensures the total probability stays = 1 after the transformation.

Think of it like unit conversion: if you convert temperatures from Celsius to Fahrenheit, the intervals change size. The Jacobian corrects for this size change in probability calculations.

The Log-Normal: Most Important Example in Finance

If X ~ N(μ, σ²) and Y = e^X, then Y ~ LogNormal(μ, σ²). Here's the derivation:

Y = e^X  ⟹  X = ln(Y) = g⁻¹(Y)

dX/dY = d(ln Y)/dY = 1/Y

f_Y(y) = f_X(ln y) · |1/y| = (1/(σ√2π)) · exp(-(ln y - μ)²/(2σ²)) · (1/y)This is exactly the Log-Normal PDF. Valid for y > 0.

Why does this matter? Stock prices must be positive. Log-Normal distribution (via the transformation Y = e^X where X is the log-return) is the standard model for stock prices in Black-Scholes.

Multivariate Change of Variables (Overview)

When you have multiple random variables (X₁, X₂) and transform them to (Y₁, Y₂) = g(X₁, X₂), the formula extends using the Jacobian matrix:

f_Y(y₁, y₂) = f_X(x₁, x₂) · |J|⁻¹

where |J| = |∂(y₁,y₂)/∂(x₁,x₂)| = determinant of the Jacobian matrixIn quant finance: used for portfolio transformations, correlated asset pricing, and multivariate risk models.

Example: If returns on two assets are jointly normal, the distribution of their weighted portfolio is also normal. The Jacobian ensures correct probability accounting.

⚠ Non-Monotone Transformations (Important Edge Case)

If g(x) is not monotone (e.g., Y = X², which maps both +x and −x to the same y), you need to split the domain:

f_Y(y) = Σₖ f_X(xₖ) · |dx/dy| where xₖ are all solutions to g(x) = yFor Y=X², both x=+√y and x=−√y give Y=y, so sum over both branches.

This is the origin of the Chi-squared distribution (X ~ N(0,1) → X² ~ Chi-squared(1)), which is important for hypothesis testing in statistics.

04 How They All Connect

A unified view of how PDF, CDF, and Change of Variables work together in quant finance.

⬡ The Concept Map — relationships between all three topics

The Unified Story

Here's the most important thing to understand: PDF, CDF, and Change of Variables are not three separate topics — they're three views of the same underlying object: a probability distribution.

1
Start with a PDF f_X(x) — the shape of uncertainty The PDF tells you how likely different values of X are. It's the fundamental description of a random variable.
2
Integrate the PDF → get the CDF F_X(x) The CDF gives cumulative probabilities P(X ≤ x). You use it to compute P(a ≤ X ≤ b) = F(b) − F(a).
3
Differentiate the CDF → recover the PDF These two operations are inverses (Fundamental Theorem of Calculus). You can always go between them.
4
Apply a transformation g(X) = Y — Change of Variables When you transform X, both its PDF and CDF change. The Jacobian ensures probability is conserved.
5
Result: new PDF and CDF for Y = g(X) The new PDF f_Y(y) = f_X(g⁻¹(y)) · |dx/dy|. The new CDF F_Y(y) = F_X(g⁻¹(y)) for increasing g.

Quant Finance Synthesis: Black-Scholes in 5 Steps

Black-Scholes uses ALL THREE concepts. Let's trace through it:

StepConcept UsedWhat Happens
1. Assume log-returnsPDF (Normal)dS/S = μdt + σdW, so log-return ~ N((μ−σ²/2)T, σ²T)
2. Get stock price distributionChange of VariablesS_T = S₀ · e^X where X is log-return → S_T ~ Log-Normal
3. Price = Discounted ExpectationPDF integralC = e^(−rT) · E[max(S_T − K, 0)] = ∫ (S_T−K)⁺ · f(S_T) dS_T
4. Evaluate the integralCDF (Φ)The integral simplifies to: S₀·Φ(d₁) − Ke^(−rT)·Φ(d₂)
5. Risk management: P(exercise)CDF evaluationΦ(d₂) = probability option ends in-the-money
C = S₀ · Φ(d₁) − K · e^(−rT) · Φ(d₂)

d₁ = [ln(S₀/K) + (r + σ²/2)T] / (σ√T)
d₂ = d₁ − σ√TΦ = standard normal CDF. This formula exists because of Change of Variables + CDF integration.

Monte Carlo Simulation: All Three in One Algorithm

Every Monte Carlo simulation you run uses all three concepts:

Step 1: Sample from Uniform

Computer generates U ~ Uniform(0,1). This is a CDF value (probability).

Step 2: Inverse CDF (Change of Variables)

Apply F⁻¹(U) to transform to desired distribution (Normal, Exponential, etc.).

Step 3: Compute Payoffs

Transform random returns to stock prices using e^X (another Change of Variables).

Step 4: Average = Expectation

Average payoffs = ∫ payoff · f(x) dx — a PDF integral approximated by samples.
